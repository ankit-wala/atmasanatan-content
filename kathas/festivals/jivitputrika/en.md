## Katha

In a valley of the Himalayas lived a Gandharva prince — **Jimutavahana**.

He was the most generous in his lineage. So generous that he had given away his entire treasury — jewels, gold, land — in charity. Whatever wealth he had received from his father as a son, he had distributed to the hungry and the needy.

One day, wandering in a forest, he heard the sound of weeping — an old Naga woman seated on a rock.

He went to her.

She sat crying on a stone.

Jimutavahana asked — "Mother, what has happened?"

She said — "Son, we are of the Naga lineage. It is our tradition that each day, one Naga goes to Garuda as his meal. Today it is the turn of my son **Shankachuda**."

Jimutavahana's heart was moved.

He said — "Mother, do not weep. In your son's place, I will go."

The old woman was alarmed — "No, no, who are you? This is not your burden."

But Jimutavahana would not relent.

---

He went and sat on that rock — the place where Garuda came each day to receive his meal.

Garuda arrived — with terrifying speed, a storm of wings. He saw that a human lay on the rock, not a serpent.

But he did not stop.

Jimutavahana offered no resistance. Made no plea. He bore everything in silence.

Garuda's gaze fell on his face — fearless, peaceful, compassionate.

Garuda stopped and asked — "You are not a Naga. Who are you? And why have you come here?"

Jimutavahana replied — "A mother's son was to come today. I came in their place."

Garuda was stunned.

In all his life he had never witnessed such courage and such compassion. That someone would offer their own life to save another mother's son — this was extraordinary.

His heart was changed.

By the grace of the Goddess, Jimutavahana was restored to health. Garuda swore — from this day, the cruel tradition of the Naga tribute was ended.

In the tradition of the Skanda Purana it is said that mothers who hear the story of Jimutavahana and observe the Jivitputrika Vrat — their sons receive protection of long life, good health, and well-being.

## Significance

**Jitiya — symbol of life:**
The word "Jitiya" comes from "Jivit" — the resolve to protect life. This vrat is a mother's vow — that just as Jimutavahana protected Shankachuda through sacrifice and compassion, may our sons also be kept safe.

**Eastern India's tradition:**
This vrat is primarily observed in the regional tradition of Bihar, Jharkhand, eastern Uttar Pradesh, and Nepal. Mothers observe a full nirjala (without water) fast for over 24 hours — it is among the most rigorous mother-vrats in India.

**Jimutavahana — the supreme giver:**
Jimutavahana's character is a union of two qualities — dana (giving) and tyaga (sacrifice). He not only gives wealth in charity but is also ready to give his life. This spirit is the soul of this vrat.

## Vidhi

1. Take the vrat sankalpa on the evening of Ashwin Krishna Saptami.
2. On Saptami, bathe and take a single meal (nahay-khay) — preparation for the vrat.
3. **Ashtami — nirjala fast** — no water at all.
4. Prepare or install an image of Jimutavahana.
5. Worship with incense, lamp, kusha grass, flowers, and river water.
6. Hear the Jivitputrika Vrat katha.
7. **Navami — parana** — regional tradition includes certain symbolic foods; generally sattvic food.

**2026 date:** Confirm on DrikPanchang (Ashwin Krishna Ashtami — ~October 3, 2026).

## Observance

**Nirjala fast:** This vrat is observed completely without water on Ashtami — one of the strictest mother-vrats in Indian tradition. Mothers undertake this rigorous fast for the longevity of their sons.

**Nahay-khay:** On Saptami, bathe and take one meal — the preparation for the vrat day.

**Parana:** On the morning of Navami at the appropriate time.

**Community:** This vrat is observed collectively — women gather at a river or pond to perform the puja together.
