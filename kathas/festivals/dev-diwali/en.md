## Katha

### The Boon of the Tripurasuras

This story is told in the Shiva Purana.

Tarakasura had three sons — **Tarakaksha**, **Vidyunmali**, and **Kamalaksha**. After their father's slaying they were the mightiest among all the asuras, yet a restless fire burned within their hearts. They resolved to win a power that no one could ever wrest from them.

All three undertook fierce austerities to propitiate Brahma. Brahma was pleased and appeared before them.

Tarakaksha asked for the boon: "O Pitamaha, grant us three cities — one of gold, one of silver, one of iron. Let these cities fly through the sky. Within them let us be invincible — let none defeat us."

Brahma granted the boon — but imposed one condition:

"Once in a thousand years these three cities will align in a single line. At that moment, all three can be destroyed by a single arrow."

The three brothers smiled to themselves. One moment in a thousand years? And that too by a single arrow? This was as good as impossible.

### The Tyranny of the Tripurasuras

The moment they received the boon, their arrogance touched the heavens. They attacked all three worlds. The gods were driven from their celestial abodes. The ashrams of sages were burned down. Yajnas and the chanting of the Vedas ceased. Dharma was in peril.

The gods went to Brahma — he was powerless. Then to Vishnu — he too was troubled. There was only one recourse: Mahadeva.

### Shiva's Cosmic Preparation

The gods prayed to Shiva. Mahadeva consented.

But this was no ordinary battle. By the terms of the Tripurasuras' boon, all three cities had to be destroyed by a single arrow — and that moment would arrive only when all three cities stood in one line.

For Shiva, a chariot was assembled unlike any that had ever existed in all creation:

- **The chariot** — the Earth herself became the chariot
- **The wheels** — the Sun and the Moon
- **The horses** — the four Vedas
- **The charioteer** — Brahma himself
- **The bow** — the Himalaya mountain
- **The bowstring** — Vasuki
- **The arrow** — Vishnu himself

All the gods, sages, yakshas, and gandharvas stood waiting for this single moment.

A thousand years passed.

### The Burning of Tripura

That moment came.

In the sky, the three flying cities drifted slowly into alignment — the golden, the silver, the iron — all three in one line.

Brahma said to Shiva: "O Mahadeva, this is that moment."

Shiva lifted the bow. For one instant, all three worlds fell silent.

Then Shiva smiled — that smile which comes before the Tandava.

He released a single arrow.

That arrow passed through all three cities — and all three were reduced to ash at once. The Tripurasuras were destroyed. A roar of victory resounded across all three worlds.

**"Tripuraghna"** — the destroyer of Tripura — this name was given to Shiva from that very day.

### The Festival of Lights of the Gods in Kashi

The gods came to Kashi to celebrate their victory — that city which has always been Shiva's beloved.

On the banks of the Ganga they lit millions of lamps. Just as, on the Kartik Amavasya, mortals had lit lamps in Ayodhya to welcome Ram's return — so too did the gods light lamps in Kashi on the night of the full moon to mark the victory over Tripura.

This was **Dev Diwali** — the festival of lights celebrated by the gods.

---

Since that day, this tradition endures: on the night of Kartik Shukla Purnima, millions of lamps are lit on the ghats of Kashi (Varanasi) along the Ganga. From Dashashwamedh Ghat to Assi Ghat — a lamp on every step, a lamp on every boat, a lamp in every hand. This sight is heaven come to earth.

The Shiva Purana tradition holds that one who bathes in the Ganga and performs lamp-offering on Kartik Purnima receives the merit of bathing at all sacred tirthas.

## Significance

- **Dev Diwali — the festival of lights of the gods:** Kartik Amavasya (Diwali) belongs to mortals; Kartik Purnima belongs to the gods. Fifteen days later, the gods celebrate that same festival of light in Kashi. The entire month of Kartik is regarded as the month of lamps and worship — and this full moon is its culmination.

- **Kashi — the city of Shiva:** Varanasi is India's most ancient living city. Shiva is its sovereign. The sight of millions of lamps on Kashi's ghats on Dev Diwali is considered the grandest and most otherworldly festival of lights in the world.

- **The slaying of the Tripurasuras — the destruction of ego:** The three cities of Tripura (gold, silver, iron) are said to represent three forms of ego — sattvic, rajasic, and tamasic. Shiva's single arrow — released when all three aligned — shows that the three forms of inner ego are destroyed together. So long as each form of ego flies separately, it eludes destruction.

- **Shiva's cosmic chariot — creation as his instrument:** Earth as chariot, Sun and Moon as wheels, the Vedas as horses, Brahma as charioteer, Vishnu as arrow — this metaphor declares that before the will of Shiva, all of creation becomes his instrument. No power can stand against his divine play.

- **Kartik Purnima — king among tirthas:** On this day, bathing in the Ganga carries exceptional merit. The Puranas declare that bathing in any sacred river on Kartik Purnima yields the fruit of bathing at all tirthas.

- **Gurpurab:** Kartik Purnima is also celebrated in the Sikh tradition as **Guru Nanak Jayanti** — the birth anniversary of Guru Nanak Dev Ji. Both traditions honour this full moon as a festival of light.

## Vidhi

- **At dawn (Brahma Muhurta):** Rise and bathe in the Ganga or another sacred river; those unable to go to the river may bathe at home adding Gangajal to the water and meditating on the Ganga.
- **Shiva worship:** Visit a Shiva temple or worship the Shiva Linga at home; offer water, milk, bel leaves, datura, bhasma, and white flowers; chant "Om Namah Shivaya." Also worship Vishnu — he too played his part in the burning of Tripura.
- **Katha of Tripura Dahana:** Sit with the family and listen to or read the story of the burning of Tripura from the Shiva Purana — this is the heart of the celebration.
- **Evening lamp offering:** After sunset, light clay lamps at the ghat or near a sacred body of water and float them in the water; those in Kashi should go to the Ganga ghats — the celebration in Varanasi is unmatched, with millions of lamps blazing at Dashashwamedh, Manikarnika, and Assi ghats; offering lamps from a boat on the Ganga is especially meritorious.
- **Decoration of the home:** Light lamps in every corner of the home, near the Tulsi plant, and at the main entrance; it is believed that on this night the gods themselves move through the earth. Do not let the lamps go out — let them burn on.
- **Charity:** Give food, clothing, and til-gur in charity according to one's means; distribute prasad among neighbours and the poor.
- **Completion of Kartik Snana Vrat:** For those who have bathed daily in a sacred river throughout the month of Kartik, this Purnima concludes that vrat; the lamp offering on the night of the full moon completes the vow.
- **What to avoid:** Do not consume meat or intoxicants on this night.

## Mantras

**Shiva Tripuraghna Mantra:**

> ॐ नमः शिवाय त्रिपुरघ्नाय महेश्वराय ।
> पाशुपतास्त्रधराय त्रिनेत्राय नमो नमः ॥

Oṃ namaḥ śivāya tripuraghnāya maheśvarāya | pāśupatāstraharāya trinetrāya namo namaḥ ||

Meaning: "Salutations to Shiva, destroyer of Tripura, Maheshvara, bearer of the Pashupatastra, the three-eyed one — salutations again and again."

---

**Lamp Offering Mantra:**

> दीपदानं महापुण्यं संसारतिमिरापहम् ।
> नमस्कुर्यात् सदा देवं दीपज्योतिर्नमोऽस्तु ते ॥

Dīpadānaṃ mahāpuṇyaṃ saṃsāratimirāpaham | namaskuryāt sadā devaṃ dīpajyotirnamostu te ||

Meaning: "The offering of a lamp is a great merit — it dispels the darkness of worldly existence. Salutations always to the divine lamp-flame; salutations to you, O light."
