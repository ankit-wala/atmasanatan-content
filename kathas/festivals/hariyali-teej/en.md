## Katha

Parvati, daughter of the Himalayas, had one resolve in her life — Shiva.

On Kailasha he sat alone. He acknowledged no one's request, heard no one's pleading. He knew the cosmos, yet remained aloof from it. Parvati understood — to attain him would be no ordinary thing.

She began her tapasya. In the forest, in solitude. First fruits, then leaves, then only water, then not even air. In the heat of summer, she stood between five fires; in the rains, she stood in the open; in winter, she stood in the river's cold current. Years passed.

The gods grew anxious. There was a trembling through the cosmos. And Lord Shiva — he knew.

One day he came — in the guise of an aged brahmin. He tried to shake Parvati's resolve. He spoke ill of Shiva; he said: *"He is unfit for you — this wandering ascetic is not worthy of your devotion."*

Parvati rose to leave.

The brahmin asked: *"Where are you going?"*

Parvati said: *"Where my Shiva's name is spoken ill of, I cannot stay even a moment."*

The disguise fell away. Lord Shiva appeared in his own form.

And then — that moment came.

Shiva looked into Parvati's eyes — that steadfastness, that love, that surrender which had only grown purer through the fire of tapasya. He said:

*"Devi, before your love, my vairagya cannot stand. From this day, you are mine, and I am yours."*

That day was Shravan Shukla Tritiya.

And in that moment, all of nature changed.

The Himalayas turned green. Flowers bloomed in the forest. The clouds of Shravan swelled and rain began to fall — as if Parvati's joy was descending from the sky itself, unstoppable.

This is why this Teej is called "Hariyali Teej" — because when Parvati's heart turned green with happiness, all of creation turned green with her.

In the tradition of the Shiva Purana, Rudra Samhita, Parvati Khanda, it is said: *"The woman who observes the fast on Hariyali Teej, remembers Maa Gauri, and worships with the wish for saubhagya — receives that very saubhagya that was Parvati's."*

## Significance

Hariyali Teej celebrates two truths — Maa Parvati's reunion with Shiva, and the green abundance of Shravan.

Maa Parvati is the symbol alive in every married woman's heart — one who gave everything for love, who remained steadfast, who in the end received what she yearned for.

"Hariyali" means not only the green earth — it is the greenness of the mind, the greenness of saubhagya (the blessing of a husband's life).

On this day, married women swing on jhulas, apply mehndi, wear new clothes, and sing folk songs. All of this is a re-enactment of the joy Parvati felt on that day — when the long waiting ended and love was victorious.

## Vidhi

1. Take an early bath and make the sankalpa (vow) for the fast.
2. Worship Maa Gauri and Lord Shiva — offer bilva leaves, green flowers, turmeric, sindoor.
3. In the North Indian regional tradition, wear green clothes and green bangles — green is the symbol of Parvati's reunion.
4. Perform the sixteen shringars — sindoor, mehndi, bindi, bangles.
5. In the North Indian tradition, swing on a jhula and sing Teej folk songs.
6. Keep a night vigil — listen to the katha of Maa Gauri.
7. Break the fast the following morning (Chaturthi).

## Observance

**Fast:** Some traditions observe a waterless (nirjal) fast; others allow fruits. According to one's strength.

**Breaking the fast:** The following day — Chaturthi — after the morning bath.

**For newly-wed women:** The first Hariyali Teej is especially significant — in many regional traditions, it is observed at one's parents' home.

**2026 date:** Confirm on DrikPanchang (~August 15, 2026, Shravan Shukla Tritiya).
