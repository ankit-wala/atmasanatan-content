## Katha

**The Churning of the Ocean and the Appearance of Surabhi:**

The gods and the asuras joined together to churn the great ocean of milk — seeking the nectar of immortality. Mount Mandara was used as the churning rod, and the serpent Vasuki as the rope. As the churning continued, one divine treasure after another rose from the depths of that sacred sea — Goddess Lakshmi, Dhanvantari, the Kalpavriksha, the elephant Airavata — and with them appeared **Surabhi** — the primordial mother of all cows, the Kamadhenu who fulfils every wish.

The day of Surabhi's appearance was none other than **Kartik Krishna Dwadashi.**

All the gods stood spellbound. Indra turned to Brahma and asked — "Lord, what is the proper manner of worshipping Surabhi and her offspring? How do we make this day eternal?"

Brahma replied — "O King of the gods, on this very Kartik Krishna Dwadashi, the tithi on which Surabhi emerged from the ocean, worship the cow and her calf each year. Adorn them with garlands of flowers and turmeric-kumkum, light a lamp before them, offer them grass and jaggery, and walk around them in pradakshina. This is 'Govatsa Dwadashi' — through this worship, progeny, wealth, and happiness — all are granted."

---

**Young Krishna's First Honour of the Cows in Gokul:**

In the Gokul of Nanda Baba at Vrindavan, thousands of cows roamed freely. Whenever young Krishna set out towards the forest, he would go to the cows first of all. He knew every cow by name — he would call to them, stroke them gently, and feed them grass with his own hands.

One Kartik Krishna Dwadashi, when Nanda Baba and Mother Yashoda were making grand preparations for Gopuja, Krishna himself rose first of all. He adorned the finest cow and her calf in Nanda's household with turmeric, kumkum, and a garland of flowers. He lit an earthen lamp before them. He placed grass and jaggery with his own hands. And then, playing with the calf, he called out to Mother Yashoda —

"Mother, first food for Gomata — then we will eat."

Mother Yashoda's eyes filled with tears.

From that day onward, Govatsa Dwadashi was celebrated in Gokul with even greater love and devotion.

**Phalashruti (The Fruit of Hearing):**

The tradition of the Skanda Purana tells us that any man or woman who worships the cow and her calf with proper rites on Govatsa Dwadashi — their household never lacks for grain or wealth, their children remain happy, and Lakshmi takes permanent residence in their family life. Through Gomata's blessing, the ancestors are satisfied and the doors to liberation open wide.

## Significance

**The Cow as the Form of Lakshmi:**
In the scriptural tradition of Hinduism, the body of the cow is held to be the dwelling of thirty-three crore deities. Brahma resides in Gomata's back, Vishnu in her throat, and Rudra in her face. This is why the cow is called 'Gomata' — mother, not animal. To worship her is equivalent to worshipping all the gods together.

**Vasu Baras — Maharashtra's Name for the Festival:**
In Maharashtra this festival is known as **Vasu Baras**. 'Vasu' means wealth and prosperity; 'Baras' means Dwadashi. The cow brings wealth and abundance into the home — and so, through the worship on this day, 'Vasu' (prosperity) is obtained.

**The First Day of the Diwali Festival Sequence:**
Govatsa Dwadashi is the first day of the five-day Diwali festival sequence. It is followed by Dhanteras (Trayodashi), Narak Chaturdashi (Chhoti Diwali), Diwali (Amavasya), and Govardhan Puja. Opening the festival with the worship of the cow is the very heart of this tradition — for in Krishna's culture, no celebration is complete without the cow.

**Surabhi (Kamadhenu):**
Surabhi emerged from the churning of the ocean — born through the grace of Lord Vishnu. All cows are considered the offspring of Surabhi; and so within every cow dwells a part of Kamadhenu, and to worship any cow is to worship Surabhi herself.

## Vidhi

- **Sankalpa:** Rise early, bathe, and take the sankalpa (sacred resolve) for the Govatsa Dwadashi vrat.
- **Gopuja:** Bathe the cow and her calf; apply a turmeric-kumkum tilak and garland them with flowers; light an earthen lamp and incense before them.
- **Offerings:** Offer fresh grass, jaggery, and grain (especially wheat, sesame, or chickpeas) to the cow and calf; walk around them in three pradakshinas.
- **Katha:** Listen to the katha of Govatsa Dwadashi together with the family.
- **Fasting:** Foods made from wheat (roti, puri, etc.) and from cow's milk (curd, ghee, paneer) are forbidden on this day — take barley, rice, fruit, and vegetables instead. This rule exists because the cow is honoured as mother, and one does not consume a mother's milk.
- **For whom:** This festival is observed especially by women — for the long life of their children and the happiness and prosperity of the family; the whole family may participate in the Gopuja.
- **At dusk:** Light a lamp at the doorstep and near the Tulsi plant — this is the first lamp of the Diwali festival sequence.

## Mantras

**Surabhi Prayer:**

> सुरभि त्वं जगन्माता लक्ष्मीर्नारायणप्रिया ।
> पुत्रान् देहि धनं देहि सर्वकामांश्च देहि मे ॥

Surabhi tvaṃ jaganmātā lakṣmīr nārāyaṇapriyā |
putrān dehi dhanaṃ dehi sarvakāmāṃśca dehi me ||

Meaning: "O Surabhi, you are the mother of the world, you are Lakshmi, the beloved of Narayana — grant me sons, grant me wealth, and fulfil all my desires."

---

**Cow-Worship Mantra:**

> नमो गोभ्यः श्रीमतीभ्यः सौरभेयीभ्य एव च ।
> नमो ब्रह्मसुताभ्यश्च पवित्राभ्यो नमो नमः ॥

Namo gobhyaḥ śrīmatībhyaḥ saurabheyībhya eva ca |
namo brahmasutābhyaśca pavitrābhyo namo namaḥ ||

Meaning: "Salutations again and again to the cows who are the form of Lakshmi, to the daughters of Surabhi, to the cows born of Brahma, and to all sacred cows."
