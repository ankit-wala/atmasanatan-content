## Katha

This is a tale told in the folk tradition of northern India.

In a certain town there lived a seth's wife — gentle, devoted. Her husband had gone away to trade. Shravan passed; Bhadrapada came — yet no word from him.

Worry had settled in the house. But the woman's heart was steady. She thought — "What is not in my hands is in Mother Gauri's."

Bhadrapada Krishna Tritiya arrived — the day of Kajari Teej.

The woman rose at dawn and bathed. In the courtyard she shaped a small earthen mound. In it she planted a fresh branch of neem — this was the form of "Nimdi Mata." Then she moulded small clay images of Shiva-Parvati and worshipped them with full rites.

She kept her fast — without water. Not a drop, not a morsel.

In the evening she lit lamps. Silently, from within, she spoke to Mother Gauri — "Mother, protect my husband. Bring him home safely."

Through the night she kept vigil. She sang the Teej folk songs — those songs that are sung on the kohl-dark nights of Shravan, songs that rumble like monsoon thunder.

The next morning, her fast was completed with paran.

And a few days later — her husband returned. Safe, smiling.

The neighbours asked — "What happened?"

The husband said — "There was a danger on the road — but from somewhere, some force acted. I was saved."

The woman smiled and said — "Mother Gauri's grace."

In this folk tradition of northern India it is said — "The woman who keeps the Kajari Teej vrat with full devotion receives Mother Gauri's blessing. Her husband's life is lengthened, and her household is filled with happiness."

## Significance

Kajari Teej is the third of the year's three Teejas — after Hariyali Teej (Shravan Shukla Tritiya) and before Hartalika Teej (Bhadrapada Shukla Tritiya).

"Kajar" or "kajal" — black kohl, the dark colour of monsoon clouds. When the sky over Shravan and Bhadrapada is shrouded in black clouds, that is the Kajari season. Those long dark nights, that longing for the beloved, those Teej songs.

This vrat is observed mainly in Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, Bihar, and Rajasthan. In the regions of Varanasi, Mirzapur, and Bundelkhand, the traditions of this day are especially rich.

The Kajari songs — the folk songs sung on this day — form a distinct genre of Indian music that sings of longing and rain in the same breath.

## Vidhi

The following ritual is practised in the regional tradition of northern India:

1. Rise at dawn, bathe, and take the vow of the fast.
2. In the courtyard or puja space, shape a small earthen mound.
3. Plant a fresh neem branch in it — this is the symbol of "Nimdi Mata."
4. Mould small clay images of Shiva-Parvati and install them.
5. Worship with flowers, incense, a lamp, and an offering (sattu / kheer / fruit).
6. Keep the fast — nirjal (without water) or on fruit.
7. In the night, hold a jagran — sing Kajari folk songs.
8. Complete the fast with paran at dawn the next day.

## Observance

**Fasting:** In regional tradition, a nirjal (waterless) fast; fruit is also acceptable.

**Paran:** The next day — Chaturthi — after sunrise.

**Sattu offering:** In some regional traditions, sattu (roasted gram flour) is offered as a special bhog.

**For new brides:** The first Kajari Teej is special — in northern Indian tradition, there is a custom of observing it at the maternal home (peehar).

**2026 date:** Confirm on DrikPanchang (~August 31, 2026, Bhadrapada Krishna Tritiya).
