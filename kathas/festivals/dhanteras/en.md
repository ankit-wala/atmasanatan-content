## Katha

### Part One — Dhanvantari Jayanti

The gods and demons came to an agreement.

Both wanted amrita — the nectar of immortality, the one thing that could conquer death. But amrita lay in the depths of the cosmic ocean, and drawing it out would require Mount Mandara as the churning rod and the serpent Vasuki as the rope.

The churning of the ocean began.

The gods took hold of Vasuki's tail, the demons his head. Mandara began to spin. Waves rose from the ocean — first came Halahala, the deadly poison, which Mahadeva held in his throat. Then, one by one, fourteen divine treasures emerged — Kamadhenu the wish-granting cow, the Kalpavriksha tree, the apsaras, Lakshmi, the Parijata flower.

And then — on the day of Kartik Krishna Trayodashi —

A luminous being appeared.

He was clothed in white. In his hands he carried a golden vessel — inside it was amrita. His eyes were like lotuses, and on his face shone a divine radiance of perfect health.

This was — **Lord Dhanvantari.**

His glory is described in the eighth canto of the Bhagavata Purana. He is a divine form of Vishnu himself — the physician of the gods, the founder of Ayurveda, the destroyer of disease. The day he emerged from the ocean bearing the vessel of nectar is celebrated as his birth anniversary — Kartik Krishna Trayodashi.

This is Dhanteras.

### Part Two — Yama Deepam and the Story of Raja Hima's Son

The Skanda Purana tradition tells another katha — of a young bride and one love-filled night.

There was a king — **Raja Hima**. He had a son, a young prince. When learned astrologers studied the prince's birth chart, they told the king: "On the fourth day after his marriage, this son will die — bitten by a serpent."

Hearing this, Raja Hima's heart grew heavy. But who can turn aside what is fated?

The prince was married.

His new bride was beautiful, wise, and courageous. She too knew of this prophecy. And she resolved in her heart — she would not let Yama come.

The evening of the fourth day after the wedding arrived.

She sat down with a vow: she would keep the prince awake through the entire night. She told him katha after katha. She sang songs. She spoke words of love. She did not let him fall asleep for even a moment.

Then she took off all her gold and silver ornaments and heaped them in a mound before the entrance to the bedchamber. She lit hundreds of clay lamps. The doorway blazed with light.

That night, Yama came in the form of a serpent to take the prince's life.

He reached the entrance — and stopped.

The brilliance of the gold and silver ornaments, the flame of hundreds of lamps — together they made such a blaze of light that his eyes were dazzled. He could not enter. He settled himself on the pile of ornaments and spent the whole night listening to the young bride's songs.

Dawn came.

Yama turned back. Without taking a life.

The prince was alive. His wife's love, her courage, and the light of those lamps had stopped Yama himself.

The Skanda Purana tradition holds that whoever lights a lamp outside their door on the evening of Dhanteras, facing it south — the direction of Yama — receives the merit of lighting the Yama Deepam, the south-facing lamp offered to Yama. The fear of untimely death does not enter that home.

This is the **Yama Deepam**.

Whoever worships Lord Dhanvantari on Dhanteras and lights the Yama Deepam — health dwells in that home, prosperity grows, and the family is protected from untimely death. This is the combined fruit of both kathas.

## Significance

Dhanteras is the first day of Diwali — yet it carries two distinct spiritual currents of its own.

**Dhanvantari Jayanti** — Lord Dhanvantari is a partial incarnation of Vishnu. He is the founder of Ayurveda and the royal physician of the gods. His emergence from the ocean bearing the vessel of nectar is not merely a mythological event — it is a symbol of the truth that **wealth and health must come together.** Where there is wealth, there should be good health; where there is life, the longing for nectar is natural. The central government has declared this date the **National Ayurveda Day**.

**The current of the Yama Deepam** — In Raja Hima's son's story, the lamp is a shield. Yama comes in darkness; light makes him stop. The lamp lit facing south on Dhanteras declares: "This home is awake. There is love here, there is devotion here, there is light here."

**The tradition of buying gold and silver** — The young bride placed her ornaments at the door, and their brilliance dazzled Yama's eyes. In remembrance of that act, it is considered auspicious on Dhanteras to buy gold, silver, or new utensils. This is not merely an investment — it is an ancient remembrance.

## Vidhi

- **Fasting:** A full fast is not obligatory. Some devotees observe a fruit-only fast until the Dhanvantari puja and take their meal afterward.
- **At dawn:** Bathe at sunrise; perform puja to Lord Dhanvantari — offer yellow flowers, turmeric, incense, and a lamp before his image; chant the Dhanvantari mantra. This puja is for the health and wellbeing of the family.
- **Buying gold and silver:** During an auspicious muhurta, purchase gold, silver, or new copper or brass utensils. As you bring them home, hold Lakshmi in your heart with the intention that this prosperity is for the family's wellbeing.
- **New broom:** Buying a new broom on Dhanteras is a symbol of Lakshmi's arrival; new kitchen vessels are also brought home.
- **Evening preparation:** Clean the house, make rangoli, and hang a torana garland at the door; arrange the puja thali for Diwali.
- **Pradosh kaal — Yama Deepam:** After sunset, during pradosh kaal (roughly one and a half hours after sunset), light a clay lamp outside the main entrance facing **south** — the direction of Yama. This is the Yama Deepam; it must always remain outside, never inside the home. Recite the Yama Deepam mantra. This protects the family from untimely death.
- **Evening — Lakshmi Puja:** Light lamps inside the house; perform puja of Lakshmi and Ganesha in the prayer room; offer incense, a lamp, and naivedya.
- **Deepadan:** Light lamps in every corner of the house, at the tulsi platform, and at the main entrance — let the whole home blaze with light.

## Mantras

**Dhanvantari Mantra:**

> ॐ नमो भगवते धन्वन्तरये अमृतकलशहस्ताय सर्वामय विनाशाय त्रैलोक्यनाथाय महाविष्णवे नमः ।

Oṃ namo bhagavate dhanvantaraye amṛtakalaśahastāya sarvāmaya vināśāya trailokyānāthāya mahāviṣṇave namaḥ |

Meaning: "Salutations to Lord Dhanvantari, who holds the vessel of amrita in his hands, who destroys all disease, who is the lord of the three worlds, the form of the great Vishnu."

**Yama Deepam Mantra:**

> मृत्युना पाशदण्डाभ्यां कालेन श्यामया सह ।
> तेभ्यो लोकहिताय च दीपो यम-प्रीतये ॥

Mṛtyunā pāśadaṇḍābhyāṃ kālena śyāmayā saha | tebhyo lokahitāya ca dīpo yama-prītaye ||

Meaning: "This lamp is lit for the pleasure of Yama and his retinue — Death, the noose, the rod, Kala, and Shyama — and for the good of all the world."
