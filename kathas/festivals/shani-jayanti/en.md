## Katha

**Sanjna's Departure for Tapasya:**

**Sanjna** — also known as Saanjnaa and Saranyु — daughter of the divine architect Vishwakarma, was Suryadev's first wife. Sanjna was radiant in her own right, but the brilliance of Surya was unbearable. Living within his boundless light was becoming more difficult for her with each passing day — as though one stood directly beside a lamp, yet that lamp was not an ordinary flame but the very source of light for the entire universe.

Sanjna gave birth to three children with Suryadev — **Vaivasvata Manu**, **Yama**, and **Yamuna**. After that, however, she could endure no longer.

Then Sanjna made an extraordinary decision.

Drawing upon the power of her own soul, she created a shadow-likeness of herself — **Chhaya** — who resembled Sanjna in appearance and speech alike. She then said to Chhaya: *"You shall serve Suryadev in my place. Care for the three children. But never tell anyone that you are not Sanjna."*

And Sanjna herself departed northward, into a dense forest, having taken the form of a mare — to perform fierce tapasya in the name of Lord Shiva.

---

**The Union of Chhaya and Surya:**

Chhaya kept the promise she had made with complete devotion. She remained in the service of Suryadev, who had no cause for suspicion — so complete was her dedication.

In time, three children were born to Suryadev and Chhaya — **Shani**, the river **Tapati**, and **Savarni Manu**.

Yet within Chhaya's heart there was always a difference. She cared for Sanjna's three children, but the pull of natural affection — toward her own — created an imperceptible distinction. Yama, who was growing into a young man, began to sense this difference.

---

**The Birth of Shani and the Marvel of the Divine Gaze:**

When Chhaya was carrying Shani in her womb, her mind was so absorbed in the worship of Shiva that there was no room left for ordinary worldly consciousness. Rigorous vrats, standing in meditation under the blazing sun, unceasing remembrance of Nilakantha Shiva — this was Chhaya's sadhana.

The energy of that tapasya poured steadily into the child within.

On Jyeshtha Krishna Amavasya, Shani was born. The newborn's complexion was dark (shyama) — a reflection of the dark energy of Shiva that had been absorbed through the mother's austerities. In Shani's gaze was a power that was anything but ordinary — the gravitational force of the Shani graha, that might of karmic justice which holds the universe in discipline.

When the newborn Shani's gaze fell upon Suryadev for the very first time — the Sun's face turned dark.

This was no accident. Such was the power in Shani's gaze that even his own father Suryadev could not bear that radiance. This is why that dark shadow is said to remain on the Sun's face to this day — a reminder of the first gaze of his son.

---

**The Revelation and the Reunion:**

As time passed, Yama and Yamuna came to feel that Chhaya did not hold for them the same tenderness she held for Shani and Tapati. Yama once spoke sharply to Chhaya — and in anger, Chhaya cursed him.

Yama went to his father Suryadev and told him everything.

Suryadev grew suspicious. He questioned Chhaya. She remained silent at first — then she broke. The truth came to light.

Suryadev searched for Sanjna. Vishwakarma revealed that Sanjna was in the forest, performing tapasya for Shiva in the form of a mare.

Suryadev went to the forest. There was a reunion with Sanjna. But the difficulty remained the same — Surya's unbearable radiance.

Then Sanjna's father Vishwakarma devised a remedy. He invited Suryadev to his divine workshop and reduced his brilliance slightly — as one might lower the flame of a lamp.

The energy that was trimmed away was not wasted. From that solar radiance, Vishwakarma fashioned Lord Vishnu's **Sudarshana Chakra**, Lord Shiva's **Trishula**, and many other divine weapons.

Surya and Sanjna were reunited.

---

**Shani — Neither Punishment nor Enemy; the Mirror of Justice:**

As Shanidev grew, he became the judge among the planets. He holds the ledger of karmic fruits — from birth upon birth. The **Sade Sati** of seven and a half years is his most spoken-of period. During it, Shani descends into a person's life — sometimes through wealth, sometimes through relationships, sometimes through health — and clears away whatever burden of accumulated karma has been carried.

Shani is not an enemy. He is the teacher whose class is hard — but the one who passes it learns life's deepest lesson.

The tradition of the Brahma Purana holds that the person who looks upon Shani not with fear but with devotion — who worships him with a sincere heart — remains steady even through Sade Sati, and in that fire only what was fit to be burned is consumed.

## Significance

**The Planet of Karma:**

Shanidev is the most just among the nine planets (navagrahas). He does not inflict suffering arbitrarily. Whatever comes is the fruit of what was done — in this life or in past lives. This is why Shani is called the **Karmaphala-data** — the giver of karmic fruits. In his court, neither bribe nor recommendation holds weight — only the scales of karma.

**Sade Sati — Not an Ordeal, but a Training:**

Sade Sati begins when Shani enters the twelfth house from a person's natal moon sign. It runs for seven and a half years. During this period life is turned upside down — but this upheaval is of the same kind as digging the earth before laying a foundation. Shani removes everything that was never truly fit to stand.

**The Bond between Shani and Shiva:**

Because of Chhaya's Shiva-sadhana, Shanidev is a supreme devotee of Shiva. In the worship of Shani, meditation on Shiva is deeply helpful — the Peepal tree is considered a dwelling of Shani, and by Shiva's grace the power of Shani finds its right direction.

**Shani Jayanti and Vat Savitri:**

Jyeshtha Krishna Amavasya is also the tithi on which Vat Savitri Vrat is observed in many regions. The convergence is profound — on one side, Shani, the deity of justice and karma; on the other, the sadhana of Savitri who preserved her husband's life. Both have the same root — karma, steadfast devotion, and unbreakable resolve.

## Vidhi

- **At dawn:** Rise before sunrise, bathe, and wear black or dark blue clothing (Shani's colours).
- **Fasting:** Observe a fast on Shani Jayanti — take only fruit during the day and break the fast after sunset; if a complete fast is not possible, eat salt-free food (salt is not offered to Shani).
- **Sesame-oil lamp:** Fill an iron lamp with sesame oil (mixed with black sesame seeds) and light it at dusk — this is the most central act of Shani Jayanti worship; recite the Shani Stotra alongside it.
- **Offerings:** Before Shanidev's image or picture offer black sesame seeds, black urad lentils, mustard oil, a small iron vessel, a piece of black cloth, and blue lotus flowers (if available).
- **Peepal worship:** Circumambulate the Peepal tree seven times; with each round offer a little water and sesame seeds; at the foot of the tree light a lamp and pour water mixed with raw milk at its roots — the Peepal is considered a dwelling place of Shani and the ancestors.
- **Shani Stotra:** Recite the Dasharatha-composed Shani Stuti or the Shani Chalisa — this stuti, which King Dasharatha recited during Sade Sati, is considered especially fruitful on this day.
- **Mantra japa:** Chant Oṃ Śaṃ Śanaiścarāya Namaḥ at least one mala (108 times); at sunset light an oil lamp and meditate on Shani.
- **Donation:** Give an iron vessel, a black blanket, black footwear, black sesame seeds, and urad lentils to someone poor or to a labourer — donating Shani-related items on this day is a direct path to his grace.

## Mantras

**Shani Mula Mantra:**

> ॐ शं शनैश्चराय नमः

Oṃ Śaṃ Śanaiścarāya Namaḥ

Meaning: "Salutation to Shanaishchara — he who moves slowly." This is the essential seed-mantra of Shani worship. Chanting one mala (108 times) daily brings stability even through an adverse Shani dasha.

---

**Dasharatha-composed Shani Stuti:**

> नमस्ते कोणसंस्थाय पिंगलाय नमोऽस्तु ते।
> नमस्ते बभ्रुरूपाय कृष्णाय च नमोऽस्तु ते॥

Namaste koṇa-saṃsthāya piṅgalāya namo'stu te |
Namaste babhru-rūpāya kṛṣṇāya ca namo'stu te ||

Meaning: "Salutation to the tawny-hued Shani who abides in the corner. Salutation to Shani of dark-brown and dark form." — This is the verse that King Dasharatha recited in praise of Shani during Sade Sati. Its recitation is said to alleviate the affliction of Shani.

---

**Navagraha Shani Shloka:**

> नीलांजनसमाभासं रविपुत्रं यमाग्रजम्।
> छायामार्तण्डसम्भूतं तं नमामि शनैश्चरम्॥

Nīlāñjana-samābhāsaṃ ravi-putraṃ yamāgrajam |
Chāyā-mārtaṇḍa-sambhūtaṃ taṃ namāmi śanaiścaram ||

Meaning: "I bow to Shanaishchara — whose radiance resembles blue collyrium (nilanjana), son of Surya, elder brother of Yama, born of Chhaya and the Sun." — This is the established Shani verse from the Navagraha Stotra; recite it at the beginning of Shani worship.
