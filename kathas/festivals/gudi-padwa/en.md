## Katha

This is the story of creation's beginning — when there was no earth, no sky, no passage of time. In the depths of darkness, only the Parabrahman existed. Then Brahma resolved to create. The Brahma Purana records that on the first day of Chaitra's bright fortnight — Shukla Pratipada — when the sun's very first ray touched the horizon, Brahma laid the foundation of creation. Sprinkling water from his kamandal, he shaped the world into being — the five elements, the wheel of time, day and night, the seasons, and the entire family of living creatures. That is why this date is also called **Yugadi** — the beginning of the age, the first moment of time.

Brahma himself declared this day the first day of the year. From that moment, Chaitra Shukla Pratipada became the opening day of the Hindu panchang. This is not merely a date — it is the memory of creation itself, a celebration of that first sunrise when Brahma swept away the darkness and built a world of light.

The second tradition is equally sacred. Rooted in the folk memory of the Valmiki Ramayana is the deep belief that on Chaitra Shukla Pratipada, Lord Shri Rama returned to Ayodhya after fourteen years of exile. Mother Kaushalya's long vigil was fulfilled — her Rama descended from the Pushpaka Vimana with Lakshmana and Sita at his side. Lamps were lit at every doorway in Ayodhya, banners were raised, and the people took up their vijay-dhwaj in joy.

In Maharashtra, the **Gudi** is raised in memory of that very victory. A long bamboo pole is wrapped in bright silk — saffron or yellow — and an inverted copper or silver kalash is placed at the top. Sprigs of neem and mango leaves are woven in, and a garland of flowers is offered. This Gudi is the vijay-dhwaj — the flag of Rama's triumph, of truth's triumph, of the dawn of a new creation.

Rising before sunrise, bathing, and erecting the Gudi facing east is the heart of this festival. When the Gudi catches the morning light and gleams against the sky, a single feeling stirs in the heart — this is a new year, a new time, a new life. The joy of Brahma's creation and the glory of Rama's victorious return are both gathered into that one flag.

The Brahma Purana tradition holds that one who raises the Gudi on Chaitra Shukla Pratipada with proper rites, listens to the creation-katha, and worships both Brahma and Shri Rama receives the fruit of a full year's prayers.

## Significance

Gudi Padwa is not merely Maharashtra's new year — it is one of the most ancient festivals of the Sanatana tradition. According to the Brahma Purana, Brahma began the work of creation on this very date; it is therefore regarded as the source of all auspicious beginnings. The Hindu panchang counts its days from this very morning. In Karnataka and Andhra, it is called **Ugadi** — uga (to rise) + adi (beginning) — the rising of new light.

The tradition of Rama's return to Ayodhya fills this festival with joy and triumph. Fourteen years of exile, the slaying of Ravana, Sita's liberation — all of this is a story of truth's victory over falsehood. When Rama returned, it was not only a prince who came home — it was the establishment of maryada, dharma, and truth. The Gudi that the people of Ayodhya raised was not merely a welcome — it was a proclamation of dharma's victory.

The neem-jaggery prasad of this festival reveals a deep philosophical vision. Neem is bitter, jaggery is sweet — life brings both. To receive both together on the first morning of the new year carries this message: accept joy and sorrow alike, with equanimity. This is the Sanatana way of life.

## Vidhi

- **At dawn:** Rise before sunrise; bathe with a sesame-paste scrub; decorate the main entrance with a torana of mango leaves and draw rangoli below where the Gudi will stand.
- **Raising the Gudi:** Take a long bamboo pole; wrap it in bright silk (yellow or saffron); place an inverted copper or silver kalash at the top; weave in neem sprigs and mango leaves; adorn with a flower garland. Raise the Gudi on the roof or to the right of the main entrance, facing east, at the moment of sunrise.
- **Puja:** Worship Brahma and Shri Rama — offer incense, a lamp, flowers, and naivedya; listen to the creation-katha and the story of Rama's return to Ayodhya.
- **Panchang recitation:** Listen to the panchang on the first day of the new year to learn the coming year's seasons, tithis, and auspicious muhurtas — a remembrance of Brahma's establishment of the reckoning of time.
- **Neem-jaggery prasad:** Receive the prasad made from tender neem shoots, jaggery, tamarind, cumin, and salt — a symbol of accepting life's bitter and sweet experiences with equanimity.
- **At dusk:** Lower the Gudi and place it respectfully inside the home.

## Mantras

> ॐ ब्रह्मणे नमः।
> सृष्टिकर्त्रे नमस्तुभ्यं वेदाय प्रभवे नमः।
> चतुर्मुखाय शान्ताय त्रिगुणाय नमो नमः॥

Om brahmaṇe namaḥ.
Sṛṣṭikartre namastubhyaṃ vedāya prabhave namaḥ.
Caturmukhāya śāntāya triguṇāya namo namaḥ.

Meaning: Salutations to Brahma. O Creator of the universe, source of the Vedas, four-faced one, the embodiment of peace, beyond the three gunas — I bow to you again and again.
