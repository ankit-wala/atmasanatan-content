## Katha

Treta Yuga. Mithila — the capital of the kingdom of Videha. Its king was Janaka — Janaka Mithilesh, whom people also called "Videha." Videha — meaning one who has gone beyond the sense of the body. He was king and rishi both, taking up the sword and teaching Vedanta alike. Dharma was so deeply rooted in his kingdom that Mithila itself was regarded as a tirtha.

It was a day in the month of Vaishakha. Shukla Paksha, the ninth tithi.

King Janaka had resolved to perform a great yajna. Before the yajna, the earth had to be purified — and the shastric tradition held that the king himself must plow the ritual ground to consecrate it. This was no mere act of agriculture; it was a sacred rite. Janaka took up a golden plow with his own hands and descended into the field.

He plowed on — one furrow, two furrows. The earth parted, and kept parting.

Then the tip of the plow struck something hard and stopped.

Janaka bent down and looked. Beneath the earth, under layers of soil, there was a golden casket.

He drew it out with his own hands. He wiped away the mud. He opened the lid.

Inside — wrapped in silk — lay an infant girl.

Newly born. Her body glowed like the first flush of dawn, as though the earliest ray of sunrise had emerged from the lap of the earth. On her face was a stillness — as if she had known from birth exactly where she was and to whom she belonged.

Janaka stood transfixed.

Across the entire royal assembly, among all the rishis and sages gathered there, everything fell silent for one moment.

Then Janaka lifted that infant girl into his arms. His eyes filled with tears — but these were not tears of sorrow. This was the feeling that comes when a deity suddenly appears at your door and you do not even know when it happened.

He understood — this is Mother Earth's gift. On this sacred yajna-ground, on this navami tithi, Bhumi-devi had placed her most beloved daughter into his lap.

Janaka accepted her as his daughter.

The tip of the plow is called sita in Sanskrit — the furrow the plow draws is called "sita." She who was found from that sita (the furrow of the plow) was called **Sita**.

---

She was also **Janaki** — daughter of Janaka. **Maithili** — princess of Mithila. **Bhumi-ja** — daughter of the earth.

When Queen Sunayana took that infant girl into her own lap, the bond of mother and daughter was formed — one that will be remembered across lifetimes.

Sita's childhood passed in the royal palace of Mithila. She became proficient in the shastras. She knew the science of the bow. The words of the Vedas were on her tongue. But her greatest power was her bhava — her inner being. In all that she was, there was a deep purity that had come from the earth — that which only the earth can give.

---

In the tradition of the Valmiki Ramayana it is said that one who hears this birth-katha and worships Sitaji on the day of Sita Navami receives the blessing of Bhumi-devi, finds peace and sweetness in household life, and forever remains in the grace of Lord Rama.

And one fact that touches the heart —

**Ram Navami falls on Chaitra Shukla Navami. Sita Navami falls on Vaishakha Shukla Navami.**

Both on navami. Both in the spring season. One from the sky — Suryavanshi kshatriya. One from the earth — daughter of Bhumi-devi.

It is as though creation had decided beforehand — the navami tithi belongs to these two alone.

## Significance

Sita Navami is not merely the celebration of a birthday — it is the celebration of Mother Earth's blessing.

Sita is **Bhumi-ja** — daughter of the earth. She was not born from any mother's womb; she appeared in a furrow, in the sacred yajna-ground. This means that in Sita all the qualities of the earth reside — forbearance, fertility, unblemished purity, and that quiet strength which endures every storm and yet goes on giving life.

In the Vedic tradition, **Bhumi Devi** is among the most ancient of goddesses. She holds — all burdens, all sins, all sorrows. And she gives — grain, water, shelter. Sita is the daughter of this very goddess. This is why in Rama's life she is not merely a wife — she is also Rama's shakti. Without Sita, Rama's kingdom is incomplete.

**The confluence of Ram Navami and Sita Navami** — Chaitra Shukla Navami and Vaishakha Shukla Navami — this confluence tells us that Rama and Sita are each other's completions. Like sky and earth. Like sun and ground. The sun's heat alone would scorch the earth; the earth alone would remain in darkness — it is their union that makes life possible.

Observing the vrat of Sita Navami in the month of Vaishakha brings the special blessing of Bhumi-devi. For those who seek peace in the home, the happiness of children, and a tender bond in marriage, this vrat is held to be especially fruitful.

## Vidhi

- **At dawn:** Rise before sunrise, bathe, and put on white or yellow clothing — both are beloved by Sita-Rama.
- **Puja setup:** Install an image or portrait of Sita-Rama at the place of worship; place Sitaji at the centre — today is her birthday; bow to Sitaji first ("Jai Siyaram" — Siya before Rama — is the proper order for today).
- **Offerings:** Offer white flowers (jasmine, mogra, or lotus — symbols of purity dear to Sitaji) and tulasi leaves and manjari — tulasi is ever present in the service of Rama-Sita and its offering today is especially meritorious.
- **Bhumi-puja:** At the courtyard or near the tulasi plant, offer water and flowers to Mother Earth — a thankful honouring of Bhumi-devi who sent Sita into this world.
- **Abhisheka and naivedya:** Perform abhisheka of Sita-Rama with panchamrita (milk, curd, ghee, honey, sugar); place kheer, mishri, and seasonal fruit as naivedya.
- **Katha-path:** Recite the Janaka-Sita birth-katha — the episode of Janaka's plowing in the Bala Kanda of the Valmiki Ramayana; gather the family to hear it together.
- **Fasting:** Fast on fruit; many devotees observe nirjala vrat; break the fast (parana) after sunrise on the Dashami tithi.

## Mantras

**1. Om Janakivallabhaya Namah**

> ॐ जानकीवल्लभाय नमः।
> ॐ जय सीताराम।

Om Jānakīvallabhāya Namaḥ.
Om Jaya Sītārāma.

Meaning: I bow to the beloved of Janaki — Lord Rama. Victory to Sita-Rama together. This mantra is the symbol of the joint worship of Sita-Rama — both are to be venerated together.

---

**2. Sita janma stuti (Valmiki Ramayana, Bala Kanda)**

> जनकस्य कुले जाता भूमेः कन्या सुमध्यमा।
> सीता नाम्नी विशालाक्षी सर्वलक्षणसंयुता॥

Janakasya kule jātā bhūmeḥ kanyā sumadhyamā.
Sītā nāmnī viśālākṣī sarvalakṣaṇasaṃyutā.

Meaning: Born in the lineage of Janaka, daughter of the earth, of graceful and proportionate form, wide-eyed, adorned with every auspicious mark — she is Sita. This shloka describes the divine form of Sita.
