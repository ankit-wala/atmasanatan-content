## Katha

From the womb of the Earth an asura was born — Narakasura. He was the son of Bhumi Devi, the Earth Goddess, and was therefore also known as Bhauma. He had obtained a boon from Brahma: his death could come only at the hands of his own mother — a woman. Sheltered by that boon, the tamas within him grew without limit.

Narakasura waged war upon all three worlds. He defeated Indra and tore from the ears of Aditi, the mother of the gods, her divine earrings — earrings she had worn since the very dawn of creation. He seized Indra's royal canopy, the emblem of the sovereignty of the gods. And worst of all: he imprisoned sixteen thousand maidens, daughters of kings and sages. The hermitages of the rishis were laid waste. Terror swept through the heavens.

The gods came before Lord Krishna and implored him for help.

Krishna resolved to go to war. But he took with him his wife **Satyabhama**. Satyabhama was no ordinary woman — she was herself a part of Bhumi Devi. The true fulfilment of the boon was this: Narakasura's own mother would be the instrument of his end.

Mounted on Garuda, Krishna and Satyabhama flew toward Pragjyotishpur, the fortress city of Narakasura. The city was ringed by five great moats, by towering mountain ranges, and by divine barriers of water and wind. One by one, Krishna pierced through every defence.

Fierce battle broke out with Narakasura's armies. In the midst of the fighting, Narakasura hurled the shakti-astra at Krishna. Krishna fell unconscious, slumping forward on the chariot.

It was yogamaya — divine illusion. Krishna had deliberately given Satyabhama her moment.

Satyabhama's fury blazed forth. The power of the Earth within her awoke. She raised her bow, drew the arrow — and struck Narakasura. He fell to the ground.

In the moment of death, Narakasura's heart was transformed. He prayed to Krishna: "O Lord, I am blessed to die at your hands. One final wish: let those who bathe in oil and light lamps on this day know this day not as a day of grief, but as a day of celebration." Krishna said: tathāstu — so be it.

Krishna then freed those sixteen thousand maidens. Those who could not return to the world — because society would not receive them — were honoured by Krishna and given shelter in Dwarka.

The tradition of the Bhagavata Purana declares: whoever performs the abhyanga-snan and lights lamps on Narak Chaturdashi is freed from all fear of hell and becomes a sharer in great merit.

## Significance

Narak Chaturdashi — known also as **Kali Chaudas**, **Chhoti Diwali**, and **Roop Chaudas** — is celebrated the night before Diwali. The soul of this festival lives on four levels:

**1. Liberation from Naraka.** "Naraka" means not only the mythological netherworld — it is any condition in which the soul is trapped in tamas. Narakasura is the embodiment of tamas. His slaying is the proclamation that light conquers darkness.

**2. The supremacy of Shakti.** It was not Krishna but Satyabhama who slew Narakasura. The power of the Earth destroyed the tamas that had arisen from her own womb. This festival is a celebration of that form of feminine power which is capable of both creation and dissolution.

**3. The boon of the abhyanga-snan.** This is Narakasura's final gift — whoever bathes in oil in memory of his death shall not become a partaker of hell. It is a rite of purification: to cleanse oneself and thus earn the right to celebrate.

**4. The festival of lamps on Diwali's eve.** The lamps lit on this night show the way to departed souls — an extension of the ancestral tradition of Pitrupaksha. Before the great night of Diwali, this night burns away the last remnants of darkness.

## Vidhi

**1. Abhyanga-snan — before sunrise.**
On Narak Chaturdashi, rise before sunrise and massage the body with sesame oil or coconut oil. After the massage apply a ubtan and then bathe. This ritual oil bath before sunrise is the most essential act of this day — the direct honouring of Narakasura's boon.

**2. Lighting fourteen lamps.**
In the evening, light fourteen clay lamps at the entrance to the home, near the tulasi plant, and on the rooftop. The number fourteen — corresponding to the chaturdashi — carries this symbolic weight.

**3. The lamp for Yama — facing south.**
Place one lamp facing south — this is for Yamaraj, so that no member of the household meets an untimely death.

**4. Lakshmi-Ganesha puja.**
In the evening worship, perform puja of Lakshmi and Ganesha. Offer red flowers, sweets, incense, and lamps.

**5. Remembrance of the slaying of Narakasura.**
Read aloud or listen to the episode of Narakasura-vadha from the Dasham Skandha of the Bhagavata Purana.

**6. Mantra for the abhyanga-snan.**
Recite before the oil massage: abhyaṅge narakasrāṇa tailābhyaṅgo yamapriye | caturdaśyāṃ prayuktasya yamaḥ prīto bhaviṣyati ||

**7. Lighting firecrackers.**
This tradition too is connected to the proclamation of Narakasura's end. Light and sound drive away all negative forces.

## Observance

On Narak Chaturdashi, the **abhyanga-snan before sunrise** is considered essential — it is the foundational observance of this day. In Maharashtra this tradition is followed with particular devotion.

In some families a fast is kept on this day — especially in households where the tradition of Yama-puja is observed under the name Kali Chaudas.

Lighting lamps in the evening is essential — it is an honouring of Narakasura's last boon.

On this night, lamps are also lit for the ancestors, so that their souls may receive peace and light.

## Mantras

**Mantra 1 — Remembrance of the slaying of Narakasura**

> नमो नारायणाय महाशक्तये तेजसे ।
> नरकासुरहराय सत्यभामासहायिने ॥

Namo nārāyaṇāya mahāśaktaye tejase | narakāsurahārāya satyabhāmāsahāyine ||

Meaning: "Salutation to Lord Narayana — the greatly powerful, the radiant, the destroyer of Narakasura, who was aided by Satyabhama."

**Mantra 2 — Lamp Mantra**

> दीपो ज्योतिः परं ब्रह्म दीपो ज्योतिर्जनार्दनः ।
> दीपो हरतु मे पापं दीपज्योतिर्नमोऽस्तु ते ॥

Dīpo jyotiḥ paraṃ brahma dīpo jyotirjanārdanaḥ | dīpo haratu me pāpaṃ dīpajyotirnamostu te ||

Meaning: "The lamp is the supreme Brahman, the lamp is Janardana. May the lamp take away my sins — salutation to the light of the lamp."
