## Katha

It was the full-moon tithi of the bright fortnight of the month of Chaitra. The sky blazed with a perfect moon, and on a rocky summit of Mount Sumeru a tapasvinī vanari — Anjana — sat absorbed in meditation, seated in padmasana. Her palms lay open, her eyes were closed, and Shiva's name rested on her lips.

Anjana was in truth an apsara who had been born in the form of a vanari because of a curse. There was only one path to release from that curse — to give birth to a son who would be extraordinary, who would surpass even the great. For years she had been worshipping Lord Shiva and Vayu Dev with this single prayer: that she be given a heroic, radiant son.

At that very time, Ayodhya's king Dasharatha was performing a putrakameshti yajna. At the yajna's completion a vessel appeared from the fire-pit containing payasa — divine sacred pudding. Vayu Dev lifted a portion of that payasa on a current of air and bore it away. And when that divine payasa fell into Anjana's open palms, she received it with reverence — and in that instant a divine radiance entered her womb.

On that auspicious purnima, on that very mountain peak beneath a banyan tree, Anjana gave birth to a child. The child's brilliance was that of the sun itself; his body gleamed like gold. His father Kesari — the mighty vanara — swayed with joy. Sages sang praises. Vayu Dev himself was present there, invisible — gazing upon his son.

---

That child grew — in brilliance, in courage, and in curiosity too.

One morning the young Hanuman was playing alone. On the eastern horizon the sun was rising — red, bright, and round.

The child's eyes lit up. He thought: "This must be some ripe red fruit!"

And he sprang.

In a single leap he was in the sky. His speed was such that even the wind would be astonished. He surged toward the sun.

That day Rahu too was approaching the sun — to cause an eclipse. He saw that someone else was also moving in the sun's direction. Rahu rushed in panic to Indra.

Indra looked — a vanara child was hurtling toward his dear friend Suryadev. He thought this must be some demon come to devour the sun.

Indra raised his vajra.

The vajra struck the chin — the hanu — of the young Hanuman.

A sharp crack — and Hanuman fell to the earth.

---

Vayu Dev looked — his son lay unconscious, injured.

His heart broke. His fury rose.

He declared: "If there is no protection for my son in this world, then I too shall give no breath to this world."

And the wind stopped.

Instantly — a cry of anguish rose through all creation. Not a leaf stirred, not a breath moved. Trees began to wither; living beings gasped in suffocation; even the gods grew distressed. All of creation seemed to have forgotten how to breathe.

The gods came to Brahma. Brahma himself went to Vayu Dev. He saw — in Vayu Dev's arms Hanuman lay motionless.

Brahma touched the crown of Hanuman's head with his lotus hands — and the child's eyes opened.

The moment he was alive, the wind flowed again. Creation breathed.

---

Now one by one, all the gods came and bestowed varadans upon Hanuman.

Indra came. Raising his vajra he said: "The chin this vajra struck shall become this child's very mark of identity — and so he shall be called Hanuman. And my varadan is this: henceforth this vajra shall never bring him any harm."

Suryadev came — he on whom Hanuman had leapt, himself. He said: "This child came to me — I shall give him my radiance. And when the time comes, I shall teach him all the shastras and all knowledge."

Varuna pledged to protect him with his noose. Yama declared: my rod shall not touch him. Vishwakarma gave a varadan — no weapon shall be able to slay him. Kubera said: may he always have inexhaustible strength and energy.

Lord Shiva gave his varadan — all his own weapons and instruments would be unable to harm Hanuman.

And finally Brahma himself declared: "My Brahmastra shall have no effect on him. And my greatest varadan is this — as long as the name of Rama resounds upon this earth, this child shall remain Chiranjeevi."

In the tradition of the Valmiki Ramayana it is said that on that dawn, on that mountain peak, the child who had been born was not merely the son of a vanara. He was the very fragment of Vayu, a rudravatar of Shiva, and the first form of Rama's unwavering devotee.

## Significance

Hanuman Jayanti is not merely a birthday — it is the celebration of that great power's descent to earth, the power that gives strength to the weak and fearlessness to the frightened. Hanuman is regarded much as the Navadurga — wherever he is present, fear cannot dwell. His birth on Chaitra Purnima is especially significant because this is the very month in which Ramnawami is observed — the greatest devotee was born close to the very days of his Lord's birth. This bond between devotee and God is not coincidence; it is the lila of Vidhata.

Hanuman is the ideal of bhakti — a bhakti that carries no ego, that is wholly surrendered, that uses power only in service. He did not fear the flames in Lanka, he was not afraid of the ocean's waves, and he stood alone before an army of demons — because he was Rama's bhakta. Shiva called him his own rudravatar; Vayu gave him life; Surya gave him knowledge — the strength of all three worlds was gathered in that one devotee.

Sundarkand — which is recited with special devotion on this day — is the account of Hanuman's journey to Lanka. It is not merely a story; it is proof that when a devotee's mind is fixed on Rama, even the impossible becomes possible. On the day of Hanuman Jayanti, hearing his katha, reciting Sundarkand, and chanting his name — this is his truest worship.

## Vidhi

- **At sunrise:** Bathe early; wear red or saffron clothes; offer flowers, akshat, roli, and chandan before Hanuman's image or portrait.
- **Sindoor chola:** Apply pure sindoor mixed with a little ghee on Hanuman's image — this is the day's most distinctive act of worship, rooted in the tradition of Hanuman covering his whole body with sindoor in Rama's court.
- **Sesame-oil lamp:** Light a sesame-oil lamp during worship and keep it burning through the night, as sesame-oil lamps are especially dear to Hanuman.
- **Sundarkand path:** Recite the complete Sundarkand alone or in a group — this day is regarded as its most auspicious occasion. Those unable to complete the full text should at minimum recite the Hanuman Chalisa eleven times.
- **Offerings:** Offer boondi, panjiri, or seasonal fruit as prasad; perform aarti again in the evening.
- **Fasting:** Observe a nirjala or fruit-only fast; those unable to fast completely should abstain from grain and take only fruit; break the fast after sunset.

## Mantras

**1. Mula Mantra**

> ॐ हनुमते नमः

Om Hanumate Namah

Meaning: Salutations to Hanuman. This is Hanuman's fundamental name-mantra — simple, powerful, and accessible to all.

---

**2. Hanuman Gayatri**

> ॐ आञ्जनेयाय विद्महे वायुपुत्राय धीमहि ।
> तन्नो हनुमत् प्रचोदयात् ॥

Om Anjaneyaya Vidmahe Vayuputraya Dhimahi |
Tanno Hanumat Prachodayat ||

Meaning: We know the son of Anjana, we meditate upon the son of Vayu — may that Hanuman inspire and impel our intellect and strength.

---

**3. Birth-Victory Verse**

> अञ्जनानन्दनं वीरं जानकीशोकनाशनम् ।
> कपीशमक्षहन्तारं वन्दे लङ्काभयङ्करम् ॥

Anjananandanam Viram Janakishokanashanam |
Kapishakshahantaram Vande Lankabhayankaram ||

Meaning: I bow to the hero who brings joy to Anjana, who destroys the grief of Janaki, who is lord of the vanaras and the slayer of Aksha, the one who spread terror through Lanka — to that Hanuman I offer my salutations.
