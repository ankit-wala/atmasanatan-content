## Katha

Kamsa had imprisoned Devaki and Vasudeva — because a divine voice had declared that their eighth son would slay him. One by one, Kamsa killed each child at the moment of birth.

Devaki conceived for the seventh time.

This was no ordinary child. The Lord Vishnu's beloved Shesha — Sankarshana — had himself descended into Devaki's womb.

Lord Vishnu commanded Yogamaya:

"Take what rests in Devaki's womb — gently — and remove it. In Gokul, Vasudeva's second wife Rohini is living at the house of Nanda. Place it in her womb."

Yogamaya did exactly that.

Devaki's womb became empty. The guards reported to Kamsa: "The seventh child did not survive in the womb. It was a curse."

---

In Gokul, at the home of Mother Rohini, a radiant son was born — fair-complexioned, strong-limbed, with calm eyes.

This child was Sankarshana — the one who had been "drawn" from one womb into another. Because of this act of drawing (karshana), he was named: **Sankarshana**.

Mother Rohini raised him as her own son. Mother Devaki, in whose womb he was first carried, would meet him only when Kamsa had met his end.

This Sankarshana came to be known as **Balarama** — the mighty one, the bearer of the plough.

His weapon was the plough — symbol of agriculture, symbol of strength. **Haladhara** — the one who bears the plough. **Halayudha** — whose weapon is the plough.

Once he drew the river Yamuna herself with that very plough. He slew many demons. He remained always at the side of his younger brother Krishna — wherever Krishna, there Balarama.

In the tradition of the Bhagavata Purana, Tenth Canto, it is said that the mother who fasts on this Shashthi for her sons and remembers Balarama receives, by Balarama's grace, the blessing of long life and fame for her sons.

## Significance

Balarama — avatar of Shesha, elder brother of Krishna.

The name Hal Shashthi comes from the plough — the weapon that is Balarama's mark. Shashthi is his birthday. Therefore, on this day, ploughing the earth is considered forbidden — for on the birthday of the one whose symbol is the plough, that symbol should not be put to labour.

This is a special fast for mothers. He who received the love of two mothers is worshipped by mothers seeking protection for their own sons.

## Vidhi

This procedure follows the North Indian regional tradition:

1. Bathe at dawn and take the vow to fast — for the long life and protection of a son.
2. Install an idol or image of Lord Balarama.
3. Offer white flowers, sesame, milk, and curd — white is Balarama's beloved colour.
4. Perform Panchamrita abhisheka.
5. Offer aarti with lamp and incense.
6. In the North Indian tradition, do not eat vegetables grown in tilled earth — consume only those grown in water or without ploughing (water chestnuts, milk, curd).
7. Break the fast in the evening or the following morning.

## Observance

**Mothers' fast:** This fast is observed primarily by mothers for the long life of their sons.

**Plough observance:** In the North Indian tradition, do not till the earth with a plough on this day.

**Food:** In the North Indian regional tradition, vegetables grown from tilled earth are avoided.

**2026 date:** Verify on DrikPanchang (Bhadrapada Krishna Shashthi — approximately September 3, 2026).

## Mantras

**Balarama seed mantra:**

> ॐ बलरामाय नमः

Oṃ Balarāmāya Namaḥ

Meaning: "Salutation to Lord Balarama."

**Revati Stuti:**

> नमस्ते रेवते देवि नमस्ते सुरपूजिते ।
> नमस्ते बलदेवस्य भार्ये नमस्ते शक्तिरूपिणि ॥

Namaste revati devi namaste surapūjite |
Namaste baladevasya bhārye namaste śaktirūpiṇi ||

Meaning: "O Goddess Revati, salutation to you. O one worshipped by the gods, salutation. O consort of Baladeva, O embodiment of Shakti, salutation."

*(Reciting this praise of Revati — Balarama's wife — is considered auspicious on Hal Shashthi.)*
