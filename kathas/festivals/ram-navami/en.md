## Katha

In the Treta Yuga, Ayodhya, the capital of the Kosala kingdom, was a great city. Its king, Dasharatha — the radiant sovereign of the Ikshvaku dynasty — was peerless in righteousness, valour, and the care of his people. He had three queens: Kaushalya, Kaikeyi, and Sumitra. All three were virtuous and devoted to dharma. Yet a deep grief lay in Dasharatha's heart — he had no son. The fear that the line of the Ikshvaku dynasty might be broken, and the anguish of having no heir to uphold dharma, kept him in constant torment.

On the counsel of Maharshi Vashishtha, Maharaja Dasharatha resolved to perform the Putrakameshti Yajna — the son-wishing sacrifice. He invited the sage Rishyashringa to preside as the yajna's chief priest. Rishyashringa was the son of Maharshi Vibhandaka — a great ascetic, a man of wisdom, and supremely accomplished in the science of yajna. Under his leadership the ritual commenced. The hall of sacrifice rang with the sound of Vedic mantras; fragrant flames from the sacred fire reached toward the heavens.

At the moment of the final oblation, a wondrous event occurred. From the yajna's fire there emerged a divine being — dark of complexion, adorned in golden raiment, bearing in his hands a golden vessel filled with payasa, the sacred pudding. He addressed Maharaja Dasharatha: "O King, this payasa is the grace of Prajapati — offer it to your queens, and you shall receive sons of divine glory." Dasharatha received the payasa with full reverence. He gave half to Kaushalya; half of the remainder — that is, one quarter — to Kaikeyi; and the remaining portion he divided into two equal parts for Sumitra — one for Lakshmana, one for Shatrughna.

The queens took the payasa. A wave of joy swept through the royal palace.

In time the queens conceived. And then came that sacred moment for which not only Ayodhya, but all three worlds, had been waiting.

**Chaitra month, the bright fortnight, the ninth day — Navami** — it was Madhyahna, the noon hour, when the sun stood directly overhead at the zenith of the sky. The auspicious confluence of Karka lagna and Punarvasu nakshatra prevailed. In that blessed muhurta, Mother Kaushalya gave birth to a child.

But this was no ordinary child.

Kaushalya beheld before her a divine presence. Dark as a rain-filled cloud, eyes wide as blooming lotus flowers, four arms bearing the Panchajanya (the divine conch), the Sudarshan Chakra (the spinning disc of light), the Kaumodaki (the mace), and the lotus — this was Vishnu himself, the Sustainer of all worlds, who had descended in human form, having heard the cry of the earth and of the gods, suffering under the tyranny of Ravana. Kaushalya was overwhelmed. Tears of joy streamed from her eyes.

Then, in an instant, that divine splendour was absorbed — and the sweet, enchanting cry of a tender infant filled the air.

At that very moment, the celestial drums of heaven sounded. The gods showered flowers from the sky. A divine fragrance filled all of Ayodhya. Brahma, Vishnu, Mahesh, and all the devas swayed in bliss. The sages and seers gave voice to the Vedic hymns. The heavens above and the realms below were all swept up in exultation.

The tradition of Valmiki's Ramayana holds that when news of this birth spread, the gandharvas broke into song, the apsaras began to dance, and in the unseen world the conch of Ravana's end was sounded. The path of dharma, for whose protection Vishnu had taken this avatar, had now been made ready.

When Maharaja Dasharatha saw the face of his son, his eyes filled with tears. He bestowed gifts upon the Brahmins and proclaimed a festival throughout the city. The name Ayodhya — she who cannot be conquered — had found its fulfilment.

That infant was named — **Rama**.

## Significance

Ram Navami is not merely the celebration of a birth — it is the festival of dharma's restoration. Rama descended on Chaitra Shukla Navami at the Madhyahna hour because the ninth day, Navami, is held to be the tithi of power and spiritual attainment, and Madhyahna is the hour when the sun's energy and brilliance reach their peak. This confluence — Navami, Madhyahna, and Karka lagna — deepens the divine significance of this avatar. This is why the abhisheka of Rama's birth is performed precisely at noon.

Rama is Maryada Purushottama — the best among men, the ideal of righteous limits. He came not only to slay Ravana; he came to show how dharma, truth, devotion to one's father, love for one's wife, and care for one's people — all these values — can be lived together as one. His life itself is scripture. This is why the Valmiki Ramayana is called the "Adi Kavya" — the first great poem — the portrait of the ideal human being.

Ram Navami in the Chaitra month holds special significance because it falls nine days after the beginning of the new year — on the final day of Navaratri. The nine days of shakti-sadhana in Navaratri find their completion in the festival of Rama's birth. This confluence of shakti and bhakti is celebrated with particular grandeur in Ayodhya.

## Vidhi

- **Fasting:** Observe a fruit-only or nirjala fast; the fast is broken the following day (Dashami tithi) after sunrise.
- **At dawn:** Bathe early; wear white or yellow garments; establish images of Rama, Sita, Lakshmana, and Hanumanji in the household shrine; place an image of infant Rama in a jhula (cradle/swing).
- **Offerings:** Panchopachara worship (fragrance, flowers, incense, lamp, food); offer panchamrita, kheer, and tulasi leaves.
- **Madhyahna Abhisheka (noon):** At precisely noon — when the sun is at its highest — bathe the infant Rama image with milk, curd, honey, ghee, and Gangajal; blow the conch; light lamps; proclaim "Jai Shri Ram!" and announce Ram Janma — this is the central moment of the festival.
- **Ramayana recitation:** Recite the Bala Kanda of the Valmiki Ramayana or the Ramcharitmanas; the birth passage from "tato yajñe samāpte tu..." through the naming of Rama should be read with special attention; group recitation and the Sundara Kanda are also meritorious.
- **Evening:** Perform the aarti and distribute prasad.

## Mantras

**1. Shri Rama Nama Mantra**

> श्रीराम जय राम जय जय राम।

Sri Rama jaya Rama jaya jaya Rama.

Meaning: O Shri Rama, may you be victorious — this is the taraka mantra that severs the bonds of life after life.

---

**2. Rama Janma Shloka (Valmiki Ramayana, Bala Kanda)**

> ततो यज्ञे समाप्ते तु ऋतूनां षट् समत्ययुः।
> ततश्च द्वादशे मासे चैत्रे नावमिके तिथौ।
> नक्षत्रेऽदितिदैवत्ये स्वोच्चसंस्थेषु पञ्चसु।
> ग्रहेषु कर्कटे लग्ने वाक्पताविन्दुना सह।
> प्रोद्यमाने जगन्नाथं सर्वलोकनमस्कृतम्।
> कौसल्याऽजनयद्रामं दिव्यलक्षणसंयुतम्॥

tato yajñe samāpte tu ṛtūnāṃ ṣaṭ samatyayuḥ.
tataśca dvādaśe māse caitre nāvamike tithau.
nakṣatre 'ditidaivatye svoccasaṃstheṣu pañcasu.
graheṣu karkaṭe lagne vākpatāv indunā saha.
prodyamāne jagannāthaṃ sarvalokanamaskṛtam.
kausalyājanayad rāmaṃ divyalakṣaṇasaṃyutam.

Meaning: After the yajna was completed, six seasons passed. Then, in the twelfth month, on the ninth day of Chaitra, under the nakshatra presided over by Aditi, with five planets in their exalted positions, and with Jupiter joined by the moon in Karka lagna — Mother Kaushalya gave birth to Rama, the Lord of the Universe, adorned with divine marks, to whom all the worlds bow.

**3. Rama Taraka Mantra**

> ॐ रां रामाय नमः।

Om Rāṃ Rāmāya Namaḥ.

Meaning: Om — together with the taraka bija "Ram" — I bow to Rama. This seed mantra is the essence of Rama's power; through its repetition the impurities of the mind are removed and devotion becomes firm.
