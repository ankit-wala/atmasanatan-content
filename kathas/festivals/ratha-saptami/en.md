## Katha

The shukla saptami of the month of Magha — the day when Suryadev turns his golden chariot northward and fresh light begins to move through creation. This sacred tithi is called Ratha Saptami — the birthday of Suryadev.

In ancient times there lived a king named Yashovarma. He was steadfast in dharma, a protector of his people, and devoted to the gods. His kingdom knew joy and prosperity, yet within his palace a deep sorrow dwelt. His only son had been born frail and sickly. Physicians gave countless medicines; yajnas were performed; vows were made to the gods — yet the boy's condition remained unchanged. The hearts of his parents were broken.

One day the divine sage Narada arrived at the royal court. He looked upon the boy, and his celestial sight descended into past and future alike. He said: "O King, this child's misfortune is not without cause. A deed from a previous life has followed him into this one."

Yashovarma folded his hands humbly and asked: "Revered one, what was the transgression?"

Naradaji said: "In his previous life, this child too had the fortune of a king. Once, on the day of Magha shukla saptami, he was riding through a forest path on horseback. On that very road a brahmin was travelling on foot. The prince neither reined in his horse nor gave way — and the brahmin suffered distress. The tithi on which this offence occurred was consecrated to Suryadev — Magha shukla saptami. The insult shown to a learned brahmin on the holy day of Suryadev — that is the sin which has returned in this life as disease."

King Yashovarma's heart grew heavy. He asked: "What is the remedy now?"

Naradaji replied: "Observe the vrat of Ratha Saptami. On Magha shukla saptami, before sunrise, place seven arka leaves (Calotropis, the swallow-wort) on your head and take the abhyanga snan — the oil bath. Offer arghya to the rising Sun. Recite the Aditya Hridayam and worship the solar chariot drawn by its seven horses. Suryadev rides upon a chariot of seven horses — these seven steeds are the symbol of the seven days of the week, the seven colours of light, and the seven Vedic metres. His charioteer is Aruna — the deity of the dawn. Surya worship performed on this tithi destroys the accumulated sins of seven lifetimes."

King Yashovarma performed this vrat with complete devotion on behalf of his son. At the hour of sunrise he took water in a copper vessel, offered arghya, recited the Aditya Hridayam, and worshipped the solar chariot. By the evening of that day, vitality had begun to stir in the prince's body. Day by day he grew stronger, and before long he was completely restored to health.

The tradition of the Bhavishya Purana declares that devotees who worship Suryadev on Magha shukla saptami are freed from disease, poverty, and accumulated karma — for the Sun is the very life-breath of this creation, and by his grace a new dawn of light rises in one's life.

## Significance

Ratha Saptami is also known as **Surya Jayanti** — it is the day of Suryadev's birth. On Magha shukla saptami, Suryadev turns his chariot northward at full speed. It is on this account that his energy is most active in creation, and the life-force in nature is renewed afresh.

The number seven in **"Saptami"** is layered with meaning — the seven horses of the solar chariot, the seven days of the week, the seven colours of light, and the seven Vedic metres (Gayatri, Brihati, Ushnik, Jagati, Trishtubh, Anushtubh, Pankti). The charioteer of the chariot is **Aruna** — who represents the dawn. It is said that Surya worship on this day destroys the **accumulated sins of seven lifetimes**.

Suryadev is the lord of health, longevity, and radiance. Worship performed on this day removes bodily ailments, brings clarity of mind, and awakens in one's life the inspiration to act rightly.

## Vidhi

- **At dawn:** Rise in the brahma muhurta; place seven arka leaves (Calotropis/swallow-wort) on the head — this is the distinctive practice of Ratha Saptami — and perform abhyanga with sesame oil; bathe with the leaves in place and allow them to float away with the water.
- **Surya Arghya:** Take clean water in a copper vessel; add red flowers, akshata (unbroken rice), and kumkum; facing the rising Sun, pour a steady stream from both hands while reciting "oṃ sūryāya namaḥ."
- **Pujan:** Install an image of Suryadev or a depiction of the seven-horsed solar chariot; offer red flowers, jaggery, sesame, and naivedya; perform arati with a lamp and incense.
- **Fasting:** Observe upavasa or ekahara (one meal only); use sesame and jaggery in the meal — these are dear to Suryadev.
- **Attire:** Wearing red clothing is considered auspicious.
- **Donation:** Donate sesame, jaggery, and cloth to a brahmin.
- **Breaking the fast:** Parana after sunset.
- **Special:** Recitation of the Aditya Hridayam on this day is held to be especially fruitful.
