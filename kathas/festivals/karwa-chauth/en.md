## Katha

Veeravati was a beautiful, gentle young bride.

She had seven brothers — and all seven loved their sister more than their own lives. Veeravati loved them just as fiercely. That year she had come to her parents' home for the first time since her marriage — and by the turn of fate, the day of Kartik Krishna Chaturthi arrived.

The day of Karwa Chauth.

Veeravati rose before sunrise and received her sargi (the pre-dawn meal gifted by her mother-in-law). Then she took her vow: "Until I have seen the moon, I will take neither water nor food."

The day wore on.

Afternoon passed. Evening fell. And Veeravati's distress deepened. Hunger made her head heavy, her lips were parched, her limbs had gone weak.

All seven brothers were home. They could not bear to see their sister suffer like this.

They spoke among themselves — "Our sister is in such pain. We must do something."

---

One brother had an idea.

All seven climbed high into a great peepal tree. They took a large chhalni (metal sieve) and lit a lamp behind it — so that from a distance it would look as though the moon had risen.

Then a voice called from below — "Veeravati! Look — the moon has come out!"

Veeravati stepped outside. She looked — there was a glow on the horizon. She believed it.

In her heart she said, "Chandra Dev, I offer you my salutation." And she broke her fast — she drank water, she ate.

The first morsel had barely touched her lips when a message arrived.

Her husband had suddenly fallen gravely ill.

Within moments — he was gone.

---

For Veeravati, it was as though the sky had fallen.

She wept and wailed without end. But she was a woman of courage. To remain submerged in grief was not her nature.

She went to Mother Parvati — to her temple. With her head bowed, she prayed: "Mother, I made a mistake. I broke my fast with a distracted heart. Please bring my husband back."

Mother Parvati appeared. She heard Veeravati's anguish.

She said: "My daughter, the fast was broken — that is why its fruit was not received. But do not despair. Next Karwa Chauth, observe the fast with complete devotion and the full prescribed method. I am with you."

Veeravati waited out the entire year. She performed puja every day, and every day she prayed for her husband's soul.

---

When the next Kartik Krishna Chaturthi came, Veeravati took her vow once more — fully waterless, following the full vidhi, with complete devotion.

This time she decorated the karva (small earthen pot) with reverence. She sat with her companions and listened to the katha. She performed the evening puja. And when the real moon rose — the moon itself in the sky — she viewed it through the chhalni.

She offered arghya to Chandra Dev. Holding her husband in her heart, she prayed.

The grace of Mother Parvati and Lord Shiva descended.

Her husband came back to life.

The tradition of the Skanda Purana holds: the married woman who observes a complete waterless fast on Kartik Krishna Chaturthi, performs the puja with proper method, and breaks her fast only after viewing the moon — her husband receives long life, good health, and auspiciousness.

## Significance

Karwa Chauth is the most emblematic vrat of **pativrata dharma** (the dharma of a devoted wife).

"Karwa" — the small earthen pot filled with water that is offered to the moon. This vessel is a symbol of the home's prosperity: full, brimming with water, nourished by love.

**The symbolism of the chhalni:** The moon is first viewed through the sieve, and then through that same sieve the wife looks at her husband's face. The sieve filters out impurities; the intention is that we strain away all faults and all afflictions from our husband's life.

This vrat is practised primarily in **North India** — in Punjab, Haryana, Uttar Pradesh, Rajasthan, and Madhya Pradesh. Here it is not merely a religious ritual — it is a social celebration too. In the evening, all the married women of the neighbourhood gather together, arrange their decorated karvas, listen to the katha, and then wait for the moon to rise, side by side.

Veeravati's katha says something profound — a lapse in devotion means the fast bears no fruit. But with true devotion and sincere repentance, the Goddess's grace is received once more.

## Vidhi

1. **Sargi — before sunrise:** The mother-in-law gives her daughter-in-law the sargi — dry fruits, sweets, fruit, sindoor, bangles. This is the mother-in-law's blessing. Eat the sargi before sunrise and take the vow of the fast.

2. **The vow:** "On Kartik Krishna Chaturthi I observe a complete waterless fast — for my husband's long life, good health, and auspiciousness. I will break my fast only after viewing the moon."

3. **Solah shringar:** On Karwa Chauth there is a tradition of the sixteen adornments of married life — including sindoor, mehendi, bangles, and the mangalsutra.

4. **Evening puja:** In the evening, all the married women of the household gather. Arrange the thali with the karva, lamp, roli, mehendi, and sweets. Perform Ganesh puja and then listen to the Karwa Chauth katha.

5. **Karva puja:** Fill the earthen karva with water, draw a swastika on its mouth with roli. Circle the karva seven times and offer it to a neighbour, saying: "Leve karva, deve suhag" — "Take the karva, give the blessing of a husband."

6. **Moon viewing:** When the moon rises — first view the moon through the chhalni, then through that same sieve look at your husband's face.

7. **Arghya to the moon:** Offer water through the chhalni: "Chandra Dev, grant my husband long life."

8. **Paran (breaking the fast):** Receive the first water from your husband's hand, then take food. This is the paran (paran = breaking of a fast).

**2026 date:** Verify on DrikPanchang (Kartik Krishna Chaturthi — approximately October 29, 2026).

## Observance

**Waterless fast:** From sunrise to moonrise — neither water nor food. This is the fundamental rule of this vrat.

**Sargi:** It is auspicious to receive the sargi from the mother-in-law's hands before sunrise. Where the mother-in-law is not present, the elder sister-in-law or the most senior woman of the family may give the sargi.

**Waiting for the moon:** The fast is broken only after the actual moonrise — this is precisely what Veeravati's katha teaches us.

**Paran:** Receiving water and food from the husband's hand is the final and most important link in this vrat.
