## Katha

Vaishakha Shukla Purnima — this is the tithi on which, in the deepest bed of the ocean, all the weight of creation rested upon the back of a great tortoise. This is the katha of Kurma Jayanti — the story of an avatar who neither spoke nor revealed himself; who only endured, and held firm.

One day, the king of the gods, Indra, was riding upon his elephant Airavata. The great sage Durvasa came toward him and offered Indra a divine garland of Parijata flowers. Indra placed it on Airavata's head, but the elephant trampled it underfoot. Durvasa's wrath blazed forth. He pronounced a curse: "Your arrogance will bring about your ruin. All your power, your prosperity, and your glory across the three worlds shall perish."

The moment the curse took hold, the strength of the gods began to ebb. Indra's might departed. Bali, king of the asuras, recognised the moment and led his forces against the heavens. The gods were defeated in battle; the realm of svarga was lost. Exhausted and without direction, the gods went to Brahma, who brought them to the shore of the Kshirasagara to seek refuge with Lord Vishnu.

Vishnu heard the distress of the gods and gave them a way forward: "Churn the Kshirasagara. From it amrita shall arise. Bring the asuras into the churning as well — without their strength, the task cannot be accomplished. Use Mount Mandara as the churning rod and the serpent king Vasuki as the rope."

The gods persuaded the asuras to join, and the great work of Samudra Manthan began. Mount Mandara — that immense peak laden with gold and gems, its summit touching the sky — was brought to the Kshirasagara and set in place as the churning rod. But the moment the mountain was lowered into the water, it began to sink. The fathomless depths of the Kshirasagara pulled it down. There was no foundation.

In that moment Lord Vishnu made a decision that was beyond all reckoning.

He himself descended into the depths of the Kshirasagara, assuming the vast form of **Kurma** — the divine tortoise. Upon his back settled the immense weight of Mount Mandara. The Lord received it — silent, steady, immovable. For a thousand years the Samudra Manthan continued; gods and asuras pulled Vasuki back and forth like a rope. The mountain turned. The ocean surged. And beneath it all, in the bed of the Kshirasagara, Kurma remained exactly as he was — the way no wave ever reaches the stillness of deep water.

What first rose from the churning was **Halahala** — that terrible poison whose heat began to scorch all three worlds. Gods and asuras both recoiled in dread. Then Lord Shiva came forward. He took all of that Halahala into his own throat; his throat turned blue, and he came to be known as Neelakantha.

Then, one by one, the chaturdasha ratnas arose — Kamadhenu the wish-fulfilling cow, Ucchaishrava the divine white horse, Airavata the divine white elephant, the Parijata tree of celestial flowers, the apsaras, and finally Lord Dhanvantari himself, emerging with the vessel of amrita held in his hands.

And then — **Devi Lakshmi**. She was seated upon a lotus in full bloom, radiant with beauty and divinity. She chose Lord Vishnu as her consort — for the one who bears the weight of the universe, who without being asked and without being seen carries away the suffering of all, is the true refuge of Lakshmi.

In the tradition of the Bhagavata Purana it is told that the asuras wished to seize and carry off the amrita. Lord Vishnu then assumed the form of **Mohini** — an unearthly beauty that confounded the asuras' reason — and quietly gave the amrita to the gods to drink.

Thus it was, on that day of Vaishakha Purnima, that a new age of creation rested upon the back of a silent tortoise.

## Significance

Vaishakha Shukla Purnima is the meeting of two traditions — and at the heart of both is a single truth: **compassion and the power of endurance**.

**Kurma Jayanti:** Kurma is the second of Lord Vishnu's ten avatars. Other avatars appeared, spoke, waged battle — but the Kurma avatar performed his work in silence. The weight of a mountain, the cold depths of the ocean, a thousand years of waiting — all of this he bore without complaint. This avatar teaches us: the foundation of creation lies not in tumult but in deep patience. One who remains still in the depths is the one who can sustain the waves above.

The Kshirasagara is our own mind — surging, churned, restless. And that foundation of Kurma is the atman within us — which bears all things, which is never disturbed. The churning is fulfilled only when this stillness abides within.

**Buddha Purnima:** In the Buddhist tradition this tithi is witness to three great events — the birth of Gautama Buddha, his bodhi (the attainment of enlightenment), and his Mahaparinirvana — all three on Vaishakha Purnima. In the Sanatana tradition, Lord Buddha is counted as the ninth avatar of Vishnu. His descent came in an age when the killing of animals was widespread — he came bearing the message of ahimsa and compassion. Thus Kurma's silent endurance and Buddha's non-violence meet upon the same purnima.

The light of Vaishakha Purnima says this to us: the true form of strength lies not in force but in stillness — and the true form of compassion lies in bearing the burdens of others.

## Vidhi

- **At dawn:** Rise in the brahma muhurta and bathe with water mixed with Gangajal; face east, meditate upon Lord Vishnu, and take the sankalpa to worship Lord Kurma and observe ahimsa.
- **Puja:** Establish an image of Lord Vishnu or Kurma; wear yellow garments; perform panchamrita-abhisheka (milk, curd, ghee, honey, sugar); offer tulasi leaves, yellow flowers, sandalwood paste, and akshat; light incense and a lamp; chant "Om Kurmaaya Namah" 108 times.
- **Kshira offering:** Place milk in a vessel, add tulasi leaves, and offer it to the Lord as a symbol of the Kshirasagara.
- **White flowers:** White lotus and white flowers are especially revered on Buddha Purnima — symbols of peace and compassion.
- **Fasting:** Those who observe a fast may take fruit; break the fast with puja in the evening.
- **Vow of ahimsa:** Abstain completely from meat, violence, and anger; cause no suffering to any living being; those who take the vow should endeavour to honour it for a full year.
- **Dana:** Jala-dana, anna-dana, and vastra-dana all carry especial merit on this day; the gift given on Purnima yields inexhaustible fruit.
- **Evening recitation:** Read the Kurma avatar episode and Samudra Manthan katha from the Eighth Skandha of the Bhagavata Purana; recitation of the Vishnu Sahasranama is also greatly fruitful.
- **Dipa-dana:** On the night of Purnima, bathe in a sacred river, lake, or tirtha; float a lamp near water as a remembrance of the infinite light of the Kshirasagara.

## Mantras

**The Vishnu Dvadasakshara Mantra:**

> ॐ नमो भगवते वासुदेवाय

Om namo bhagavate vasudevaya

Meaning: Salutations to you, O Lord Vasudeva. This mantra calls to mind all the lilas of Lord Vishnu — Kurma, Buddha, and every avatar.

---

**The Kurma Avatar Mantra:**

> ॐ कूर्माय नमः

Om kurmaaya namah

Meaning: Salutations to you, O Lord in the form of Kurma.

> कच्छपाय नमस्तुभ्यं सुरासुरनमस्कृत।
> मन्दरस्य धरित्रे च नमो मन्दरधारिणे॥

Kachchhapaaya namastubhyam suraasura-namaskrite।
Mandarasya dharitre cha namo mandaradhaarine॥

Meaning: Salutations to you, O Kurma in the form of the tortoise, honoured by gods and asuras alike, bearer of Mount Mandara — to the one who holds Mandara, I bow.

---

**Samudra Manthan — Verse of Lakshmi's Arrival:**

> क्षीरोदार्णवसंभूते सुरासुरनमस्कृते।
> रूपेण लोकमोहिन्यै नमो लक्ष्म्यै नमो नमः॥

Kshirodaarnava-sambhoote suraasura-namaskrite।
Ruupena lokamohinyai namo lakshmyai namo namah॥

Meaning: Salutations again and again to Mother Lakshmi, born of the Kshirasagara, honoured by both gods and asuras, she who enchants the world by her beauty.

(Recite this verse on the night of Buddha Purnima and Kurma Jayanti as a remembrance of the Samudra Manthan katha.)
