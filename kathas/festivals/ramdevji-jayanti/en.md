## Katha

In the Thar desert of Rajasthan there is a name that comes equally to the lips of both Hindus and Muslims: **Baba Ramdev.** Hindus revere him as an avatar of Vishnu; Muslims call him 'Ramsa Pir'. This unity is not the fruit of politics — it is the natural consequence of one saint's life.

**Birth — Undukasmer, Pokaran:**

In Vikrama Samvat 1409 (approximately 1405 CE), on Bhadrapada Shukla Dvitiya, a son was born to **Ajmalji Tanvar**, the ruler of Pokaran (near Jaisalmer). His mother was **Minaldevi**. The boy was named **Ramdev**.

In the tradition of the Ramdev Purana, it is said that when this infant arrived in Minaldevi's arms, an inexplicable light filled the house, lamps burned without ghee, and the child's hands and feet carried an extraordinary energy from birth itself. The village elders said — "This child is no ordinary being."

---

**The Child's Compassion — the Meghwal Community and Dalibai:**

The most important thing about Ramdevji's childhood was that he did not know the distinctions of caste — or if he knew them, he did not accept them. Living in his village was the **Meghwal community** — people who in that era were called 'untouchable', who worked with leather, and who were kept outside the mainstream of society.

Ramdevji played with their children. He visited their homes. He listened to their sorrows.

Among them was a girl — **Dalibai** — an ordinary daughter of the Meghwal community. But Dalibai's devotion was extraordinary. She regarded Ramdevji as her cherished deity. Ramdevji, in turn, recognised her as his **dharma-sister**. This bond was revolutionary in that era — a high-born Rajput saint and a Meghwal girl, joined by the sacred tie of brother and sister.

---

**Miracles — The Manifestation of Divine Power:**

In the tradition of the Ramdev Purana, many of his miracles are described.

Once, in a poor family's home, the milk vessel was empty — no worship or service could be performed. Ramdevji looked at that empty vessel, closed his eyes — and the vessel filled with milk.

Once, a demon named Bhairav spread terror across the region. The villagers were terrified. Ramdevji confronted that force, overpowered it, and freed the village from fear.

His most celebrated lila is — **giving life back to his horse Ramla.** His beloved dark-coloured horse Ramla had died. Ramdevji whispered something in his ear — and Ramla rose again.

The blind received sight, the paralysed walked, the sick were healed — his fame spread far and wide.

---

**The Reverence of Both Communities:**

It is said that once five Muslim pirs came from Mecca to Rajasthan — they wished to test this 'Hindu deity'. They were tired and hungry. Ramdevji welcomed them and said — "Please rest first."

The pirs waited for food. Ramdevji went into meditation. In a moment, before each of the five was the very food — the very pure food that was proper according to their tradition.

All five pirs bowed their heads. They said — "You are truly 'Ramsa Pir'." And they returned to their land. From that day, the 'five pirs' became part of Ramdevji's identity.

---

**Samadhi — The Living Samadhi:**

Ramdevji departed from this world at about thirty-three years of age — but he did not go through death. He took a **jivit samadhi** — of his own will, in full consciousness, knowing his mission was complete.

Before the samadhi, he blessed all those present. When Dalibai heard that Ramdevji was about to take samadhi, she resolved at that very moment to give up her own life too. It is said that Dalibai entered samadhi just moments before Ramdevji — like an elder sister, going ahead to prepare the way for her younger brother.

Ramdevra — that village in what is today Jaisalmer district — became the site of his samadhi. Today vast fairs are held there. From Bhadrapada Shukla Dvitiya to Dashami, the **Ramdevra Mela** is one of the largest fairs in Rajasthan — where Hindus and Muslims come together, bow together.

In the tradition of the Ramdev Purana, it is said that whoever comes to Ramdevji's threshold with devotion — of whatever caste, class, or creed — never returns empty-handed.

## Significance

**An Avatar of Vishnu — A Messenger of Social Justice:**

In the Rajasthani tradition, Ramdevji is regarded as **a new form of the Dvapara-era Krishna** — one who descended in an age when caste division and untouchability had torn society apart. Just as Krishna dissolved distinctions by living among the cowherds of Gokul, so did Ramdevji, though born a Rajput, forge a brother-sister bond with the Meghwal community.

His message in a single sentence was — **"God belongs to all, and all belong to God."**

**A Living Symbol of Hindu-Muslim Unity:**

Ramdevji is perhaps among the rarest saints in India's history whom both communities have claimed simultaneously and spontaneously as their own. Without any political effort — this unity was the natural outcome of his life. At his samadhi even today, Hindu devotees hoist the panchrang dhvaja and Muslim devotees offer a chaadar.

**The Ramdevra Mela — Cultural Heritage of Rajasthan:**

The fair held at Ramdevra from Bhadrapada Shukla Dvitiya to Dashami is one of Rajasthan's greatest pilgrimage gatherings. Millions of devotees come from across the country and abroad. The **Terahtal dance** — performed by Meghwal women, in which manjiras are tied at thirteen points on the body — is the unique cultural signature of this fair.

## Vidhi

- **At dawn:** Bathe and wear clean clothes; install an image or picture of Ramdevji at the place of worship.
- **Panchrang Dhvaja:** Hoist a five-coloured flag (orange, green, red, blue, yellow) on the home, shop, or field — a symbol of Ramdevji's presence and protection representing the five elements.
- **Offerings:** Light a ghee lamp; offer fresh flowers, gulal, sandalwood paste, and incense; present sweet churma as naivedya — the worship is simple, devotion and surrender are the essence.
- **Bhajan-Kirtan:** Sing Rajasthani folk bhajans (e.g. 'Balo re balo, Babo Ramdev balo') — a jagaran and bhajan-sandhya are considered essential; songs traditionally sung by Meghwal and Thori community singers.
- **Katha reading:** Read or listen to the Ramdev Purana (Ramdevji's life-story).
- **Seva:** Give food, clothing, or money to the marginalised, the poor, and those in need — this is the most direct form of Ramdevji's seva and the essence of his teaching.
- **Evening aarti:** Perform the aarti and distribute prasad.
- **Pilgrimage (optional):** Those who can travel to Ramdevra should make the pilgrimage — devotees often go on foot, covering hundreds of kilometres; the journey itself is a tapasya.

## Mantras

**Invocation-Vandana of Ramdevji:**

> जय रामदेवजी बाबा री जय,
> रुनेचा धणी री जय जय जय।

jaya rāmdevajī bābā rī jaya,
runecā dhaṇī rī jaya jaya jaya |

Meaning: "Victory to Baba Ramdevji — again and again victory to the lord of Runcecha (Ramdevra)." This is the simplest and most widely used victory-cry for Ramdevji in the folk tradition of Rajasthan.

---

**Vishnu Avatar Shloka:**

> नमामि तँवरवंशजं रामदेवं जनार्दनम्।
> दलितोद्धारकं वीरं सर्वधर्मप्रकाशकम्॥

namāmi tāṃvaravamśajaṃ rāmadevaṃ janārdanam |
dalitoddhārakaṃ vīraṃ sarvadharmaprakāśakam ||

Meaning: "I bow to Ramdev, born in the Tanvar lineage — the form of Janardana Vishnu — who is the liberator of the downtrodden, the valiant one, and the illuminator of all dharmas."
