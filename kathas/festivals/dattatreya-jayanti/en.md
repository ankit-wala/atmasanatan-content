## Katha

This is a story from the Treta Yuga. Near Chitrakoot, Maharshi Atri lived in his ashram with his wife Anasuya, deep in tapas. Anasuya's pativrata virtue was renowned across all three worlds. Such was the power of her chastity that even the sun rose and set in its proper order above their ashram.

This fame reached the heavenly realms. Curiosity arose in the hearts of the three great goddesses — Mother Saraswati, Mother Lakshmi, and Mother Parvati — and each spoke to her consort: "Is the pativrata devotion of this mortal woman truly as it is said? Put it to the test."

Brahma, Lord Vishnu, and Lord Shiva conferred among themselves and, disguising themselves as wandering mendicants, arrived at Atri's ashram. At that time, Maharshi Atri had gone out on his alms round. Anasuya welcomed the three guests with devotion and made ready to serve them a meal.

Then the three placed a severe condition before her — "We will accept food only if you serve us unclothed."

Hearing this, Anasuya fell momentarily silent. She closed her eyes and meditated upon her husband, Maharshi Atri. Her pativrata power stirred and awakened within her. Inwardly she made a solemn resolve — "If my pativrata is true, let these three become infants."

The moment her resolve was complete, a wondrous thing came to pass. In an instant, the three mendicants were transformed into three small infants. Anasuya lifted them with a mother's tender love, nursed them with milk, and served them food on leaf plates — exactly as a mother feeds her little ones.

When Maharshi Atri returned, he perceived the truth at once with his divine sight. He smiled and said — "Devi, you have turned the three gods into infants." He told Anasuya to now restore them to their original forms.

Anasuya released her resolve. Brahma, Lord Vishnu, and Lord Shiva appeared in their resplendent divine forms. All three gods bowed their heads. They said — "O Mother, we came to test you, yet you have overwhelmed us with a mother's love. Ask for a boon."

Anasuya folded her hands and said — "If you are pleased with me, then grant me a son in whom all three of you are present."

The three gods were delighted. They said "Tathāstu — so be it" and disappeared. On the Purnima of the month of Margashirsha, a radiant child was born in Mother Anasuya's arms, bearing three faces — the face of Brahma, the face of Vishnu, and the face of Shiva. This was Lord Dattatreya.

"Datta" — meaning that which was given by the three gods. "Atreya" — meaning the son of Atri. Dattatreya — the Adi Guru of all three worlds.

In the tradition of the Devi Bhagavata Purana, it is said that devotees who remember Lord Dattatreya on Margashirsha Purnima and listen to or read this katha of Anasuya's pativrata with reverence are granted knowledge, dispassion, and the attainment of liberation.

## Significance

Lord Dattatreya is a singular avatar in the Hindu tradition — the only deity in whom all three of the Trimurti — Brahma, Vishnu, and Mahesh — are united. He is the Adi Guru of creation, the foundational inspiration of the Nath tradition and Avadhuta yoga.

His most remarkable teaching is that he recognized twenty-four elements of nature as his own gurus. From the earth he learned patience; from water, purity; from fire, radiance; from the sun, steadfastness in duty; from the python, contentment; from the bee, the art of gathering; from the deer, dispassion — in this way he drew something from every being and every thing in the world. This is the Avadhuta vision — one who is truly awake sees a guru everywhere.

He is the beloved of renunciants, wanderers, and sadhus. He is commonly depicted with four dogs and a cow — the four dogs symbolizing the four Vedas, and the cow symbolizing Mother Earth.

Worshipping Lord Dattatreya on Dattatreya Jayanti destroys ignorance, bestows knowledge and dispassion, and opens the path to moksha.

## Vidhi

- **Materials:** A three-faced image of Dattatreya (or portrait), flowers, incense, lamp, naivedya, panchamrita.
- **At dawn:** Bathe early in the morning and wear clean clothing; bathe the image of Dattatreya with panchamrita, then offer flowers, incense, and a lamp.
- **Japa:** Chant **ॐ दत्तात्रेयाय नमः** — a minimum of 108 times.
- **Scripture recitation:** Recite the Dattatreya Stotra or a chapter of the Avadhuta Gita; reading the Avadhuta Gita, Gurucharitra, or Datta Mahatmya on this day is considered greatly meritorious.
- **Feeding sadhus:** Feed wandering sadhus and sannyasis — this is the special tradition of this Jayanti; also offer food, clothing, or dakshina to the poor.
- **Fasting:** Strict waterless fasting is not prescribed; devotees who wish to fast may observe a single meal (ekabhukta) during the day.
- **Evening:** Light a lamp in the home and chant "Digambara Digambara Shripad Vallabha Digambara."
- **Night vigil (jagaran):** There is a tradition of keeping a night-long vigil on the night of Margashirsha Purnima.

## Mantras

**Root name-mantra:**

> ॐ दत्तात्रेयाय नमः

oṃ dattātreyāya namaḥ

Meaning: O Dattatreya, I bow to you.

---

**Digambara maha-mantra:**

> दिगम्बर दिगम्बर श्रीपाद वल्लभ दिगम्बर।

digambara digambara śrīpāda vallabha digambara

Meaning: To Shripad Vallabha Dattatreya — the Digambara, clothed only in the directions of space, formless, of the nature of Brahman — I offer my salutation.
