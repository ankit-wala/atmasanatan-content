## Katha

The day of Bhadrapada Krishna Paksha Amavasya. The fields have been sown, the kharif crop stands lush and green. The earth tilled since Ashadha is now ready to yield its harvest. On this day, farmers of Maharashtra, Vidarbha, and Chhattisgarh set aside all other work to do one thing — worship their bullocks.

**In the folk tradition of Pola, it is said that** there once lived a poor farmer in a village. He had two old bullocks — they panted as they walked, they tired as they pulled the plough. In the same village lived a wealthy farmer with strong, young bullocks. On the day of Pola, when the poor farmer began to adorn his aged bullocks — rubbing sindoor on their horns, garlanding their necks with flowers, placing the finest fodder, jaggery, and chickpeas before them — the wealthy farmer came there and said with a laugh, "What is the use of worshipping these old, decrepit bullocks? They can neither pull the plough properly nor till the fields. How will such bullocks fill your belly?"

The poor farmer joined his hands and said, "Brother, they are old, that is true. But they have never asked for rest in all their years. Through sun and rain, they have tilled the fields by my side. I can never repay my debt to them. Today is their day." With complete devotion he worshipped his bullocks, washed their feet, bowed to them, and waved the aarati lamp.

That night the farmer saw Nandi himself in a dream. He came in white form, adorned with golden horns, his face full of joy, and said, "My child, you have recognised my true form. In the bullocks who till your fields, I dwell. Your guileless love has pleased me." The next morning when the farmer rose, he saw — both his aged bullocks stood as if newborn. There was a gleam in their eyes, strength in their bodies. That very year his fields yielded the finest harvest. And the wealthy farmer who had mocked him — his bullocks fell weak with illness that year.

This folk tale of Pola is told to this day from village to village across Maharashtra. Its message is simple — the animal who labours for you, who is the cause of your grain, carries a spark of the divine.

**In the tradition of the Bhagavata Purana, it is said that** on this very Bhadrapada Amavasya, a demon had come hiding among the cows of Gokul. This asura named Vatsasura had taken the form of a calf and mingled with the animals playing alongside infant Krishna, intending to harm them. But Krishna recognised him and slew Vatsasura without injuring a single cow or calf. This is why Pola also commemorates Krishna, the protector of the cow-clan. He who is the sustainer of the cowherd community is also the protector of all animals.

Both traditions point to the same truth — animals are not merely animals; they are fragments of the divine.

## Significance

The central feeling of Pola is **gratitude**. A farmer's entire year rests on the labour of the bullock. When the first rains fall in Ashadha, it is the bullock who carries the plough on his shoulder and tills the fields ridge by ridge — in blazing heat, through heavy soil, without complaint. Pola is the day of acknowledging that debt.

In the Shaiva tradition, Nandi is not merely a vehicle — he is the gatekeeper, the first disciple, and the symbol of dharma. It is said that Nandi dwells in every being who serves without self-interest. The bullock of the fields is the living embodiment of that very spirit.

In the Vaishnava tradition, the episode of Vatsasura's slaying establishes that the cow-clan is under divine protection. Cows and bullocks are not merely economic assets — they are the foundation of civilisation.

From a social perspective, Pola is the **midpoint of the agricultural calendar**. Sowing is done; harvest is yet to come. To give the bullock rest and celebrate in this interval — this is both the farmer's wisdom and his heartfelt sentiment.

## Vidhi

- **Bullock rest:** No work is taken from the bullocks on this day — neither ploughing nor pulling loads; the folk belief holds that the farmer who observes this rule will have bullocks that remain healthy throughout the year.
- **Morning bath:** Bathe the bullocks in a river, pond, or courtyard with water mixed with turmeric and neem leaves; women smear the courtyard with cow-dung and draw rangoli before worship.
- **Adornment:** Apply sindoor to the horns and a tilak of turmeric and kumkum on the forehead; place colourful garlands of flowers, cowries, and bells around the neck; drape a new jhool (coloured cloth) over the back; tie dhajiyāñ (pennants) on the horns; in some regions, fit brass horn-caps as well.
- **Naivedya:** Offer jaggery, soaked black chickpeas, bread, sorghum, and pearl millet; women also prepare urad lentil vadas and puran poli as offerings.
- **Puja:** Elders of the household light a lamp and incense before the bullock, touch his feet, and bow; the plough, yoke, and other farming tools are also worshipped; auspicious time for worship is the morning.
- **Procession:** All farmers gather for a collective procession through the village (generally midday to evening), accompanied by dhol-tasha, songs, and nagadas; women wave the aarati lamp and apply a black tikka along the route.
- **Clay Pola:** Children purchase and worship clay bullocks — connecting the next generation to the festival's spirit.
- **Shared meal:** Puran poli, karanji, and jaggery-rice are prepared; field labourers and neighbours are invited to share the meal.
- **Fasting:** Pola is principally a festival of worship, not strict fasting; some devotees observing Shiva-Nandi bhakti keep ekāhāra (eating only once).

## Mantras

**1. Nandi Stuti**

> नन्दिकेश्वर महाभाग शिवभक्त महाबल।
> वृषभध्वज नमस्तुभ्यं पाहि माम् वृषभाधिप॥

nandikeśvara mahābhāga śivabhakta mahābala,
vṛṣabhadhvaja namastubhyaṃ pāhi māṃ vṛṣabhadhipa.

Meaning: O great and illustrious Nandikesvara, mighty devotee of Shiva, bearer of the bull-banner — I bow to you. O lord of bulls, protect me.

---

**2. Govamsha Raksha Shloka (Gopala Stuti)**

> गावो विश्वस्य मातरः पवित्राः सर्वकामदाः।
> गोपालः सर्वभूतानां रक्षकः पापनाशनः॥

Gāvo vishvasya mātaraḥ pavitrāḥ sarvakāmadāḥ,
gopālaḥ sarvabhūtānām rakṣakaḥ pāpanāśanaḥ.

Meaning: Cows (and the cow-clan) are the mothers of the entire world, pure and the fulfillers of all desires. Gopala (Krishna) is the protector of all beings and the destroyer of sin.
