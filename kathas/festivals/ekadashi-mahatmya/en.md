## Katha

This story comes from the Uttara Khanda of the Padma Purana.

In the earliest age of creation there was a demon — Mura, known as Murasura. He had attained extraordinary power through austerity. He was unconquerable — neither the devas could defeat him, nor could humans. He unleashed terror across all three worlds. The devas fled and hid. Even the heavens fell under his dominion.

All the devas went to Vishnu. Vishnu fought Murasura — for a thousand years. But the demon would not tire; he would not die.

At last Vishnu grew weary. He thought — I must rest for a time. He went to a cave in the Badari forest and lay down.

Murasura followed. He saw that Vishnu was asleep. Here was his chance. He intended to kill Vishnu while he slept.

At that very moment, a divine radiance emerged from Vishnu's sleeping body. From the combined luminance of all his senses, limbs, and vital force, a goddess appeared — brilliantly radiant, armed, magnificent. She was the power of the eleventh day — Ekadashi.

The goddess saw Murasura. She fought him — and reduced him to ash in moments.

When Vishnu awoke, the ashes of Murasura lay before him and a goddess stood in his presence.

Vishnu said — *"You have accomplished the impossible. You were born from my own power — you are my beloved. Ask for any boon."*

The goddess said — *"Lord, I ask that whoever fasts on my day — Ekadashi — and worships you, may have all their sins destroyed. May they be liberated from the cycle of birth and death. May they attain your abode."*

Vishnu said — *"Tathāstu. Ekadashi is my most beloved tithi. Both Ekadashis — of the bright fortnight and the dark — belong to you. Whoever does not eat grain on this day and remembers me shall receive merit equal to all tirthas, all yajnas, and all acts of charity. Even one who refrains from grain on this day unknowingly shall receive great merit."*

This is why grain is forbidden on Ekadashi.

In the Padma Purana, Vishnu further said — *"On the day of Ekadashi, the remnant of that demon enters grain. Whoever eats grain on that day takes the demon within himself. Whoever does not eat — remains pure through the grace of the goddess Ekadashi."*

This is the foundation of all twenty-four Ekadashis of the year. Each Ekadashi has its own name, its own story, its own special fruit — yet all of them rest on this single origin: the goddess born from Vishnu's power, who slew the demon, and who asked for the boon that this day become a gateway to human liberation.

In the tradition of the Uttara Khanda of the Padma Purana, it is said that the merit of the Ekadashi vrat surpasses all pilgrimages, all yajna observances, and all acts of charity and giving.

## Significance

There are 24 Ekadashis in a year — one each in the dark fortnight and the bright fortnight. In years with an adhika masa (leap month), there are 26.

Each Ekadashi has a special name — such as Nirjala Ekadashi (Jyeshtha Shukla), Devshayani Ekadashi (Ashadha Shukla), Devuthani Ekadashi (Kartika Shukla) — and each has its own story.

Yet one thing is common to all Ekadashis: the abstention from grain. On Ekadashi, grain (rice, wheat, lentils, all cereals) must not be eaten. Fruits, milk, water, and roots may be taken.

Behind this lies the reason Vishnu himself gave — on that day the remnant of the demon enters grain. This is also a symbolic teaching: on Ekadashi, keep the body light and the senses restrained — so that the mind may rest in Vishnu.

The paran on Dvadashi (the twelfth day) is equally important. The Ekadashi fast is complete only when the vrat is broken on Dvadashi by remembering Vishnu and taking prasad. The paran should take place after sunrise and before Dvadashi ends.

## Vidhi

- **For whom:** The Ekadashi vrat is for everyone — men, women, the elderly, the young. Children may be fed grain; they are not required to fast.
- **The day before (Dashami):** Give up salt in the evening meal; observe brahmacharya through the night.
- **At dawn on Ekadashi:** Bathe before sunrise; take the sankalpa — *"Today on Ekadashi I observe a fast without food — for devotion to Vishnu and the removal of sin."*
- **Puja:** Offer tulsi leaves, yellow flowers, incense, and a lamp before Vishnu's image; chant *Om Namo Bhagavate Vasudevaya* 108 times or more; read or listen to the Vishnu Sahasranama or the Bhagavad Gita; hear the special story of that Ekadashi.
- **Abstention from grain:** Rice, wheat, lentils, and all grains are forbidden. Fruits, milk, water, sabudana, singhara flour, and kuttu may be taken.
- **Fasting levels:** Nirjala (without even water) is the most difficult and most meritorious; sajal (with water) is the middle path; a single fruit meal is the minimum — but abstaining from grain is obligatory in all cases.
- **Night vigil:** Keep vigil through the night in Vishnu bhajans and remembrance.
- **Paran on Dvadashi:** After sunrise, before Dvadashi ends — first offer a tulsi leaf to Vishnu, recite the concluding verse of the Ekadashi vrat katha, and break the fast. A paran that falls after Dvadashi has ended breaks the vrat. It is auspicious to take tulsi water or a tulsi leaf first.
- **Note:** There are 24 Ekadashis in an ordinary year and 26 in a year with an adhika masa. The story of each is given separately in this collection.

## Mantras

**Vishnu Dvadasakshari mantra:**

> ॐ नमो भगवते वासुदेवाय

oṃ namo bhagavate vāsudevāya

Meaning: O Vasudeva (Vishnu), Lord — salutation to you. This twelve-syllable mantra is the foremost mantra for Ekadashi japa.

---

**Ekadashi vrat sankalpa shloka:**

> एकादश्यां निराहारः स्थित्वाहं सुरसत्तमः ।
> भोक्ष्यामि पुण्डरीकाक्ष शरणं मे भवाच्युत ॥

ekādaśyāṃ nirāhāraḥ sthitvāhaṃ surasattamaḥ | bhokṣyāmi puṇḍarīkākṣa śaraṇaṃ me bhavācyuta ||

Meaning: O Pundarikaksha (lotus-eyed Vishnu), O Achyuta — I remain without food on Ekadashi and take refuge in you; on Dvadashi I shall eat. Be my shelter. (This is the traditional sankalpa shloka recited at the start of the Ekadashi vrat.)
