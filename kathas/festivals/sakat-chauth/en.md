## Katha

In a certain village there lived a Brahmin woman — simple, straightforward, and deeply devout. Her only son was the very breath of her life. When he was home, the house felt alive; when he stepped out, her gaze never left the doorway.

One winter in the month of Pausha, that son fell gravely ill. The fever burned so fiercely he could not even rise from his bed. The physician came, prescribed herbs — nothing helped. The mother kept vigil day and night. Still her son's condition worsened.

Then came Pausha Krishna Chaturthi.

An elderly woman from the neighbourhood told the Brahmin woman: *"Today is Sakat Chauth — the vrat of Lord Ganesha. A mother who fasts this day with complete devotion, takes not even a drop of water until nightfall, and breaks her fast only after beholding the moon at chandrodaya and offering bhog to Ganesha — Ganesha himself lifts every sakat from her son's life."*

One thought alone filled the mother's heart — her son. She took her vow.

As the day began, she bathed, meditated on Ganesha, and started her fast. The whole day — no food, no water. Her son groaned in the next room; she sat beside him, quietly repeating Ganesha's name within her heart. When someone urged her, *"Eat something — watching over the sick, you will collapse yourself,"* she only smiled and shook her head.

Evening came. She prepared laddoos of til-gud, shaped modak, and placed them as offerings. Before a clay image of Ganesha she lit a lamp and laid flowers.

Night fell. The moon rose in the sky.

The moment the Brahmin woman beheld the moon and offered bhog to Ganesha, a voice came from the inner room.

Her son called out — *"Mother, I am hungry."*

She ran to him. Her son was sitting up. His eyes were clear; the fever had broken.

The mother's eyes filled with tears. Words would not come. She simply bowed before Ganesha.

From that night onward, she observed this fast every Pausha Krishna Chaturthi — without fail, for the rest of her life. And her son lived healthy, happy, and full of years.

The tradition also holds a second story. A daughter-in-law was observing this very fast when her mother-in-law, having cooked special dishes, urged her to eat: *"What difference does one day's fast make? Come, eat."* The young woman wavered. She had barely parted her lips when sleep overtook her.

In her dream, Lord Ganesha appeared. His gaze held both tenderness and warning. He said: *"Complete your vrat. Your son has gone on a long journey and is in danger — your fast today is his armour of protection."*

She awoke. She kept her fast to the end.

The next day her son returned home — and said that on the road he had faced great peril, but a stranger had saved him. Who that stranger was, no one ever knew.

The tradition of the Skanda Purana declares that the vrat of Sakat Chauth is a mother's resolve that makes Ganesha the guardian of her devotee's children — and there is no remover of obstacles greater than Vighneshwara.

## Significance

"Sakat" means calamity — obstacle, impediment, adversity. And Ganesha is Vighneshwara — the Lord of all obstacles.

Sakat Chauth falls on the Krishna Chaturthi of Pausha month and is regarded as the most important of all the Sankat Chaturthi fasts observed through the year. This vrat belongs almost entirely to mothers — kept for the protection of a son, for his long life, and for the removal of every sakat that may come his way.

The rite of breaking the fast at chandrodaya is prescribed because the Chaturthi tithi of the moon belongs to Ganesha — this is the night when a devotee seals the resolve of an entire day with the sight of the moon, and the door to Ganesha's grace opens wide.

The offering of til-gud suits the winter month of Pausha — both sesame and jaggery warm the body and are held to be sacred. Modak is Ganesha's most beloved food.

## Vidhi

- **At dawn:** Bathe before or at sunrise; take the sankalpa — dedicate the fast to Ganesha, holding your son's name in your heart.
- **Fasting:** Observe a complete nirjal (waterless) fast through the day — the ideal; those unable to remain without water may drink, but not eat.
- **Evening puja:** Establish a clay image of Ganesha (or worship the home murti); prepare til-gud laddoos and modak as bhog; light a lamp; offer flowers and durva grass; chant Oṃ gaṃ gaṇapataye namaḥ; perform aarti.
- **At night:** Sight the moon at chandrodaya — the fast is not broken before beholding the moon (this rule is binding); offer bhog and jal arpan to Ganesha; receive prasad and break the fast.
- **Offerings:** Til-gud laddoos, modak, and seasonal fruit; offer to Ganesha first, then receive as prasad.
- **For whom:** Observed primarily by mothers for the welfare of their sons; may also be kept for a daughter; in many regions married women keep it for both husband and son.

## Mantras

**Ganesha Mula Mantra:**

> ॐ गं गणपतये नमः

Oṃ gaṃ gaṇapataye namaḥ

Meaning: O Ganapati, I bow to you.

---

**Vighnashana Shloka:**

> वक्रतुण्ड महाकाय सूर्यकोटिसमप्रभ ।
> निर्विघ्नं कुरु मे देव सर्वकार्येषु सर्वदा ॥

Vakratuṇḍa mahākāya sūryakoṭisamaprabha |
nirvighnaṃ kuru me deva sarvakāryeṣu sarvadā ||

Meaning: O Vakratunda — great of form, radiant as a crore of suns — O Lord, remove all obstacles from my every endeavour, always.
