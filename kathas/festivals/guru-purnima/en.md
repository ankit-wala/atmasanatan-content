## Katha

On that day the Yamuna flowed quietly. The great sage Parashara was on pilgrimage and had come to the river's edge to cross. The boat was run by a young woman — a fisherman's daughter named Kali.

Parashara, a seer of the lineage of the Saptarishis, looked at this young woman and saw something extraordinary in her — a quiet, sleeping greatness. A feeling rose in his heart. Kali said humbly: "Sage, I am a fisherman's daughter. My body carries the smell of fish." Parashara smiled and blessed her: *"From today, a fragrance will flow from you that carries for miles."* From that day, Kali became the fragrant Satyavati.

In the middle of the Yamuna there was a small island. There, from their union, a child came into the world — but it was no ordinary birth. A light appeared and in the same moment there was already a radiant young man. His complexion was deep dark, like a rain cloud, and he had been born on an island — a dvipa. So he was called Krishna Dvaipayana.

He bowed to his mother and said: *"Mother, I have been born. My work now is to go into the forest and seek knowledge through tapasya. I am going. But whenever you need me — simply remember me. I will be present at once."*

And he left.

In the forests, in the mountains, in the unbroken river of austerity, Krishna Dvaipayana lived his life. He saw that the knowledge carried by the rishis was scattered — if it remained this way, future generations would lose it.

Then he did something that seems impossible for any one man: he divided the single Veda into four. Rigveda, Samaveda, Yajurveda, Atharvaveda — each organized into its own lineage. This is why he came to be called Veda Vyasa: the one who made the "vyasa" — the arrangement, the division — of the Vedas.

Still, that was not enough. He gave each of the four Vedas to a disciple to carry and teach: Paila received the Rigveda, Vaishampayana the Yajurveda, Jaimini the Samaveda, and Sumantu the Atharvaveda. A fifth disciple, Romharshana, received the tradition of the Puranas.

Then he composed the Mahabharata — that vast epic of a hundred thousand verses, holding within it war and dharma, love and betrayal, and most importantly the Bhagavad Gita: the answer given through Arjuna to all of humanity.

Then eighteen Puranas.

When he had completed all of this, he was still not at peace. There was something unfinished in him. Then Narada came to him and said: *"Vyasa ji, you have written everything — but you have not yet described the Lord's own lilas. Write what will move the listener's heart."* Only then did he compose the Srimad Bhagavatam — the text he narrated to his son Shukadeva, and which Shukadeva narrated to King Parikshit.

This Ashadha Purnima is Vyasa's birth anniversary — Vyasa Purnima. And because he was the adi-guru, the first teacher who gave knowledge its lineage and form, this day became Guru Purnima. On this day we honor our own teachers — and through them, we bow to him.

The shastra tradition says: whoever honors their guru sincerely on this day receives the merit of having studied all four Vedas.

## Significance

Guru Purnima is the only festival in the Hindu calendar that does not honor a deity — it honors a rishi's tapasya and what grew from it.

In the Indian tradition, the guru's place is held above even that of God — *"Gurur Brahma Gurur Vishnu Gurur Devo Maheshvarah."* This is not mere reverence taken too far; it is the acknowledgment of a truth: without someone showing you, nothing can be known. The one who brings light into your ignorance — that person is your guru.

Guru Purnima is for all teachers: the teacher of scripture, the teacher of life, the parent who was the first guide.

## Vidhi

The central observance of Guru Purnima is the Guru Puja. If your guru is living, seek their darshan and offer puja. If your guru is distant or has passed on, honor Vyasa ji and the lineage.

1. Bathe in the morning and wear clean clothes.
2. Set up an image of Vyasa ji — or of your own guru.
3. Offer flowers, tulsi, unbroken rice (akshat), and a ghee diya.
4. Recite the Vyasa Stotra or the Guru Stotram.
5. Offer dakshina to your guru — cloth, fruit, or whatever is available.
6. If possible, take a sacred dip in a river on this day.

## Observance

**Fasting:** Many devotees observe a fast on this day, but it is not required. This is a festival, not a vrat — eat simply and sattvik if you choose to eat.

**A study commitment:** Taking on a learning commitment on Guru Purnima is a beautiful practice — one text, one chapter, to be read through all of Chaturmas. This is real reverence to the guru.

**Connection to Chaturmas:** Guru Purnima falls just four days before Devshayani Ekadashi and the beginning of Chaturmas. The commitment taken on Guru Purnima becomes the foundation of the four months of practice that follow.

## Mantras

**Vyasa Stuti — recited at the opening of puja:**

> नमोऽस्तु ते व्यास विशालबुद्धे
> फुल्लारविन्दायतपत्रनेत्र ।
> येन त्वया भारततैलपूर्णः
> प्रज्वालितो ज्ञानमयः प्रदीपः ॥

*Namo'stu te Vyāsa viśāla-buddhe
Phullāravindāyata-patra-netra |
Yena tvayā Bhārata-taila-pūrṇaḥ
Prajvālito jñāna-mayaḥ pradīpaḥ ||*

Meaning: "Salutations to you, Vyasa, of vast intellect and eyes wide as open lotuses — you who lit the lamp of knowledge, filled with the oil of the Mahabharata."

**Guru Vandana — the central shloka of Guru Purnima:**

> गुरुर्ब्रह्मा गुरुर्विष्णुः गुरुर्देवो महेश्वरः ।
> गुरुः साक्षात् परं ब्रह्म तस्मै श्रीगुरवे नमः ॥

*Gurur Brahmā Gurur Viṣṇuḥ Gurur Devo Maheśvaraḥ |
Guruḥ Sākṣāt Paraṃ Brahma Tasmai Śrī-Gurave Namaḥ ||*

Meaning: "The Guru is Brahma, the Guru is Vishnu, the Guru is the great Lord Shiva. The Guru is directly the Supreme Brahman itself — to that blessed Guru I bow."
