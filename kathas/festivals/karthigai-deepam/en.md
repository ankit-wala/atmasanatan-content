## Katha

At the very beginning of creation, a dispute arose one day between Lord Brahma and Lord Vishnu — who is supreme? Both were the sustainers and nourishers of creation. Brahma said — "I am the creator of creation, therefore I alone am the supreme one." Vishnu said — "I am the protector of all this creation, therefore supremacy belongs to me." This debate grew from words into a battle of pride.

At that very moment — from no direction, for no apparent reason — something extraordinary happened between the two of them. A tremendous pillar of light appeared. This pillar was neither a tongue of fire nor the light of the sun — it was something that had never been seen before, never been imagined. Upward it disappeared into infinite heights; downward it descended into the depths of the netherworld. Neither its beginning was visible, nor its end.

From that pillar came a divine voice — "Whoever finds the upper end of this pillar, or finds its lower end — that one is supreme."

Lord Vishnu took the form of Varaha and, cleaving through the earth, descended into the depths of the netherworld. Even after countless ages he could not find the base of that pillar. He returned and humbly acknowledged — "I could not find the beginning of that pillar."

Lord Brahma took the form of a swan and flew upward. He flew for thousands of years — yet the summit of the pillar was not found. Then, as he was descending, he saw a ketaki flower drifting down from above. Brahma asked that flower — "From where do you come?" The flower said — "Ages ago I was offered at the very top of that pillar; I have been falling ever since."

Hearing this, greed arose in Brahma. He made that ketaki flower his witness and came before Vishnu and said — "I have seen the summit of that pillar. This ketaki is my witness."

At that very moment, the pillar of light burst open and from within it, Lord Shiva himself appeared — Mahadeva in person, his flame piercing the sky. In his eyes was the recognition of Brahma's falsehood.

Lord Shiva looked upon Brahma and said — "You have spoken an untruth. You made an innocent flower a false witness. As the fruit of this sin — neither will you be worshipped in the world, nor will this ketaki ever be accepted in my worship." This is why, even today, temples to Brahma are rare throughout all of India, and ketaki flowers are forbidden in Shiva worship.

Lord Shiva was pleased with Vishnu's truthfulness and humility. He praised Vishnu greatly and said — "You are always dear to me."

That infinite pillar of light — which was beyond the limits of both Brahma and Vishnu — is the original Jyotirlinga. Shiva himself is light — a light that has neither beginning nor end. The Arunachala hill at Tiruvannamalai is considered the manifestation of this very pillar of light upon earth. On the day of Karthigai Deepam, a great lamp is lit on the summit of that hill — visible for miles around — and through this act devotees remember Shiva's infinite light.

This festival has another dimension as well — it falls on the full moon of Margashirsha month in conjunction with the Krittika nakshatra. Krittika — whom we know as the six mothers near the Saptarishi — are the nurturing mothers of Lord Murugan (Kartikeya). He was born and raised under this nakshatra. Therefore this festival is a celebration of both Shiva's infinite light and the birth of his son Murugan.

In the tradition of the Skanda Purana, it is said that a devotee who lights a lamp with a pure mind on the day of Karthigai Deepam and meditates upon Shiva receives a ray of that infinite light — and the sins of many lifetimes are burnt to ash in that radiance.

## Significance

Karthigai Deepam is not merely a festival of lights — it is the deepest expression of Shiva's true nature. Shiva cannot be contained in any idol; his most true form is the formless light — beyond beginning and beyond end.

**The Mystery of the Jyotirlinga:** All twelve Jyotirlingas across India are symbols of this same infinite pillar. Wherever Shiva manifested in the form of light, a Jyotirlinga was established there. The Arunachalesvara temple at Tiruvannamalai represents the element of fire among them — the Agni Linga.

**Arunachala — Shiva's Light on Earth:** In Tamil tradition, the Arunachala hill is considered Shiva's very body. Great saints like Ramana Maharshi made this hill the center of their sadhana. The great lamp that burns on the summit of this hill on this day is the most extraordinary spectacle of Karthigai.

**The Spiritual Meaning of the Lamp:** The lighting of a lamp is not merely a ritual — it is a remembrance that within us too burns the same light that blazes upon Arunachala. The resolve to dispel the darkness of ignorance with the light of knowledge is the core spirit of this festival.

**Murugan and Krittika:** The connection of the Karthigai month with Lord Murugan is equally deep. Shanmukha (six-faced) Murugan was nurtured by the six mothers of Krittika. In this month, remembering Murugan is especially meritorious.

## Vidhi

**Evening Lamp-Lighting (main ritual):**
- After sunset, light sesame oil lamps at the main entrance of the home, near the tulsi plant, and in the puja room.
- Light as many lamps as possible — at all directions and doorways of the home.
- While lighting the lamps, recite "Oṃ namaḥ śivāya."

**Shiva Puja:**
- Offer water, milk, bilvapatra, datura, and white flowers on the Shivalinga.
- **Take note:** never offer ketaki (kevada) flowers in Shiva worship — this is a prohibition established by the Puranas.
- Light a ghee lamp before Shiva and meditate.

**Murugan/Kartikeya Puja:**
- Light a lamp before the image or idol of Lord Murugan (Kartikeya/Skanda).
- Offer red flowers, fruits, and peacock feathers.
- Recite the shadakshara mantra "Oṃ Śaravaṇabhava."

**Arati and Circumambulation:**
- Perform arati for both Shiva and Murugan.
- If there is a Shiva temple nearby, visit with the family for darshan.
- At Tiruvannamalai, the circumambulation of Arunachala (giri-pradakshina) holds special significance — those who are able to go should certainly do so.

## Observance

**Lamp-Lighting:**
Lighting a lamp in the home at the time of sunset is the central act of this festival. A sesame oil lamp is the best; a ghee lamp is also excellent. Do not let the lamp go out — keeping it burning through the night is considered auspicious.

**Shiva Worship:**
Perform Shiva puja both in the morning and at dusk. Fasting is optional — observing phalahar (fruit diet) or eating once is meritorious. On this day, speak the truth, avoid anger and falsehood — remembering Brahma's untruth, resolve to strengthen the truth in your own life.

**Murugan/Kartikeya Worship:**
On the day of the Krittika nakshatra, perform a special abhisheka and puja for Murugan. In South India, special festivals take place at Murugan temples on this day.

**Vrat:**
There is no prescription for strict fasting on this festival, but maintaining restraint and a spirit of devotion is essential. Some devotees abstain from tamasic food (meat, alcohol).

## Mantras

> ॐ नमः शिवाय

oṃ namaḥ śivāya

Meaning: O Lord Shiva, I bow to you. This panchakshara (five-syllable) mantra evokes Shiva's five elements — earth, water, fire, air, and space.
