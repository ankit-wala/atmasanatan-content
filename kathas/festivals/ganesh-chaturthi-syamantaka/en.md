## Katha

### Part One: The Moon's Pride and Ganesha's Curse

One evening, Lord Ganesha was returning from a great feast. His belly was full — laden with modaks and laddoos. He was riding home on his beloved mount, the mouse Mushak.

Along the path, a snake crossed their way. Mushak was startled — and stopped short.

Ganesha's great belly shifted forward with the sudden halt. He pitched over and fell. The modaks scattered. His belly split open. Ganesha got up, gathered all the modaks back inside, and fastened the same snake around his waist like a belt.

The Moon was watching all of this from the sky.

The Moon was proud of his beauty — proud of his radiant glow, proud of his place in the night sky. And this scene before him — Ganesha with his elephant head and vast belly, tumbling off a mouse — struck him as ridiculous.

He laughed. He laughed loudly and openly.

Ganesha looked up. His eyes turned red.

He said: "Moon, you have mocked me. Hear my curse — **whoever sees you on the night of Bhadrapada Shukla Chaturthi will suffer Mithya Dosha — false accusation. They will be innocent, but the world will blame them for something they did not do.**"

Mithya Dosha — to be accused of theft, deception, or wrongdoing that you never committed. To have your name dragged through suspicion even though you are clean.

The Moon was frightened. He begged forgiveness.

Ganesha's anger eased slightly. He said: "The curse stands. But here is the remedy — whoever worships me on Bhadrapada Chaturthi and hears the story of the Syamantaka jewel, their dosha will be gone."

### Part Two: The Syamantaka Jewel — Krishna's False Accusation

This story is the proof that the curse is real — that it worked even on Shri Krishna himself.

In Dwarka, there was a king named **Satrajit** — a devoted worshipper of Surya, the Sun god. One day Surya was pleased with his devotion and gave him the **Syamantaka jewel** — a divine gem that produced eight measures of gold every single day, and kept disease, famine, and misfortune away from wherever it was kept.

Satrajit wore the jewel around his neck with great pride.

Once, Shri Krishna gently suggested that Satrajit offer the jewel to King Ugrasena — so its blessings could benefit the whole kingdom. Satrajit refused. The matter was dropped.

Some days later, Satrajit's brother **Prasena** took the jewel and went hunting in the forest. A lion attacked him and killed him. The lion took the jewel and carried it away.

Deeper in the forest, the great bear-chief **Jambavan** saw the lion — Jambavan, the mighty devotee of Shri Rama, who had fought in the Ramayana and still lived. He killed the lion and carried the jewel to his cave as a gift for his young son Sukumar to play with.

---

Back in Dwarka, Satrajit learned that Prasena had not returned — and the jewel was gone.

He told everyone in the city: "Shri Krishna has stolen the Syamantaka."

It was a lie. But it spread.

Shri Krishna heard the accusation. He set out to clear his name.

He went into the forest. He found Prasena's footprints. Then the lion's. Then Jambavan's. The trail led to a deep cave in the hillside.

He went inside.

---

Jambavan saw Shri Krishna enter and assumed he had come to take the jewel. The fight began.

For twenty-eight days they fought — in the darkness of that cave, alone. Outside, Krishna's companions waited and waited. Finally, exhausted and grieving, they returned to Dwarka believing he was dead.

But inside, the battle continued.

Slowly, Jambavan began to feel something was different. This was no ordinary man. The strength of this person was like the strength of the Supreme itself.

He looked more carefully — and recognized him.

He said: **"You are he for whom I crossed the ocean. You are Raghunath. You are Vishnu himself."**

Jambavan fell at Shri Krishna's feet. He brought out the Syamantaka jewel and offered it. And he gave his daughter **Jambavanti** in marriage to Shri Krishna.

---

Shri Krishna returned to Dwarka with the jewel.

He placed it before King Ugrasena's assembly — in front of everyone — and told the full story.

Satrajit bowed his head in shame. He asked for forgiveness, and in penance gave his daughter **Satyabhama** in marriage to Shri Krishna. Shri Krishna returned the jewel to Satrajit.

---

Then **Narada** arrived.

He said to Shri Krishna: "Madhava, do you know why this false accusation came upon you? That day was Bhadrapada Shukla Chaturthi. And you — unknowingly — had seen the moon that night. It was Ganesha's curse."

Shri Krishna asked: "What is the remedy?"

Narada said: "Observe the Ganesh Chaturthi vrat. Worship Ganesha. And hear the story of the Syamantaka jewel. This is what removes the dosha."

Shri Krishna observed the vrat. His name was fully cleared.

It is said in the tradition of the Ganesha Purana (Upasana Khanda) and the Bhagavata Purana (Skandha 10) — whoever worships Ganesha on Bhadrapada Shukla Chaturthi and hears this Syamantaka katha will be protected from Mithya Dosha. And if someone accidentally sees the moon that night, reciting the Syamantaka shloka three times will lift the curse.

## Significance

**On the night of Bhadrapada Shukla Chaturthi, do not look at the moon.**

This is Ganesha's curse — and it is observed across every part of India, in every household.

The curse means exactly this: whoever sees the moon on this night will face false accusation — being blamed for something they did not do. A stolen jewel. A broken trust. An unfair rumour. The person may be entirely innocent, but the world will suspect them.

This dosha is so real that even Shri Krishna — the Supreme Lord himself — could not escape it. The moment he saw the moon that night, the chain of events began: a missing jewel, a dead man, a city full of whispers pointing at him.

This is why across India, on this night, people stay indoors, close windows toward the sky, and make certain not to catch even an accidental glimpse of the moon. The safeguard is simple: worship Ganesha and hear this katha. Narada himself gave this remedy to Krishna.

## Vidhi

- **Note:** This katha is read as part of the Ganesh Chaturthi puja — for the full puja procedure, see the **Ganesh Chaturthi Janma Katha** entry.
- **Protecting against moon-sighting:** From the evening of Bhadrapada Shukla Chaturthi, avoid going outdoors where the open sky is visible; close windows and doors on the side where the moon rises.
- **Best protection:** Worship Ganesha and hear this Syamantaka katha — this is the most effective safeguard against the curse.
- **If you accidentally see the moon:** Recite the Syamantaka shloka (given below) three times; then listen to or read the full Syamantaka katha — this is the remedy Narada gave to Krishna himself.

## Mantras

**The Syamantaka Shloka — for removing Mithya Dosha**

This is the verse that Jambavan's young son Sukumar was singing while playing with the jewel — the song Shri Krishna heard from inside the cave. Reciting this verse removes the false-accusation dosha.

> सिंहः प्रसेनमावधीत् सिंहो जाम्बवता हतः ।
> सुकुमारक मा रोदीस्तव ह्येष स्यमन्तकः ॥

Siṃhaḥ Prasenāvadhīt Siṃho Jāmbavatā Hataḥ |
Sukumāraka Mā Rodīstava Hyeṣa Syamantakaḥ ||

**Meaning:** "The lion killed Prasena. Jambavan killed the lion. O dear child, do not weep — here is your Syamantaka jewel."

*If you accidentally see the moon on Chaturthi night — recite this shloka three times. This is the remedy Narada gave to Krishna himself.*
