## Katha

Dharmaraja Yudhishthira asked Lord Sri Krishna: "Madhava, what is the name of the ekadashi of Phalguna Shukla Paksha? Whose worship is performed on this day, and what fruit does it yield?"

The Lord smiled. He said: "Rajan, this ekadashi is called **Amalaki**. On this day, the dhatrī — the amalaki/amla tree — is worshipped. And this is no ordinary tree-worship — it is, in truth, worship of me. Listen, let me tell you an ancient story."

---

In the city of Vaidisha there lived a prosperous merchant — his name was **Chandramati**. He was wealthy, yet his heart was always inclined toward dharma. Every year, on the ekadashi of Phalguna Shukla Paksha, he would go to the amalaki grove outside the city, arrange a puja beneath an ancient dhatrī tree there, and keep vigil through the night.

One year, while Chandramati was making preparations for the puja, a hunter came into that same forest. His name was **Malyadhwaj**. He had spent his whole life hunting — killing deer, trapping birds — this was his life. That day too he had gone out in search of game, but nothing had come his way.

Night fell. The weary Malyadhwaj came and sat down near that same dhatrī tree.

Before him was a remarkable sight.

Rows of lamps, the fragrance of incense, the ringing of bells — and in the midst of it all, Chandramati was worshipping the amalaki/amla tree, chanting the name of Lord Vishnu. He offered water to the tree, placed flowers, set out naivedya. His eyes were closed, mantras on his lips.

Malyadhwaj watched all of this. He had not observed the vrat. He was not part of the puja. But something happened that night which he could not explain — his mind began to grow still. Thoughts of violence fell away. That night he neither ate nor slept — he simply watched Chandramati's worship, and slowly, quietly, sank into that atmosphere of devotion.

At dawn, Chandramati performed his parana and returned home.

Malyadhwaj too went home, silently.

---

Time passed. Both came to the end of their days.

Chandramati — who had for years offered dhatrī-puja with faithful devotion — went directly to the abode of Lord Vishnu. His entire lineage was redeemed.

And Malyadhwaj? He had kept no vrat, performed no puja — yet that night he had been present at the ekadashi vigil, and the atmosphere of devotion beside the amalaki/amla tree had entered him with every breath. He too received merit. In his next life he was born as a righteous, valiant king — and was given the name **Vasuratha**. And Lord Vishnu himself protected him.

In the tradition of the Brahma Vaivarta Purana it is said that merely hearing this katha is equal to the fruit of an Ashvamedha yajna. Whoever observes the Amalaki Ekadashi vrat even once with sincere devotion and worships the dhatrī has the sins of countless lifetimes destroyed.

## Significance

**Dhatrī** — this word itself is the soul of this ekadashi. Dhatrī means one who nourishes, one who raises like a mother. The amalaki/amla tree nourishes all of creation — its fruit holds a portion of amrita, its shade offers medicine, its roots carry the force of life. The scriptures say that of all the trees that first appeared on this earth at the time of the creation of Brahma, Vishnu, and Mahesh, the dhatrī was the first — and so this tree is the dwelling of all three deities.

**Lord Vishnu** in particular abides within the amalaki. Whoever offers water to this tree makes an offering to Vishnu. Whoever sits beneath it and chants his name sits at the threshold of Vaikuntha. This is why this ekadashi is held to be special among all ekadashis — here, the Lord is seen not in a temple, but in a living, sacred, fragrant tree.

Amalaki Ekadashi falls just before **Holashtak** and **Holi**. It is the festival of a threshold moment — when winter is departing, spring is arriving, and nature stands at a new beginning. The vrat of this ekadashi is the means of drawing that newness all the way into the soul.

## Vidhi

- **Puja location:** Go to an amalaki/amla tree in a forest or garden; if unavailable, worship an amla plant at home; if nothing is available, place an amla fruit in an earthen vessel and worship it as a symbol of Vishnu.
- **At dawn:** Bathe, spread a clean seat beneath the amalaki/amla tree; offer water saying "dhātryai namaḥ" three times; then offer flowers, tulasi leaves, akshat, incense, and a lamp; place fruits and sweets as naivedya.
- **Chanting:** Meditate on Lord Vishnu and chant "oṃ namo bhagavate vāsudevāya" throughout.
- **Fasting:** Observe a nirjal (waterless) or fruit-only fast; grains, lentils, and rice are forbidden; meat, alcohol, and tamasic food are also prohibited; do not cut hair; avoid anger and quarrelling.
- **Night vigil:** Keep vigil through the night beneath the dhatrī with kirtan — this is the central practice and the tradition of Chandramati.
- **Breaking the fast:** On dvadashi, after sunrise and before Rahu Kala; consuming tulasi-water and amla on the parana is especially auspicious.

## Mantras

> ॐ नमो भगवते वासुदेवाय

oṃ namo bhagavate vāsudevāya

Meaning: O Lord Vasudeva, I bow to you. — The twelve-syllable great mantra of Vishnu; its chanting on this ekadashi yields especially abundant fruit.
