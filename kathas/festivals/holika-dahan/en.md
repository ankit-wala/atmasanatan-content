## Katha

Hiranyakashipu, the enemy of the gods, performed fierce austerities on Mount Mandara. For thousands of years he stood within fire, endured bitter cold, and held his arms raised toward the sky. His tapas shook all three worlds, and Brahmadev himself appeared before him. Hiranyakashipu asked for a boon: "Let no man kill me, nor any beast. Let me not die by day, nor by night. Let me not die inside a dwelling, nor outside it. Let me not die on the earth, nor in the sky. Let me not be slain by any forged weapon, nor by any unformed one." Brahma granted the boon. Hiranyakashipu then conquered all three worlds and proclaimed: "I alone am God. Whoever utters the name of Vishnu shall be punished."

Yet within his own palace lived a child — Prahlada — who had already heard the Bhagavata-katha in his mother's womb. The sage Narada had given shelter to his mother Kayadu and had narrated to her the glory of Lord Vishnu. That nectar-like teaching descended into the heart of the child still in the womb. Prahlada was a devotee of Vishnu from birth.

When Hiranyakashipu learned of this, he burned with rage. He reasoned with his son, threatened him, sent him to the gurukula — but the name on Prahlada's lips never changed: "Narayana." Hiranyakashipu then attempted to destroy him by every means he could devise.

Prahlada was hurled from a mountain peak — Vishnu's wind caught and held him. He was trampled beneath the feet of great elephants — he remained unharmed. Poison was given to him — it became nectar. He was crushed under boulders and thrown into the sea — he rose above the water. Serpents bit him — he was unmoved. Every time, there was only one reason: his total, undivided bhakti.

Then Hiranyakashipu's sister Holika came forward. She possessed a divine boon — a garment that would protect her from being consumed in fire. She seated Prahlada in her lap and entered a blazing pyre, calculating that she would incinerate the devotee. Prahlada closed his eyes and continued to silently chant "Vishnu … Vishnu" within his heart.

Then the Lord's lila unfolded. A divine wind arose. That protective garment flew off Holika and wrapped itself around Prahlada. Holika began to burn, and the child Prahlada walked out of that roaring Holika Dahan unharmed — calm, fearless, a smile on his face. This is Holika Dahan: the moment when arrogance was consumed and devotion survived.

Hiranyakashipu's rage now reached its peak. He seized Prahlada and roared, "Where is your Vishnu?" Prahlada answered with perfect composure: "He is everywhere. He is in this stambha too." Hiranyakashipu struck that pillar with his mace — and from that stambha, with a thunderous roar, emerged Lord Narasimha: neither fully human, nor fully lion.

It was dusk — neither day nor night. He stood at the threshold of the doorway — neither inside the dwelling nor outside. He placed Hiranyakashipu across His thighs — neither on the earth nor in the sky. And with His nails — neither a forged weapon nor an unformed one — He slew him. Not a single condition of Brahma's boon was violated, yet the Lover of His devotees protected His devotee completely.

In the tradition of the Bhagavata Purana it is said that Lord Narasimha granted liberation even to Hiranyakashipu — for in his final moment the name "Narayana" arose in his mind. The hands that had been raised to kill his own son were, in the end, raised toward that very Vishnu. The bhakti of the devotee had accomplished the deliverance of his father as well.

## Significance

Holika Dahan is not merely a mythological event; it is a symbolic enactment of spiritual truth. Holika represents that arrogance which believes devotion can be suppressed by power, by boons, and by worldly force. And Prahlada represents that unbreakable faith which knows: God is everywhere — in every pillar, in every fire, in every crisis. When this conviction is firmly rooted in the heart, even fire turns cool.

The fall of Hiranyakashipu is testimony to the truth that kingship, military might, and even boons granted by the gods are helpless before bhakti. The greater he became, the greater his arrogance grew — and the natural end of arrogance is combustion. The fire of Holika Dahan reminds us every year that a Hiranyakashipu also dwells within us — arrogance, fear, and the craving to control — and the time to burn him is the night of Phalguna Purnima.

Prahlada's character also teaches that true devotion is not obstructed by age, by circumstance, or by the limits of family. A child whose own father was his enemy, surrounded on all sides by a demonic world — even he could keep God alive in his heart. The fire of Holika Dahan is a collective summons to consign all such inner states — envy, anger, hatred, pride — to the flames, so that the next morning we may rise renewed, like Prahlada.

## Vidhi

Forty days before Holika Dahan — on the day of Vasanta Panchami — a symbolic post (the Holika stambha) is planted in a designated open ground in the village or neighbourhood. From that day onward, people bring dry wood, cow-dung cakes, shrubs, and old items and heap them around it day by day. On the night of Phalguna Shukla Purnima, Holika Dahan is performed at the auspicious muhurta determined after the bhadra period has ended. A neem branch or a symbolic pillar representing Prahlada is placed at the centre of the Holika pyre. Families come bearing water, roli, akshat, flowers, coconut, and new stalks of wheat. The pandit or an elder of the family kindles the fire, and all present perform seven or three pradakshinas. During each pradakshina, an offering of water is made toward the fire while the name of Narayana is remembered. New grain stalks from the harvest are offered to the fire — an act of gratitude. The following morning, on Dhulendi, colours are played — and the bhasm of Holika is applied as a tilak on the forehead, welcoming the new day.

## Observance

**Holika Dahan:** On the night of Phalguna Shukla Purnima — light the fire at the auspicious muhurta set by the pandit, after the bhadra period has ended. Not alone, but together with family and community.

**Pradakshina:** Perform seven circumambulations of the Holika (three if seven are not possible). With each pradakshina, pour a stream of water toward the fire. Keep the name of Narayana in the mind throughout.

**Dhulendi:** On the morning after Holika Dahan, apply the cooled bhasm of Holika as a tilak on the forehead. Receive it as bhasm-prasad. Then play with colours — setting aside anger and enmity, embracing everyone.

**Bhajan-Kirtan:** On the night of Holika Dahan, sit around the fire and sing bhajans of Lord Narasimha and Prahlada. Spend the night in jagaran with the proclamation of "Jai Narasimha" and "Prahlada ki Jai." This is not merely celebration — it is worship.

## Mantras

**1. Vasudeva Mantra**

> ॐ नमो भगवते वासुदेवाय ॥

Om namo bhagavate vasudevaya

Meaning: O Lord Vasudeva (Vishnu), I bow to You.

---

**2. Narasimha Mantra**

> ॐ उग्रं वीरं महाविष्णुं ज्वलन्तं सर्वतोमुखम् ।
> नृसिंहं भीषणं भद्रं मृत्युमृत्युं नमाम्यहम् ॥

Om ugram viram mahavishnum jvalantam sarvatomukham,
nrisimham bhishanam bhadram mrityumrityum namamyaham

Meaning: I bow to Maha-Vishnu Narasimha, who is fierce, heroic, blazing everywhere, facing all directions, terrifying, auspicious, and the death of death itself.

---

**3. Prahlada Bhakti Shloka**

> नारायण परं ब्रह्म तत्त्वं नारायणः परः ।
> नारायणः परो ज्योतिरात्मा नारायणः परः ॥

Narayanah param brahma tattvam narayanah parah,
narayanah paro jyotir atma narayanah parah

Meaning: Narayana alone is the Supreme Brahman; Narayana alone is the ultimate truth; Narayana alone is the supreme light; and Narayana alone is the supreme Self. (This is the very essence of Prahlada's bhakti.)
