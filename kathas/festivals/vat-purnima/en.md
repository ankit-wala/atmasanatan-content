## Katha

King Ashwapati of the Madra kingdom had been childless for years.

He performed intense worship of Devi Savitri — for eighteen years. A hundred thousand oblations every day, strict disciplines, unbroken resolve. Pleased by his tapasya, Devi Savitri appeared before him.

The Devi said — "O King, I am satisfied by your devotion. You will be blessed with a radiant daughter."

When that daughter was born — she shone with the lustre of the sun. She was named **Savitri**. She grew up — extraordinary in form, extraordinary in intellect, extraordinary in courage.

When the time for her marriage came, King Ashwapati said to Savitri himself — "Daughter, you go yourself. Choose whichever bridegroom you find suitable."

Savitri journeyed from land to land.

When she returned, she was accompanied by a simple, radiant young man — **Satyavan.** Son of Dyumatsena, the blind and exiled king of the Shalva kingdom. They lived in the forest, cutting wood. There was no palace, no wealth — but his heart was pure and truth lived within him.

Savitri said — "Father, I have chosen him."

At that very moment the divine sage Narada was present there.

Narada paused for a moment and said — "O King, Satyavan's soul is luminous — there is no doubt of that. But only one year of life remains to him. Twelve months from now he will meet his death."

The entire assembly fell silent.

King Ashwapati's heart trembled. He said to Savitri — "Daughter, did you hear? There is still time. Choose someone else."

Savitri spoke with a calm and firm voice — "Father, the mind chooses once. I have chosen Satyavan — in life or in death. I will not change my decision."

Narada said quietly to the king — "Do not stop this girl. There is extraordinary power within her."

---

The wedding took place. Savitri came to live in the forest.

She set aside her royal garments and wore bark cloth. She served her in-laws. She helped daily with the work of the forest. And inwardly she counted — day by day.

When the final month of the year arrived, Savitri took a firm resolve — three days of rigorous fasting, vigil through the night.

That day came — **Jyeshtha Purnima.**

In the tradition of Maharashtra and Gujarat, this is the day on which Savitri faced her most difficult trial. The full moon's light spread through the forest.

Savitri's heart knew — today is that day.

She said to Satyavan — "Today I will also come with you into the forest."

Satyavan smiled and said — "Come."

In the forest Satyavan was cutting wood. Then an unbearable pain rose in his head. He swayed.

Savitri ran to him. Satyavan was falling to the ground — Savitri caught him. She laid his head in her lap. Satyavan's eyes closed.

Then Savitri saw — an extraordinary being. Yellow robes. Dark complexion. A noose in his hand. Otherworldly radiance in his eyes.

**Yama himself.**

Yama drew Satyavan's soul out of the body with his noose — a subtle form the size of a thumb — and began walking toward the south.

Savitri rose. And she began to follow Yama.

---

Yama turned and looked back.

"Savitri, do not follow me. Return to your husband's body. This is your dharma."

Savitri folded her hands — but did not stop.

She said — "Lord, where my husband is, there is my dharma. A wife's dharma is to follow her husband — in this world and in the next."

Yama saw her devotion. He said — "I am pleased by your bhakti. Ask for anything except Satyavan's life."

Savitri said — "Restore my father-in-law's sight."

"Tathāstu."

But Savitri did not stop. She kept following.

Yama said again — "Ask for more."

Savitri said — "May my father-in-law's lost kingdom be restored to him."

"Tathāstu."

Still Savitri kept walking.

Yama said — "One more boon. Ask."

Savitri said — "May my father have a hundred sons."

"Tathāstu."

Yama thought — now she will surely stop.

But Savitri's feet did not halt.

Yama said for the last time — "Savitri, your devotion is unmatched. Ask for the final boon."

Savitri said with a smile — "May I have a hundred sons — from Satyavan."

Yama stopped.

He understood — Savitri had not set a trap; she had spoken truth. Yama himself had said "Tathāstu." This boon could not be fulfilled without Satyavan.

Yama gently released Satyavan's soul from the noose.

"Go, Savitri. You have won love's victory over death."

---

Savitri returned.

Satyavan lay beneath that same vat-vriksha. Slowly his eyes opened. He looked at Savitri.

"Savitri... where was I?"

Tears flowed from Savitri's eyes.

She said — "Let us go home."

When they reached the ashram — Dyumatsena's sight had returned. And not long after, his kingdom too.

In the tradition of the Mahabharata, it is said that the woman who observes Vat Purnima with devotion and steadfastness — she remembers that very radiance of Savitri that made even death bow down. Her saubhagya, her husband's long life, and the joy of progeny are protected.

## Significance

**Amavasya and Purnima — one story, two traditions:**
This vrat is observed across all of India, but there are two main traditions regarding the tithi.

In North India (Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, Rajasthan, Punjab, Madhya Pradesh) it is observed on **Jyeshtha Krishna Amavasya** — known as "Vat Savitri Vrat." In Maharashtra and Gujarat, this same vrat is observed on **Jyeshtha Shukla Purnima** — known as "Vat Purnima."

There is a difference among traditional scholars: some acharyas hold that Satyavan's death occurred on Amavasya, others on Purnima. Both tithis fall within the month of Jyeshtha and lie within the same month's orbit. The original katha, the resolve, and the puja-vidhi are one — only the tithi differs by regional tradition.

**The special significance of Purnima:**
In the tradition of Maharashtra and Gujarat, the moon of Purnima is regarded as the witness to Savitri's victory. The full moonlight — symbol of wholeness and unending life — is linked with the vat-vriksha's power of longevity. This is why in Maharashtra this vrat is held in especially high regard.

**The symbol of the vat-vriksha:**
The tree under which Satyavan fell was a mighty **vat-vriksha** — the banyan. The banyan's aerial roots descend from sky to earth; it is never fully destroyed; it lives for centuries. This is why it became the symbol of unending life and a husband's long life.

**Savitri's courage — the ideal of stri-shakti:**
This is not a story of submission — it is a story of power. Savitri chose, Savitri resolved, Savitri fought, and Savitri won. Defeating Yama was not achieved by physical force — it was achieved by intellect, love, and steadfast devotion.

## Vidhi

- **For whom:** Observed by married women in Maharashtra and Gujarat on Jyeshtha Shukla Purnima.
- **Fasting:** Full-day nirjal or phalahara upavasa; in Maharashtra many women fast from sunrise to moonrise — they offer arghya to the full moon on Purnima night and then break their fast.
- **Day before Purnima:** Gather materials — red-yellow thread, five or seven kinds of grain (wheat, chana, moong, urad, jowar, bajra, rice), bamboo basket, bel, flowers, akshata, sindur, haldi-kumkum, bangles, paan, coconut.
- **Purnima morning:** Bathe and dress in saubhagya attire (green, red, or yellow); take the sankalpa holding water, akshata, and flowers — "I observe Vat Purnima vrat for the protection of my husband's long life and my saubhagya."
- **Gauri puja:** In Maharashtra, Gauri (Devi Parvati) is also worshipped alongside this vrat — she is the goddess of saubhagya; the joint worship of Savitri and Gauri carries special significance.
- **Vat-vriksha puja:** Go to a vat-vriksha (if none nearby, bring a branch home in a clay pot); offer water, haldi-kumkum, and flowers at the root; arrange five or seven kinds of grain in a thali and offer them at the root — a rite characteristic of Maharashtra's tradition; establish an image or picture of Savitri-Satyavan beneath the vat.
- **Tying the thread:** Walk around the vat seven times wrapping raw thread (sutra) with each circumambulation — making the wish for the husband's long life; this thread is the symbol of a bond that even death cannot break; listen to the Vat Purnima vrat katha together with family after the puja.
- **Breaking the fast:** On Purnima evening, after sighting the full moon and offering arghya, conclude the vrat; break the fast with sattvic food or fruit; offer haldi-kumkum, coconut, and gifts to Brahmins or suhagin women.

## Mantras

**Savitri mantra:**

> ॐ सावित्र्यै नमः

Oṃ Sāvitryai Namaḥ

Meaning: "Salutations to Devi Savitri." — This mantra is chanted at the beginning of the puja and with each circumambulation.

---

**Vat-vriksha puja verse:**

> वटमूले स्थितो ब्रह्मा वटमध्ये जनार्दनः ।
> वटाग्रे तु शिवो देवः सावित्री वटसंश्रिता ॥

Vaṭamūle sthito Brahmā vaṭamadhye Janārdanaḥ |
Vaṭāgre tu Śivo devaḥ Sāvitrī vaṭasaṃśritā ||

Meaning: "At the root of the vat-vriksha abides Brahma, in the middle Vishnu (Janardana), and at the crown Shiva — and Devi Savitri herself resides within the vat-vriksha." — This verse is recited while watering the vat-vriksha.

---

**Saubhagya-longevity verse:**

> अखण्डसौभाग्यं देहि पुत्रपौत्रान् यशस्विनः ।
> भर्तुः आयुर्वृद्धिं कुरु सावित्रि नमोऽस्तु ते ॥

Akhaṇḍasaubhāgyaṃ dehi putrapautrān yaśasvinaḥ |
Bhartuḥ āyurvṛddhiṃ kuru Sāvitrī namo'stu te ||

Meaning: "O Savitri, grant me unbroken saubhagya, illustrious sons and grandsons, and increase my husband's lifespan — salutations to you." — This verse is recited at the end of the vrat with folded hands before the image of Savitri.
