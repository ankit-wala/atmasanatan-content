## Katha

Āśvina Śukla Pūrṇimā — the autumn night when the moon shines in its fullest radiance. This tithi marks the birthday of a great soul whose transformation did not begin in a royal lineage but in a dark corner of the forest — where a bandit's life was turned inside out.

In those days, a man named Ratnakar lived deep in the dense forests. He was a hunter, a robber. Waylaying travellers on jungle paths, resorting to violence when needed — this was how he sustained himself. He had a wife, children, and an aged father to feed. He saw no wrong in any of it. Whatever is done for one's family must be right — that was his conviction.

One day Devarṣi Narada was passing through that forest — his vīṇā on his shoulder, his ever-serene smile on his face. Ratnakar stopped him.

Narada looked at Ratnakar with calm eyes. There was neither fear nor anger in him. He asked — "Why do you do this? You rob, you sometimes take lives — for whom?"

Ratnakar answered plainly — "For my family. For my wife, my children."

Narada was quiet for a moment, then said — "Then go and ask them one question: the sin from which you earn their bread — will they share that sin with you?"

The question seemed strange to Ratnakar. But he thought — Narada might slip away. So he tied Narada to a tree and walked home.

He arrived. He asked his wife — "Everything I rob, every act of violence — I do it for you. Will you carry the weight of that sin alongside me?"

His wife did not pause for even a moment. She said — "What you do is your karma, your sin. We want bread and clothing — not sin."

He went to his father. His father too said — "Bear the fruit of your own deeds yourself. We are no sharers."

The ground shifted beneath Ratnakar's feet.

Those for whom he had been sinning for years — they had said plainly: we are not with you. The sin is yours; so too the merit.

He returned. He fell at Narada's feet.

Narada placed a hand on his back and said — "Now remember Rāma. Keep repeating 'Mara Mara' — it will itself become 'Rāma Rāma.'"

Ratnakar sat down right there. He closed his eyes. 'Mara Mara' — the words left his lips. And then… time stood still.

Years passed. Decades passed. He did not rise. Earth gathered around him; a valmīka — an anthill of termites — covered him over. An entire mound formed above him — and inside, a flame burned on.

When that mound finally opened — the one who emerged was no longer Ratnakar.

He was **Maharṣi Vālmīki** — born of the valmīka, and therefore Vālmīki. His face was radiant; his eyes held a depth that comes only from tapasya spanning many lifetimes.

He now lived on the banks of the Tamasā river. One morning, he was walking with his disciple Bharadvāja toward the river for their bath. Near a clear forest pool, a pair of Krauñca birds were playing together — absorbed in each other, lost in love.

Then a hunter (Niṣāda) released an arrow.

The male Krauñca fell where he stood. The female's cry of grief tore through the forest.

Vālmīki stood frozen. Something broke inside him — or something opened. The anguish that was the female's descended into his own heart. The arrow of compassion that struck him was far deeper than the hunter's arrow.

And from his lips, unbidden, came —

**mā niṣāda pratiṣṭhāṃ tvam agamaḥ śāśvatīḥ samāḥ |**
**yat krauñca-mithunād ekam avadhīḥ kāma-mohitam ||**

— O Niṣāda, may you never find lasting honour; for you have slain one of this Krauñca pair who were lost in love.

These words did not come from his will. It was grief — *śokaḥ ślokatvam āgataḥ* — grief itself became verse. The first line of Sanskrit poetry. The world's first metre. Born from compassion.

Bharadvāja saw it — on his guru's face there was both wonder and astonishment.

That night Brahma appeared. He said — "Vālmīki, this verse came from your lips by my own inspiration. Now take the full story of Rāma — as Narada once narrated it to you — and compose it in this very metre. This will be the Rāmāyaṇa. As long as this earth endures, your fame will endure."

From that day Vālmīki composed the Rāmāyaṇa — twenty-four thousand verses, seven kāṇḍas. The hands that had once wrought violence were the same hands that now wrote the epic which would shelter hundreds of millions across the centuries.

The journey from Ratnakar to Vālmīki says this: no one falls so far that they cannot rise; and compassion is the one power that can turn even a stone heart into a poet.

## Significance

**Ādi-kavi — The First Poet:**

Vālmīki is called the 'Ādi-kavi' — the first poet of Sanskrit. Before him there were Vedic mantras and hymns of praise — but a human metre born from the pain of a lover's loss first came from Vālmīki's lips. That is why his Rāmāyaṇa is not merely scripture — it is the fountainhead of human literature.

**Compassion — The Seed of Epic:**

The episode of the Krauñca's slaying is not merely an incident — it is a philosophical truth. An epic is born not from anger but from compassion. When the heart fills so completely with another's pain that words pour forth unbidden — that is poetry. Vālmīki's first verse teaches exactly this.

**The Greatest Example of Transformation:**

The story of Vālmīki is Hinduism's most powerful declaration that past deeds cannot bind a person for ever. Through the power of Rāma's name, tapasya, and the guru's grace, anyone can be transformed. From Ratnakar to Vālmīki — this is not merely one person's story; it is the possibility that lives within every human being who wishes to change.

**The Coincidence of Śarad Pūrṇimā:**

Vālmīki Jayanti falls on Āśvina Śukla Pūrṇimā — Śarad Pūrṇimā. This is the most brilliant night of the moon. Vālmīki's radiance was of the same kind — emerging from darkness into full light. The moon of Śarad Pūrṇimā and the birthday of the Ādi-kavi are two expressions of the same single truth.

**The Tradition of the Rāmāyaṇa:**

The Vālmīki Rāmāyaṇa is not merely a story — it is a mirror of Rāma's character. Vālmīki gave Sītā refuge when Ayodhyā did not. He raised Lava and Kuśa and taught them the Rāmāyaṇa. In this way Vālmīki is not only the author of the text — he is the living guardian of the Rāma-kathā tradition.

## Vidhi

- **At dawn:** Rise at brahma-muhūrta, bathe, wear clean clothes; place an image or portrait of Maharṣi Vālmīki at the place of worship; take the saṅkalpa — "On this auspicious occasion of Vālmīki Jayanti, I shall worship Ādi-kavi Vālmīki and recite the Rāmāyaṇa."
- **Vālmīki Pūjana:** Offer flowers, sandalwood paste, akṣata, incense, and a lamp; offer banana as naivedya; perform abhiṣeka with pañcāmṛta; chant "Oṃ Vālmīkaye Namaḥ" 108 times.
- **Rāmāyaṇa recitation:** Recite or listen to the Rāmāyaṇa — especially the Krauñca episode and first verse from the Bāla Kāṇḍa; those who cannot recite the full text may recite the Sundar Kāṇḍa; in Vālmīki communities, an unbroken (akhaṇḍa) Rāmāyaṇa recitation is held throughout the day.
- **Evening lamps:** At sunset, light lamps at the entrance of the home, at the place of worship, in the temple, and in the street; Śarad Pūrṇimā night is regarded as the moon's amṛta-shower — lighting lamps in memory of Maharṣi Vālmīki honours the light of his transformation.
- **Bhajan-Kīrtana:** Hold programmes of Rāma-bhajans and Vālmīki-stutis; sing kīrtana on the story of how 'Mara Mara' became 'Rāma Rāma.'
- **Śobhāyātrā:** In Vālmīki communities, a decorated image of Vālmīki is placed on a chariot and a procession moves through the streets with chants of "Jai Vālmīki" and "Jai Śrī Rāma."
- **Dāna:** Donate books — especially the Rāmāyaṇa or other scriptures; offer food to the poor.

## Mantras

**Vālmīki Rāmāyaṇa — The First Verse:**

> मा निषाद प्रतिष्ठां त्वमगमः शाश्वतीः समाः।
> यत्क्रौञ्चमिथुनादेकमवधीः काममोहितम्॥

mā niṣāda pratiṣṭhāṃ tvam agamaḥ śāśvatīḥ samāḥ |
yat krauñca-mithunād ekam avadhīḥ kāma-mohitam ||

Meaning: O Niṣāda, may you never attain eternal honour — for you have slain one of this pair of Krauñca birds who was lost in the rapture of love.

(This is the first verse of Sanskrit poetry — born from compassion, it emerged unbidden from the lips of Maharṣi Vālmīki. On Vālmīki Jayanti, its recitation is an act of remembrance of the Ādi-kavi.)

---

**Rāmāyaṇa Phala-Verse:**

> यद्यत्र भवति श्रुत्वा रामायणमनुत्तमम्।
> आयुष्यं पुष्टिदं धन्यं यशस्यं पापनाशनम्॥

yad yatra bhavati śrutvā rāmāyaṇam anuttamam |
āyuṣyaṃ puṣṭidaṃ dhanyaṃ yaśasyaṃ pāpanāśanam ||

Meaning: Wherever this supreme Rāmāyaṇa is heard, there one finds long life, nourishment, prosperity, fame, and the destruction of sins.

In the tradition of the Vālmīki Rāmāyaṇa, it is said that whoever listens to or recites this great epic, the sins of seven generations are destroyed and Rāma's grace dwells in that home for ever.
