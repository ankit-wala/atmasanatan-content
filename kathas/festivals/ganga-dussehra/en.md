## Katha

Three generations. One vow. One impossible task.

Maharaja Sagar of the Ikshvaku dynasty was the mighty king of Ayodhya. He performed a great Ashvamedha yajna — a sacrifice that would earn him the title of sovereign emperor. The Ashvamedha horse was released, and Sagar's sixty thousand sons marched behind it as its guardians.

The horse wandered, traversing all directions — and then one day it vanished.

Sagar's sons searched, but the horse was nowhere to be found. Then they began to dig through the layers of the earth, going deeper and deeper — toward the netherworld. The digging destroyed countless beings; all creation trembled. The gods, stricken with fear, went to Brahma — "What is happening? The earth itself is being rent apart."

Brahma replied — "This is the havoc of Sagar's sons. But do not worry — its end is already written in Vishnu's plan."

At last, Sagar's sons found the horse — near the ashrama of the great sage Kapila. Kapila Muni sat absorbed in meditation, and the horse was tethered beside him. In truth, it was Devraj Indra who had placed the horse there — to obstruct the yajna.

Without reflection, Sagar's sons accused the great sage of theft. His tapas had been unbroken for ages — divine energy had gathered in every pore of his being. His eyes opened.

One glance.

Just one glance — and sixty thousand sons of Sagar were reduced to ash on that very spot. Their remains became one with the earth.

---

Anshuman, grandson of Maharaja Sagar, traced the place. He stood humbly before Kapila Muni, asked for forgiveness, and received the horse back — and with it, the question that burned in his heart: "O great sage, how may these ancestors be redeemed?"

Kapila Muni answered — "Their liberation will come only when the Ganga of heaven flows over their ashes. Ganga dwells now in the celestial realm. She must be brought down to earth."

Hearing this, Anshuman's heart grew heavy. He undertook tapas — but did not succeed.

His son Dilipa took the same vow, performing austerities for years — yet Ganga did not descend.

Then Dilipa's son Bhagiratha made his resolution — "What three generations could not accomplish, I shall accomplish."

---

Bhagiratha entrusted his kingdom to his ministers and departed for penance.

On those heights of the Himalayas where even the wind grows still, Bhagiratha began a fierce tapas for Brahma. He stood on one leg, arms raised, without food or water — for divine years uncounted.

Brahma was pleased. He appeared and said — "Child, ask."

Bhagiratha said — "Lord, send Ganga to earth. Sanctify the ashes of my ancestors."

Brahma replied — "I can release Ganga — but her force is so immense that the earth cannot bear it. The earth will be shattered to pieces. Therefore first propitiate Lord Shiva — only he can hold that force within his matted locks."

Bhagiratha sat in tapas once more — this time for Lord Shiva. For a full year he renounced food and water, absorbed entirely in the repetition of his name.

Lord Shiva was pleased. He appeared and said — "Bhagiratha, I shall receive Ganga in my matted locks."

---

The day of Jyeshtha Shukla Dashami arrived.

Brahma released Ganga. She descended from the heavens with thunderous force — the sound of her coming filled the sky. The gods gazed. Sages rose to their feet.

And Ganga descended directly into the matted locks of Lord Shiva.

Shiva held that force. The locks grew wet. Ganga's streams were absorbed into that tangled mass. For some time Ganga wandered within the labyrinth of those locks — then Bhagiratha praised and pleaded once more.

Shiva was pleased. He released one stream from his locks — and Ganga flowed onto the earth.

Bhagiratha's chariot moved forward. Ganga followed behind — as a mother follows her son.

Through mountains, through forests, across plains they travelled — and at last came the place where the ashes of sixty thousand sons of Sagar lay.

The waters of Ganga flowed over those ashes.

In that instant, a wondrous sight arose — sixty thousand souls rose upward. Their bonds were broken. They set out toward liberation.

The tradition of the Valmiki Ramayana holds that from that day, Ganga's current carries not merely water — she is witness to the liberation of Sagar's sons. Whoever bathes in the Ganga touches that very stream of moksha born of Bhagiratha's tapas. From that time on, Ganga has been called "Bhagirathi" — daughter of Bhagiratha.

## Significance

Ganga Dussehra is not merely the celebration of a river's descent — it is the celebration of an unbreakable resolve that passes from generation to generation and, at last, bears fruit.

**The meaning of Dussehra:** The word "Dussehra" is formed from "Dash + Hara" — that which removes ten. On this day, bathing in the Ganga destroys ten kinds of sin:

Three bodily sins: violence, theft, and illicit union.
Four sins of speech: false utterance, harsh words, slander, and idle dispute.
Three sins of the mind: malice toward others, covetousness of another's wealth, and dwelling in untruth.

It is Ganga who removes these ten — and that is why this day is called "Dussehra."

**The glory of Ganga:** The scriptures declare that merely beholding Ganga, touching her, and bathing in her — all three bestow merit. Jyeshtha Shukla Dashami is held to be Ganga's birthday — the tithi on which she first came to earth. Bathing on this day is considered infinitely more fruitful than on ordinary days.

**The message of Bhagiratha:** The katha of Bhagiratha also teaches how deep the debt to one's ancestors is — the duty toward those who came before. A single descendant gave his entire life to grant liberation to sixty thousand ancestors across three generations. This is the very spirit that underlies Ganga-tarpan and pitru-shraddha.

On Ganga Dussehra, multitudes of devotees pour onto the riverbanks — from Haridwar, Varanasi, Prayagraj, and Rishikesh to Gangasagar. This bathing purifies not only the body but the mind as well.

## Vidhi

- **Morning sankalpa:** Rise in the Brahma muhurta; take a vow — "On this Jyeshtha Shukla Dashami, I praise Mother Ganga, bathe in her waters, and seek the removal of ten sins."
- **Ganga-snan:** Go to the Ganga's bank without concern for the summer heat — the touch of the Ganga on this day is of especial merit; if unable to go, add Gangajal to water at home and bathe with it, repeating "Om namo bhagavatyai gaṅgāyai namaḥ."
- **Ganga-pujan:** After bathing, offer flowers, akshata, roli, and dhoop-deep at the riverbank or before a vessel filled with Gangajal at home; recite the Ganga-Ashtak.
- **Dan:** Giving in multiples of ten holds special significance — ten kinds of grain, ten coins, ten flowers; donating grain, water, sesame, barley, gold, silver, cloth, fruit, ghee, and jaggery is auspicious; for those with the means, giving of a cow, land, or sesame is especially meritorious.
- **Tarpan:** Offer tarpan for the ancestors with Gangajal, holding the intention — "As sixty thousand sons of Sagar attained liberation, may my ancestors also attain liberation."
- **Deepdan:** At dusk, light a lamp and set it afloat on the Ganga — or in flowing water at home; remembering departed members of one's family, pray for their liberation.
- **Ganga Aarti:** Join the evening aarti at the Ganga's bank — especially magnificent at Haridwar and Varanasi on this day; at home, perform aarti before a vessel filled with Gangajal.
- **Fasting:** Optional — some devotees observe a single meal (ekahara) or take only fruit; abstaining from meat, intoxicants, and tamasic food is especially enjoined on this day.

## Mantras

**ॐ नमः शिवाय — Shiva-stuti**

> ॐ नमः शिवाय ।

Om namaḥ śivāya |

Meaning: O Shiva, I bow to you. The very Shiva who held Ganga within his matted locks, through whose grace this holy source of water descended safely onto the earth.

---

**Gangashtak — praise of Mother Ganga**

> नमामि गंगे तव पादपंकजं
> सुरासुरैर्वन्दितदिव्यरूपम् ।
> भुक्तिं च मुक्तिं च ददासि नित्यं
> भावानुसारेण सदा नराणाम् ॥

Namāmi gaṅge tava pādapaṅkajaṃ surāsurairvanditadivyarūpam | bhuktiṃ ca muktiṃ ca dadāsi nityaṃ bhāvānusāreṇa sadā narāṇām ||

Meaning: O Ganga, I bow to your lotus feet — worshipped by gods and demons alike in your divine form. You bestow upon all beings, according to the sincerity of their heart, both earthly fulfilment and liberation.

---

**Pitru-tarpan shloka — prayer for liberation**

> गंगे च यमुने चैव गोदावरी सरस्वती ।
> नर्मदे सिंधु कावेरी जलेऽस्मिन् सन्निधिं कुरु ॥

Gaṅge ca yamune caiva godāvarī sarasvatī | narmade sindhu kāverī jale'smin sannidhiṃ kuru ||

Meaning: O Ganga, Yamuna, Godavari, Sarasvati, Narmada, Sindhu, and Kaveri — make your presence known in this water. May our ancestors find liberation through the tarpan offered from this water.
