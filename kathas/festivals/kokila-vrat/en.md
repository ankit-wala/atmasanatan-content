## Katha

It was the month of Ashadha. Clouds had gathered in the sky — deep, heavy, the way a very old waiting finally descends.

And in the midst of that rain, a cuckoo was singing in a forest.

She was no ordinary cuckoo.

In folk tradition it is said that in some previous birth, Parvati — who was then in the form of Sati — came to a moment that became the cause of separation between her and Shiva. Some traditions say she inadvertently committed an error; others say the play of fate set her apart from Shiva for a time. But the essence is one — she was separated from him. And the fruit of that separation was a curse: that she would wander this earth in the form of a cuckoo.

Consider — Parvati, who is Mahashakti herself, whose very existence is inseparable from Shiva — she is confined within the small body of a cuckoo. Shiva is on Kailasa, deep in samadhi — and she cannot reach him. She can see him, but cannot touch him. She can speak his name, but not in words — only in that unceasing call that rises from a cuckoo's throat.

And then Ashadha came.

Ashadha is the month when the rains arrive in their full power. The forests turn green. The rivers fill. Earth and sky meet. And as witness to that meeting — the cuckoo sings.

Parvati poured all her longing into that call.

Through the night she sang — that sweet, tender, urgent voice that can only be heard in the rains. In her call was love, was longing, and was that unbreakable devotion which does not grow quiet even after countless births.

On Kailasa, Mahadeva was in samadhi. But that call reached deep within him.

As a river does not break a stone — it flows past it, and the stone itself begins to yield.

Lord Shiva opened his eyes.

In the cuckoo's voice he recognised what only he could recognise — the bhava of Parvati. Her devotion, her waiting, her unbreakable love.

And the curse was broken.

She became Parvati once more. Shiva accepted her. And on that Purnima night — the Purnima night of Ashadha — when the rains were at their peak, there came that union of consciousness and shakti which is the very foundation of creation.

From that time on, this tradition has been observed — whenever Ashadha Purnima arrives, when the first call of the cuckoo is heard in the rain, women know it is auspicious. That call reminds them of Parvati — of the love that did not break even after separation, of the tapasya that survived even within a curse.

Kokila Vrat is the answer to that call.

## Significance

The katha of Kokila Vrat is a folk tradition of North India that sees the season of rains and the reunion of Shiva and Parvati in the same light.

The cuckoo — kokila — is in Indian tradition the symbol of both longing and love. Her voice comes with the rains, in the season of union. When the earth is thirsty and the sky pours down — then the cuckoo sings. This is no coincidence. It is the memory of that katha.

In this vrat, women bring the same bhava to their worship that Parvati carried in her call — that love for a husband which does not waver with circumstance. Just as Parvati, even as a cuckoo, never forgot Shiva, so too does the woman who keeps this vrat receive protection of her suhāg, and the bond between husband and wife is strengthened further.

The mango tree — the cuckoo's home, where she dwells, the branch on which she sits and sings — is venerable in this vrat. That tree evokes the memory of Parvati's cuckoo-form.

Ashadha Purnima — the tithi on which Shiva and Parvati were reunited — is a celebration of the meeting of sky and earth, consciousness and shakti, Shiva and Shakti. The rains themselves are the celebration of this union.

## Vidhi

- **For whom:** Observed by married women on Ashadha Purnima for the longevity and good fortune of their husbands.
- **Fasting:** Complete fast or fruit-only fast; abstain from salt and grains throughout the day.
- **At dawn:** Rise before sunrise; bathe; wear clean clothes — yellow or green garments are auspicious.
- **Shiva-Parvati pūjan:** Install an image of Shiva and Parvati; perform abhiṣeka of the Shivalinga with water, milk, and bilva leaves; offer sindūra, bangles, bindī, and white or yellow flowers to Parvati; light incense and a lamp; chant *Oṃ Namaḥ Śivāya* and *Oṃ Umā-Maheśvarābhyāṃ Namaḥ*.
- **Tree pūjan:** Go to a nearby mango tree; offer water at the roots; tie raw thread (kaccā sūta) or red maulī around the tree as a vow of protection of the suhāg — do not omit this; offer flowers and akṣata.
- **Katha-śravaṇa:** In the evening, listen to or read the Kokila Vrat katha — this is considered essential; the vrat is not complete without it.
- **Special:** If the cuckoo's voice is heard on the day of the vrat, receive it as auspicious — it is the blessing of Parvati.
- **Breaking the fast:** Break the fast after the Purnima night pūjā, or early the next morning; before pāraṇa, bow to Shiva and Parvati and offer dāna-dakṣiṇā.

## Mantras

**Shiva-Parvati pūjan mantra:**

> ॐ उमामहेश्वराभ्यां नमः

Oṃ Umā-Maheśvarābhyāṃ Namaḥ

Meaning: "I bow to Uma (Parvati) and Maheshvara (Shiva) — salutations to you both."

---

**Parvati stuti — recite during pūjan:**

> सर्वमङ्गलमाङ्गल्ये शिवे सर्वार्थसाधिके ।
> शरण्ये त्र्यम्बके गौरि नारायणि नमोऽस्तु ते ॥

Sarva-maṅgala-māṅgalye śive sarvārtha-sādhike |
Śaraṇye tryambake gauri Nārāyaṇi namo'stu te ||

Meaning: "O auspicious one who is the source of all auspiciousness, O Shive, O fulfiller of all purposes — O Gauri, consort of Tryambaka, O Narayani, salutations to you."

---

In the folk tradition of Kokila Vrat, it is said that the married woman who keeps this vrat with devotion on Ashadha Purnima receives protection of her husband's life and health, love and harmony in married life are sustained, and just as Parvati's call did not go unanswered, so too does the prayer of the one who observes this vrat reach Shiva and Parvati.
