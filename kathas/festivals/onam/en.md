## Katha

**Mahabali's Golden Age:**

Mahabali was the grandson of Prahlada.

Born into the lineage of Hiranyakashipu — yet the opposite of that tradition. Where his ancestors had been the very image of arrogance and adharma, Mahabali stood for justice, compassion, and generosity. He had received the seed of devotion from his grandfather Prahlada — and he had let it flourish in his kingdom.

Mahabali's reign was an age that lives on even today in Kerala's collective memory.

In that reign, no one went hungry, no one was poor.
There was no high, there was no low.
No one spoke falsehood, no one betrayed.
There was no sickness, no sorrow.
In every home a lamp burned; in every heart there was contentment.

The people said — "This is heaven itself."

Mahabali had, by his prowess, brought all three worlds under his dominion. The devas were driven out of the heavens. Indra had lost his throne. The gods went before Lord Vishnu and beseeched him — "O Hari, the order of the universe is broken. Restore us to our place."

---

**The Arrival of Vamana:**

Lord Vishnu took the form of Vamana, born of Devamata Aditi — the form of a small brahmin boy. Small of stature, gentle of face, a water-pot and umbrella in hand.

At that time, Mahabali was performing the Vishwajit Mahayagna on the banks of the Narmada. This yagna was a testament to his generosity — no one who came seeking was to leave empty-handed.

Vamana arrived at that yagna.

Mahabali's guru Shukracharya recognized the boy. He came to Bali and warned him —

"This is no ordinary brahmin child. This is Vishnu himself, come to deceive you. Do not grant his request."

Mahabali heard Shukracharya's words. He paused a moment. Then, with a calm heart, he spoke —

"Gurudev, you speak the truth. But consider — if this truly is Vishnu, then it is my good fortune that he himself has come to my yagna to ask. The king who turns away a petitioner who has come to his yagna falls from dharma. Even if this costs me my kingdom, even my all — I will not send this brahmin away empty-handed."

---

**The Request for Three Paces:**

Vamana said to Mahabali — "I ask only for three paces of land."

Mahabali laughed. At so grand a yagna, from so generous a king — only three paces of land?

"Ask for more," he said, "what use are three paces?"

"Only three paces is what I need," said Vamana.

Mahabali lifted water from his vessel. He took the sankalpa of the gift. He began to pour.

And in that very moment — everything changed.

---

**Trivikrama — Measuring the Three Worlds:**

Vamana's form began to grow.

From a small child to vast — then vaster still — then so immense that his form could not be contained by all the sky. One step fell upon the earth — the entire earth was measured. The second step rose to the heavens — all of svargaloka was measured.

He became **Trivikrama** — the measurer of all three worlds.

He now turned to Mahabali — "O King, where shall I place my third step?"

---

**Bali's Head:**

Mahabali beheld the cosmic form of Trivikrama. He understood — this is lila, this is grace. What Gurudev had said had proven true.

But Mahabali did not waver.

He stepped forward. He bowed. And in a calm voice he said —

"Lord, I have no land left. But I have my head. Place your third step upon my brow."

The Lord placed his foot upon Mahabali's head — and Bali descended into the netherworld.

---

**The Boon — To Return Home Each Year:**

But in this moment, something more occurred.

Lord Vishnu was deeply moved by that generosity, that devotion, that selfless surrender. He himself bowed before his devotee's largeness of spirit.

He granted Mahabali a boon —

"Bali, you are extraordinary. Your generosity, your dharma, your resolve — all of it is immortal. I give you the kingdom of Sutala, which is finer even than heaven. Now ask a boon."

Mahabali said — "Lord, I ask for only one thing. My people love me, and I love them. Once a year — let me go to visit my people. To see their homes. To look into their eyes."

The Lord said — "Tathaastu. So be it."

---

That day came — and comes every year.

When in the courtyard of every home in Kerala the pookalam is laid — that rangoli of coloured flowers. When the Onam Sadya is prepared — twenty-seven dishes, served on a banana leaf. When the waves of the boat-race (vallam kali) rise — then Mahabali comes.

He looks upon his people.

His people welcome their king.

This is Onam.

In the tradition of the Bhagavata Purana, it is said that the devotee who on Onam hears this katha of Bali and Vamana, lays a pookalam, and performs puja to Vishnu-Vamana — receives happiness and prosperity in this world, and in the end attains the shelter of the Lord.

## Significance

**Bali's Golden Age — Kerala's Collective Memory:**
Onam is not merely a festival — it is a memory. The people of Kerala remember that king in whose reign no one went hungry, no one was unequal. This memory runs so deep that centuries later the joy of Onam is as vivid as if the king were arriving right now — because the belief holds that he does come.

**Vamana — The Fifth Avatar of Vishnu:**
Vamana is the fifth among the ten principal avatars of Lord Vishnu. This avatar belongs to Treta Yuga. Vamana took all three worlds from the demon king Bali in three steps and restored the gods to their place. But at the same time, by granting Bali immortality and a boon, he delivered this message: devotion and generosity are never in vain.

**The Philosophy of Trivikrama — The Measure of the Universe:**
In the form of Trivikrama, Vishnu reveals that the Supreme pervades all existence — from earth to heaven, from the gross to the subtle. What appears 'small' is in fact all-pervading.

**Bali's Devotion — Dharma Even in Adversity:**
Mahabali's greatest quality is this: even when Shukracharya warned him — he did not withdraw from his gift. His faith was that no protector is greater than dharma. This resolve remains an ideal for humanity even today.

**Kerala's Harvest Festival:**
Onam falls in the Malayalam month of Chingam (Bhadrapada) — this is the time of the new paddy harvest. The Onam Sadya feast is a celebration of that new harvest. In this way the festival brings together both the spiritual and the agricultural traditions.

## Vidhi

- **Ten-day pookalam:** Each day from Atham to Thiruvonam, add one more ring of fresh flowers to the courtyard rangoli; on the tenth day (Thiruvonam) the largest pookalam is completed; use seven or more varieties of flowers.
- **Bali puja:** Place a symbolic image or picture of Mahabali at the centre of the pookalam; welcome him with flowers, a lamp, and akshata.
- **Vamana/Vishnu puja:** After bathing in the morning, place an image or picture of Lord Vamana or Vishnu; perform Panchamrita abhisheka; offer yellow flowers, tulasi, and a lamp; recite Vishnu Sahasranama or the Vamana Stotra.
- **Bali-Vamana Katha:** On Thiruvonam, read or listen to the Bali-Vamana katha from Bhagavata Purana, Skandha 8.
- **Onam Sadya:** Partake of the Sadya on a banana leaf with the family — rice, sambar, avial, payasam, and traditional dishes; the shared meal is a remembrance of Mahabali's golden reign when all were equal.
- **Vallam Kali (Boat Race):** Boat races on Kerala's rivers and backwaters are the central communal celebration of Onam — a symbol of collective joy.
- **Deepa dana:** In the evening, light lamps at the entrance of the home and beside the pookalam.
