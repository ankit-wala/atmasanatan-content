## Katha

Chaitra month, the eighth day of the dark fortnight. In a small village in northern India there lived a Brahmin woman named Gunavati. She was a devoted worshipper of Sheetala Mata. Every year, on the evening of Saptami — the night before Sheetala Ashtami, which falls eight days after Holi — she would cook dal-bati, rotis, and rice and set them aside. The next morning, this basi bhojan (day-old cold food, left unheated through the night) would be offered to the goddess. No fire was lit in the house on Ashtami — that was the tradition, that was Mata's command.

That particular year the household was caught up in many preparations. Her eldest son's wedding was approaching, guests were coming and going. Gunavati was preoccupied, and that Saptami night she forgot to prepare the food in advance. When Ashtami's dawn arrived she remembered, but it was already too late. The children were hungry; the guests were waiting. She reasoned with herself — just this once, surely Mata will forgive — and cooked fresh hot food, serving it first to everyone in the house. She told herself she would offer the goddess her bhog afterward.

That very afternoon both her younger sons — Radheshyam and Mohan — were suddenly seized by high fever. Red rashes broke out across their bodies. These were unmistakable signs of Sheetala Mata's displeasure — she is herself the goddess of coolness, and to offer her hot, freshly cooked food was an affront to her very nature. Gunavati was stricken with fear. The elderly neighbour Savitri Devi noticed and said quietly, "Daughter-in-law, on this Ashtami — did you offer Mata basi bhojan, or did you cook fresh?" Gunavati felt her heart rise into her throat.

At that very moment she set off for Mata's temple — barefoot, hair uncombed. She wept all the way. Arriving at the temple, she fell at the goddess's feet. She had gathered what little stale food remained from Saptami night, a bowl of curd, some neem leaves (the medicinal leaves sacred to the goddess), and water, and offered them to Mata with trembling devotion. Tears poured from her eyes; on her lips was a single prayer — "Mother, I made a mistake. Forgive my children."

That night Sheetala Mata herself appeared to Gunavati in a dream — dressed in white, seated on her donkey, holding a broom and her kamandal (the water vessel she carries). She said, "Daughter, your devotion is true and your repentance is pure. Your children will recover by morning. Remember from now on — I am Sheetala, the mother of coolness. Freshly cooked fire-food is not for me; what I love is food that is basi, cold, and sattvic."

By the time morning came, the fever of both boys had broken and the rashes had subsided. Gunavati's eyes filled with tears of gratitude. From that day forward she took a vow — preparing basi bhojan on the night of Saptami, before every Sheetala Ashtami, became the unbreakable rule of her life.

---

The tradition of the Skanda Purana holds that devotees who worship Mata on Sheetala Ashtami with the proper rites and offer basi bhojan receive protection for their family from smallpox, fever, and skin afflictions.

## Significance

Sheetala Mata — whose very name means "she who bestows coolness" — is an ancient folk goddess of northern India, also described in the Skanda Purana. She is the goddess of disease-relief, especially smallpox (badi mata), measles, and other fever-and-rash ailments. Her vahana is the donkey (gardabha), a symbol of patience and service. In her hands she carries a broom — which sweeps away disease; a kamandal filled with water — which cools the fever; and a branch of neem — the symbol of antiseptic medicine that heals. She wears white garments, signifying purity and coolness.

The insistence on basi bhojan is not merely symbolic — it is seasonal and practical as well. The month of Chaitra marks the beginning of summer. The body begins to overheat. At this seasonal cusp, pitta increases and skin diseases spread. The worship of Sheetala Mata — cold water, day-old food, neem — is an ancient health tradition, a devotional-symbolic expression of building the body's resistance to illness. That Sheetala Ashtami arrives exactly eight days after Holi reflects a precise understanding of this seasonal turning.

In religious terms, Sheetala Mata is a compassionate mother, not a wrathful goddess. Even her displeasure is like a mother's correction — as a mother disciplines a child for its fault, but forgives from the heart the moment the child repents. This is the very spirit expressed in this katha: the goddess's response to the transgression was swift, but her forgiveness and healing were swifter still.

## Vidhi

Preparations for Sheetala Ashtami begin on the evening of Saptami. That evening, dal-bati, rice, rotis, pua, and sweet dishes are cooked and set aside. On Ashtami no fire is lit in the house — this is the heart of the observance. In the morning, bathe and go to Mata's temple or the household shrine. Offer cold water, curd, neem leaves, sindhur, and the basi bhojan prepared the previous evening. Perform abhishek on Mata's image or portrait with water. Place neem leaves at her feet. Light incense and a lamp. After the puja, this same basi bhojan is distributed as prasad throughout the family, and everyone eats only this food through the day. This is not an upvas (fast) but a "basi bhojan vrat" — an abstention from fire.

## Observance

**Basi bhojan:** On Saptami evening, prepare the entire next day's food — dal, rotis, pua, rice, sweet dishes — and set it aside. On Ashtami this cold food is offered to Mata and then shared among the family. Freshly cooked hot food is forbidden all day.

**Fasting:** This is not a complete fast. Some devotees — especially those with young children — observe a salt-free fast or subsist on fruit. Elderly women typically take only basi bhojan and curd with water.

**Mata's puja:** Bathe in cold water at dawn and perform the puja of Sheetala Mata. Carry basi bhojan, curd, and neem leaves to the temple in an earthen vessel. The puja includes offerings of sindhur, akshat, flowers, and water.

**Neem leaves:** Neem is Sheetala Mata's sacred tree. Offering neem leaves to the goddess is essential. Tying a neem branch above the doorway of the home is considered protection against disease. After the puja, taking a little neem juice or a few tender neem leaves is also part of the tradition.

## Mantras

> ॐ ह्रीं श्रीं शीतलायै नमः

Om hrim shrim shitalayai namah

Meaning: Salutations to Mata Sheetala. Hrim is the seed-syllable of Shakti; Shrim is the energy of Lakshmi — through this mantra Mata is invoked and healing from disease is sought.

vande shitalām devīm rāsabhasthām digambarām |
mārjanīkalashopetām shūrpālamkritamastakim ||

Meaning: I bow to Mata Sheetala — who is seated upon the donkey, who pervades all directions (digambara), who carries the broom and the kamandal, and whose head is adorned with the winnowing fan (shurpa). This verse from the Skanda Purana invites meditation upon the form of the goddess.
