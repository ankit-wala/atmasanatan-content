## Katha

The trading ship of Sadhukara's son-in-law was now returning from Ratnapur.

Months of journeying, hard bargaining, and tireless effort — all had borne fruit. The hold of the ship was full of precious goods, jewels, and wealth. The heart was at ease; the eyes held pride.

---

Meanwhile, in Kashipur, Kalavati awaited him.

When someone brought word that the ship had drawn near the town, she was filled with joy. Hearing news of her husband's return after so many months, she set out from home at once.

But in her eagerness, one thing was forgotten.

The pūrṇāhuti — the final completion of the Satyanarayan puja's prasad rite — still remained to be done at home. She set off toward the shore without completing that rite.

---

On the other side, the ship reached the bank.

The boat came ashore. The son-in-law stood on the shore — master of a ship laden with wealth, a victorious merchant.

At that very moment, an elderly sadhu came there. He was dressed as a vānaprasthī — saffron cloth, a serene bearing, and a strange depth in his eyes.

The sadhu asked the son-in-law plainly —

*"O merchant, what is in this ship?"*

The son-in-law glanced once at the ship. Then at the sadhu.

A thought arose in his mind — who is this who asks me? My ship, my trade, my wealth.

He said with a laugh —

*"What can I say, Baba — only straw and dry wood."*

The sadhu said quietly —

*"Tathastu."* (So be it.)

And he walked on.

---

An unknown unease stirred in the son-in-law's heart.

He stepped toward the storage hold of the ship. He opened the door.

In the hold where precious cargo had been — only straw and dry leaves lay scattered.

All the wealth, all the goods, all the jewels — everything had vanished.

The son-in-law sat down right there. He put his hands to his forehead. A deep anguish rose in his heart.

He remembered — the incident of the third chapter, when he had rejected the puja prasad on the riverbank out of arrogance. That time the ship had stopped.

And now — the same Bhagavan had taken the form of a sadhu and asked. And he had done the very same thing again — he had called the wealth given by Bhagavan worthless.

He fell to his knees. With both hands joined in prayer, he said —

*"Prabhu, I have erred. Out of pride I called Your grace 'straw.' Forgive me."*

---

Kalavati, having arrived at the shore and seeing the situation, understood at once.

She turned and went straight home.

The puja tray was still there at home — the prasad was incomplete. With full devotion she meditated on Bhagavan Satyanarayan, completed the pūrṇāhuti, and prepared the prasad.

She then took that prasad herself and went to her husband at the shore.

The son-in-law stretched out both hands. Humbly, gratefully, he received the prasad.

---

At that very moment, a change came over the hold of the ship.

The straw and leaves were gone. Wealth, jewels, and precious goods — all reappeared.

The ship was as full as before — no, perhaps even more so.

Kalavati and her husband entered the town. Sadhukara welcomed them. There was joy throughout the family.

That night, once again, the puja of Bhagavan Satyanarayan was performed. The katha was recited. Prasad was prepared and distributed to all — not one person left without prasad.

---

In the tradition of the Skanda Purana, Reva Khanda, it is said that — devotees who listen to this fourth chapter with attention and never treat Bhagavan's grace as something trifling, sorrow departs from their homes and Bhagavan Satyanarayan's protection remains with them always on their journeys.

## Significance

The teaching of the fourth chapter is subtle and deep.

In the third chapter, the son-in-law had refused the prasad — that was outward arrogance. In the fourth chapter, he called the wealth given by Bhagavan himself "straw" — this is an even subtler form of inner pride.

When someone asks — *"What do you have?"* — and we either hide it, or make it seem trivial, or deny it with pride — we are in truth rejecting the Giver who bestowed it.

Kalavati's lapse is also present in this katha — leaving before completing the pūrṇāhuti of the prasad. There was love, there was eagerness — but the rite was left unfinished. The katha teaches that in Bhagavan's puja there must be neither haste nor delay — only a completion carried out with full reverence.

Two chapters, two different characters, two different lapses — but one teaching: **receive Bhagavan's grace with complete humility and gratitude.**

## Vidhi

- **When:** Any Purnima, Ekadashi, or auspicious occasion.
- **Sankalpa:** After a morning bath, take the sankalpa — "I am observing the Satyanarayan Vrat and performing the puja."
- **Fasting:** Keep a fast before the katha — fruit may be taken; break the fast after the katha and prasad.
- **Setup:** Place an image or idol of Bhagavan Satyanarayan (Vishnu) on a pure seat; perform panchamrita abhisheka (milk, yoghurt, ghee, honey, sugar); offer tulasi leaves, flowers, yellow cloth, and a lamp.
- **Katha shravan:** Recite or have recited all five chapters — family members and all present should listen together in a single sitting.
- **Aarti:** Perform the aarti of Bhagavan after the katha.
- **Prasad:** Prepare panjiri or sheera (wheat flour, ghee, sugar, banana); offer prasad to every person present — not one person should leave without it; do not treat prasad as trifling or hold it back.
- **Pūrṇāhuti first:** If a Satyanarayan puja is underway at home, complete the pūrṇāhuti and distribution of prasad before leaving for any other task — the lesson of Kalavati's departure in this chapter.
- **Accepting Bhagavan's grace:** When asked about wealth, health, or blessings received — answer with humility and gratitude, not with pride; in this katha, calling them "straw" was the cause of calamity.

## Mantras

**Satyanarayan Mula Mantra:**

> ॐ नमो भगवते सत्यनारायणाय

oṃ namo bhagavate satyanārāyaṇāya

Meaning: O Bhagavan Satyanarayan, I bow to You.

---

**Parama Satya Dhyana Mantra:**

> सत्यं परं धीमहि।

satyaṃ paraṃ dhīmahi

Meaning: We meditate upon the Supreme Truth. (Shrimad Bhagavata 1.1.1)
