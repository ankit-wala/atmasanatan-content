## Katha

**A problem at the heart of creation:**

When Brahma created the universe — humans, gods, and demons all came into being. The worlds filled with life. Living beings multiplied. And as beings multiplied, so did their deeds.

But a problem arose.

Souls began arriving at Yamaraj's court — yet where was the full record of their lives? Who had done what — when — and how much? How was a judgment to be made? Who would receive heaven, who liberation — how could any of this be decided when there was no ledger at all?

Yamaraj placed this question before Brahma.

---

**Brahma's meditation:**

Brahma heard the problem and fell into deep contemplation. Creation needed a deity who was all-knowing — one who would know every deed of every being, who would be witness to every moment, and whose memory would hold no gap.

Brahma entered meditation — for 11,000 years.

His mind held a single intention: to bring forth a deity who would not merely see, but would record without cease.

---

**The manifestation of Chitragupta:**

After 11,000 years of meditation, Brahma opened his eyes.

A radiant being stood before him. In his hands were a pen and a book. Upon his face rested a stillness unlike any other — as though he had known everything from the very moment of his birth.

Brahma asked: "Who are you? Where have you come from?"

He replied: "Lord, I was born from your own body. While you were in meditation, the intention within your body — 'one who keeps the record is needed' — that very intention gave birth to me. My name is **Chitragupta**."

'Chitra' — meaning the record, the image, that which is otherwise unseen.
'Gupta' — meaning hidden; yet nothing hidden escapes his sight.

Brahma's eyes filled with tears. He said: "Go to Yamaloka and serve as Yamaraj's assistant. Every human, every god, every living being — whatever good or ill they do — write the full account in your **Chitragupta-panji** (the ledger of deeds). This record will stand as the final testimony in all of creation. No one will be able to alter it, nor erase it."

Chitragupta accepted this charge.

---

**The Kayastha tradition:**

Chitragupta was born from Brahma's *kaya* (body) — and for this reason his descendants are called **Kayasthas**. The Kayastha community has traditionally served as scribes, administrators, and ministers in royal courts — because their ancestor Chitragupta took upon himself the work of writing and justice.

On this day, in Kayastha households, the pen, inkpot, and book receive special worship — these are Chitragupta's emblems. Beginning new books and new accounts on this day is considered auspicious.

The tradition of the Padma Purana holds: whoever worships Chitragupta on Kartik Shukla Dwitiya finds the merit in their ledger of deeds increased, and justice done to them in Yamaraj's court.

## Significance

**The ledger of karma:**
Chitragupta is the divine scribe of all creation. No deed — whether open or hidden — escapes his gaze. This very thought is the foundation of moral life in the Indian tradition: "Whatever you do, Chitragupta is watching."

**A special celebration for the Kayastha community:**
For Kayastha families, this is the day of their ancestral deity. They regard Chitragupta as their forefather — and on this day they perform special worship, gather as families, and begin new accounts.

**Kalam-Dawat Puja — the worship of pen and inkpot:**
The instruments of knowledge — pen, inkpot, book — are worshipped. This tradition declares: what is written is permanent; therefore treat the act of writing as sacred.

**Alongside Bhai Dooj:**
This puja falls on Kartik Shukla Dwitiya — the same day as Bhai Dooj. The reunion of Yamaraj and Yamuna (Bhai Dooj) and the worship of Chitragupta share one tithi — a festival of Yamaloka and the justice of dharma.

## Vidhi

- **At dawn:** Bathe and take a vow (sankalp) of observance.
- **Establishing the deity:** Place an image or picture of Chitragupta at the worship space — preferably one showing him holding a pen and book.
- **Kalam-Dawat Puja:** Place a pen and inkpot at the worship space; perform abhishek with panchamrit (milk, curd, ghee, honey, water) or purify them with clean water. After worship, put them to use that same day — the first writing is considered auspicious.
- **Offerings:** Place a new ledger, notebook, or book as an offering; offer incense, a lamp, flowers, and naivedya (sweets or fruit).
- **Katha and first writing:** Read or listen to the Chitragupta Katha; then with the pen write the first letter or numeral in the new account — this is the auspicious beginning.
- **For Kayastha families:** This is the principal festival of the Kayastha community. All family members gather for collective worship of Chitragupta, their ancestral deity.
- **New accounts:** Beginning new account books and ledgers on this day is highly auspicious — those in trade and administration open their new accounts today.
- **Alongside Bhai Dooj:** In households where Bhai Dooj is celebrated, Chitragupta Puja is observed together with it on the same tithi.

## Mantras

**Chitragupta Namaskar Mantra:**

> चित्रगुप्ताय नमस्तुभ्यं लेखनीपट्टधारिणे ।
> कर्मणां साक्षिणे तुभ्यं धर्मराजानुचारिणे ॥

Citraguptāya namastubhyaṃ lekhaṇīpaṭṭadhāriṇe |
karmaṇāṃ sākṣiṇe tubhyaṃ dharmarājānucāriṇe ||

Meaning: "Salutation to Chitragupta — bearer of the pen and tablet, witness to all deeds, attendant of Dharmaraj."
