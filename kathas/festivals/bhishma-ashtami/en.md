## Katha

After the great war of Kurukshetra had ended, an extraordinary sight remained upon the battlefield. While countless warriors had attained a hero's death, one great charioteer still lived — Bhishma Pitamah. He lay tranquil upon his shara-shaiya, his body pierced through with innumerable arrows, yet on his face there was neither pain nor agitation — only the deep stillness of profound meditation.

Pitamah Bhishma had received the boon of iccha-mrityu — the power of chosen death — from his father King Shantanu. By virtue of this boon, he could not depart from his body until he himself desired to do so. But he was waiting — waiting for the sun's northward journey into Uttarayan. At that time the sun was in Dakshinayan, and according to the shastras, to leave the body during Dakshinayan is not considered auspicious. And so the Pitamah held his life-breath in place through the force of his yogic power and transformed the shara-shaiya itself into a place of tapasya.

In those days Kurukshetra became a remarkable gurukul. Every day Dharmaraja Yudhishthira would come to the Pitamah in the company of Bhagavan Shri Krishna. Yudhishthira carried within him a heavy burden of remorse — the weight of the destruction of so many kinsmen pressed upon him without respite. Then Bhishma, veiling the torment of the arrows within the cloak of yoga, would expound upon dharma, rajadharma, and mokshadharma with a sweep and depth that lives on today as immortal wisdom in the Shanti Parva and Anushasana Parva of the Mahabharata. The Vedas, the Upanishads, the science of statecraft — all flowed from his lips with effortless grace. Even in that dying state, he poured forth an unbroken Ganga of knowledge from that ocean of wisdom so profound.

The bright fortnight of the month of Magha arrived. At Makar Sankranti the sun left Dakshinayan and entered Uttarayan. Still the Pitamah waited eight days more. On Magha Shukla Ashtami — the eighth day of Uttarayan — when the sun's radiance was supremely auspicious and ascending, the Pitamah knew: this was the moment.

Yudhishthira, the Pandavas, and Shri Krishna were present at his side. The Pitamah looked upon them all with a gaze full of affection and said — "The sun has now turned toward the north. This is that sacred hour."

Thereupon he turned his senses inward. He fixed his gaze upon the sun. He gathered the full force of his lifelong yoga-sadhana and offered a final inner hymn of praise at the feet of Shri Krishna. Then — without a cry of anguish, without any struggle — calm, steady, fully aware — he directed the prana-vayu upward and relinquished the body. The arrows remained sunk in the earth; the great soul was liberated.

It is held in the tradition of the Mahabharata that whoever offers tarpan to the Pitamah with devotion on the day of Bhishma Ashtami — the ancestors of his seven lineages, in whatever condition they may be — are satisfied and become partakers in the merit born of the great Pitamah's own final departure.

## Significance

Bhishma Pitamah is counted among the twelve Mahajanas of the Bhagavata tradition. His life — his lifelong vow of brahmacharya, his boundless devotion to his father, and his final departure from the body through yoga — stands as living proof of dharma's highest ideals.

The most distinctive significance of Bhishma Ashtami lies in the tradition of his tarpan. He himself remained a lifelong brahmachari — he had no son who could offer him tarpan. For this reason the dharmashastra ordained that the whole of Hindu society should offer him tarpan with the reverence one accords a father. For those who are childless, or whose lineage has no male heir to perform the ancestral rites, Bhishma-tarpan on this day is held to be especially fruitful — for the Pitamah himself knew that grief of having no son to offer him water.

This festival also reminds us that death need not be an accident — it can be a choice — if throughout one's life one has practised yoga, dharma, and devotion to the Lord.

## Vidhi

- **Observed on:** Magha Shukla Ashtami.
- **Fasting:** Optional; tarpan and charitable giving are the principal observances.
- **At dawn:** Bathe and put on clean clothes.
- **Tarpan:** At a river, a lake, or at home — fill a copper vessel with water; mix in black sesame seeds (til); cup both hands, take the water in anjali, and slowly pour it while reciting the Bhishma tarpan mantra three times; sankalpa: "I offer tarpan with devotion to Bhishma Pitamah — of the Vaiyaghrapadya gotra, of the Sankruti pravar, son of Ganga, and lifelong brahmachari."
- **For the childless / sonless lineage:** Those whose ancestors have no one to offer them tarpan should perform Bhishma-panchaka tarpan with particular care — through this, liberation reaches even those ancestors.
- **Reading:** Read or listen to a passage from the Anushasana Parva or Shanti Parva of the Mahabharata — great merit accrues from this.
- **Charity:** Give food to Brahmins; offer dakshina according to your means.
- **Special:** Celebrated with special devotion in northern and central India.

## Mantras

**Bhishma Tarpan Mantra:**

> वैयाघ्रपद्य-गोत्राय सांकृति-प्रवराय च।
> गंगापुत्राय भीष्माय आजन्म-ब्रह्मचारिणे॥

vaiyāghrapadya-gotrāya sāṃkṛti-pravarāya ca
gaṃgāputrāya bhīṣmāya ājanma-brahmacāriṇe

Meaning: To Bhishma Pitamah — of the Vaiyaghrapadya gotra, of the Sankruti pravar, son of Ganga, lifelong brahmachari — this tarpan-water is offered.

---

**Bhishma Namaskar Mantra:**

> ॐ भीष्माय नमः

oṃ bhīṣmāya namaḥ

Meaning: O Bhishma Pitamah, I bow to you with reverence.
