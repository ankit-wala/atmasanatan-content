## Katha

King Ashwapati of the Madra kingdom had been without a child for many years.

He undertook fierce worship of Devi Savitri — for eighteen long years. One lakh oblations every day, rigorous vows, an unbroken resolve. Pleased by his tapasya, Devi Savitri appeared before him.

The goddess said — "O King, I am satisfied by your devotion. You shall be blessed with a radiant daughter."

When that daughter was born — she shone with the brilliance of the sun. She was named **Savitri**. She grew up — extraordinary in beauty, extraordinary in intellect, extraordinary in courage.

When the time for her marriage arrived, King Ashwapati himself said to Savitri — "Daughter, go forth yourself. Choose the groom you find worthy."

Savitri travelled from kingdom to kingdom.

When she returned, she brought with her a simple, luminous young man — **Satyavan.** The son of Dyumatsena, the blind and exiled king of the Shalva kingdom. He lived in the forest, cutting wood. There was no palace, no grandeur — but his heart was pure and truth dwelled within it.

Savitri said — "Father, I have chosen him."

At that very moment, the divine sage Narada was present there.

Narada paused and said — "O King, Satyavan's soul is radiant — of that there is no doubt. But only one year remains in his life. Twelve months from now, he will meet his death."

The assembled court fell silent.

King Ashwapati's heart trembled. He said to Savitri — "Daughter, did you hear? There is still time. Choose another."

Savitri replied in a calm and resolute voice — "Father, the heart chooses only once. I have chosen Satyavan — in life or in death. I will not change my decision."

Narada said quietly to the king — "Do not stop this daughter. There is extraordinary power within her."

---

The marriage took place. Savitri came to live in the forest.

She set aside her royal garments and wore bark-cloth. She served her mother-in-law and father-in-law. She joined in the daily work of the forest. And within herself, she kept counting — one day at a time.

When the final month of that year arrived, Savitri took a firm vow — three days of strict fasting, vigil through the nights.

That day came — **Jyeshtha Krishna Amavasya.**

Savitri's heart knew — today was that day.

She said to Satyavan — "Today I will come with you into the forest."

Satyavan smiled and said — "Come."

In the forest, Satyavan was cutting wood. Then a searing pain rose in his head. He swayed.

Savitri ran to him. Satyavan was falling to the earth — Savitri caught him. She placed his head in her lap. Satyavan's eyes closed.

Then Savitri saw — an extraordinary figure. Yellow robes. A dark form. A noose in his hand. An otherworldly radiance in his eyes.

**Yama himself — the god of death.**

Yama drew Satyavan's soul out of his body with the noose — a subtle form the size of a thumb — and turned southward.

Savitri rose. And followed Yama.

---

Yama turned around.

"Savitri, do not follow me. Return to the body of your husband. This is your dharma."

Savitri joined her palms — but did not stop.

She said — "Lord, wherever my husband is, that is where my dharma lies. The dharma of a wife is to follow her husband — in this world and in the next."

Yama saw her steadfastness. He said — "I am pleased by your devotion. Ask for any boon except Satyavan's life."

Savitri said — "Restore my father-in-law's sight."

"Tathāstu — so be it."

But Savitri did not stop. She kept following.

Yama said again — "Ask for another boon."

Savitri said — "Let my father-in-law regain his lost kingdom."

"Tathāstu."

Still Savitri walked on.

Yama said — "One more boon. Ask."

Savitri said — "May my father be blessed with a hundred sons."

"Tathāstu."

Yama thought — surely she will stop now.

But Savitri's feet did not halt.

Yama said one final time — "Savitri, your steadfastness is beyond measure. Ask for a last boon."

Savitri said with a smile — "May I have a hundred sons — from Satyavan."

Yama stopped.

He understood — Savitri had not laid a trap; she had spoken the truth. Yama himself had said "Tathāstu." Now, without Satyavan, this boon could never be fulfilled.

Yama gently released Satyavan's soul from the noose.

"Go, Savitri. You have won the victory of love over death."

---

Savitri turned back.

Satyavan lay beneath that very tree. Slowly, his eyes opened. He looked at Savitri.

"Savitri... where was I?"

Tears flowed from Savitri's eyes.

She said — "Let us go home."

When they reached the hermitage — Dyumatsena's sight had returned. And not long after, his kingdom was restored to him as well.

The tradition of the Mahabharata holds that a woman who observes Vat Savitri Vrat with devotion and steadfastness invokes the very radiance of Savitri — the radiance that made death itself bow down. Her good fortune, her husband's long life, and the blessing of children are protected.

## Significance

**The symbol of the Vat tree:**
The tree beneath which Satyavan fell was a great **Vat tree** — the banyan. The aerial roots of the banyan descend from the sky to the earth; it is never fully destroyed and lives for centuries. This is why it became the symbol of eternal life and a husband's longevity.

**Savitri's courage — the ideal of feminine power:**
This is not a story of submission — it is a story of strength. Savitri chose, Savitri acted, Savitri fought, and Savitri won. Defeating Yama was not achieved by physical force — it was achieved by intellect, love, and steadfast devotion.

**Jyeshtha Amavasya:**
This vrat is observed on Jyeshtha Krishna Amavasya — in North India. In some regions (particularly Maharashtra, Gujarat, and Bengal) there is also a tradition of observing this vrat on Jyeshtha Purnima.

**The supreme form of pativrata-dharma:**
Vat Savitri Vrat is the vrat of married women — for the long life, health, and prosperity of their husbands. It is among the most important women's vrats of North India.

## Vidhi

- **For whom:** Observed by married women for the long life, health, and prosperity of their husbands.
- **Fasting:** Full-day fast — nirjal or fruits only; regional custom permits a single meal (ekahara).
- **The day before Amavasya:** Gather materials — soot (raw thread), a bamboo basket, flowers, akshat, sindoor, bangles, paan, turmeric, kumkum, and fruit.
- **Morning of Amavasya:** Bathe and dress in colours of saubhagya (red or yellow); take sankalp holding water, akshat, and flowers — "For the protection of my husband's long life and good fortune."
- **Vat tree worship:** Go to the Vat tree (or a temple, or place a Vat branch at home); offer water at the roots; apply turmeric and kumkum; present flowers, fruit, and naivedya. In the absence of a Vat tree, worshipping the Peepal tree is also traditional.
- **Thread wrapping:** Circumambulate the Vat tree seven times while winding raw thread (soot) around it — with each round, hold the prayer for the husband's long life in the heart. This thread is the symbol of the bond that death itself cannot break.
- **Image and katha:** Establish an image of Savitri and Satyavan beneath the Vat tree; listen to the complete Vat Savitri Vrat Katha with family — the puja is incomplete without the katha.
- **Dakshina:** Offer dakshina to Brahmins; present gifts to the mother-in-law or elder women.
- **Breaking the fast:** On the evening of Amavasya or the following morning, after the final puja, take parana with sattvic food or fruit.

## Mantras

**Savitri Mantra:**

> ॐ सावित्र्यै नमः

Oṃ Sāvitryai Namaḥ

Meaning: "Salutations to Devi Savitri." — This mantra is chanted at the beginning of the puja and with each circumambulation.

---

**Vat tree worship shloka:**

> वटमूले स्थितो ब्रह्मा वटमध्ये जनार्दनः ।
> वटाग्रे तु शिवो देवः सावित्री वटसंश्रिता ॥

Vaṭamūle sthito Brahmā vaṭamadhye Janārdanaḥ |
Vaṭāgre tu Śivo devaḥ Sāvitrī vaṭasaṃśritā ||

Meaning: "At the roots of the Vat tree dwells Brahma, in the middle dwells Vishnu (Janardana), and at the crown dwells Shiva — and Devi Savitri herself resides within the Vat tree." — This shloka is recited while offering water to the Vat tree.

---

**Saubhagya and longevity shloka:**

> अखण्डसौभाग्यं देहि पुत्रपौत्रान् यशस्विनः ।
> भर्तुः आयुर्वृद्धिं कुरु सावित्रि नमोऽस्तु ते ॥

Akhaṇḍasaubhāgyaṃ dehi putrapautrān yaśasvinaḥ |
Bhartuḥ āyurvṛddhiṃ kuru Sāvitrī namo'stu te ||

Meaning: "O Savitri, grant me unbroken good fortune, glorious sons and grandsons, and increase my husband's years — I bow to you." — This shloka is recited with joined palms before the image of Savitri at the close of the vrat.
