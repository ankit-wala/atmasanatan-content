## Katha

Gangaur — a union of two words. "Gan" meaning Lord Shiva (called "Isar" in Rajasthani) and "Gaur" meaning Mata Gauri. This festival is a celebration of the reunion of those two loving souls whose love is the very foundation of creation.

In the tradition of the Skanda Purana, the story is told thus —

After the marriage of Mata Parvati and Lord Shiva, Gauri went to her pihara (her parents' home). As is natural for a newly wed daughter, she stayed at her parents' house for a time. The spring season had arrived, and the festival of Holi was at the doorstep.

On the day of Holi, Mata Gauri descended into the village. All around, the air was alive with joy and color. The village women — who labored in the fields and homes, whose clothes were plain and whose ornaments were few — when they heard that Mata Gauri herself had come, they came running to her. They arrived late, but their hearts were pure and their devotion unshakeable.

Mata Gauri saw the reverence of those simple women, and love welled up in her heart. She knew that the long life of their husbands and the peace and prosperity of their homes were their deepest dream. Mata Gauri drew the saubhagya-sindoor from her own parting — the very sindoor that Shiva himself had filled there — and gave it generously to those ordinary women. They were blessed, and their saubhagya became unbroken.

Meanwhile, the kulina women of the village — who had been taking their time with their full sixteen adornments, who arrived dressed in silk and gold — came late. No sindoor remained for Mata Gauri to give. But she was a mother — a mother who would never send any daughter away empty-handed. She took a thread dipped in water and marked the foreheads of the kulina women with a tilak of sacred water.

For a moment, disappointment stirred in the hearts of the kulina women. Then Mata Gauri gently explained to them — "In this water-tilak my blessing is as complete as it was in the sindoor. Your husbands will live long lives, your homes will be filled with wealth and grain, and even time will not be able to touch your love."

From that day, for eighteen days — from Holi until Chaitra Shukla Tritiya — women fashion clay idols of Mata Gauri and Isar, worship them, sing songs, and pray for the long life of their husbands. On the day of Tritiya, the jal-yatra takes place — Mata Gauri bids farewell to her pihara and returns to Kailash, together with Isar. That farewell is filled with both joy and tears — as every daughter's farewell is.

In the tradition of the Skanda Purana, it is said that a woman who observes this vrat with devotion receives the same blessing that Mata Gauri gave to the village women on that Holi day — unbroken saubhagya, love, and prosperity.

## Significance

Gangaur is the most beloved and important festival of women in Rajasthan, Madhya Pradesh, Gujarat, and parts of Maharashtra. It is not merely a festival — it is a bond of the heart: one woman to another, a mother to her daughter, a wife to her husband. This festival holds everything that is deepest in a married woman's heart — the love of her husband, the peace and happiness of her home, and the memories of her mother's house.

The most poignant truth of this story is that Mata Gauri gave her first and most generous blessing to the ordinary women — those who came first, whose clothes were plain. The message is profound: in the goddess's grace there is no distinction of lineage, wealth, or beauty. Devotion and the purity of one's heart are her only measure. Gangaur is a celebration of this truth.

In Rajasthani culture, Gangaur holds a place of reverence equal to Durga Puja. The Gangaur processions held in Jaipur, Udaipur, and Jodhpur are world-renowned for their splendor, folk music, and color. Both newly married women and unmarried girls observe this vrat — unmarried girls for the wish of a worthy husband, and married women for the long life of their husband and their saubhagya.

## Vidhi

The Gangaur vrat begins on the second day of Holi (Chaitra Krishna Ekam) and continues for eighteen days until Chaitra Shukla Tritiya. Each morning, women fashion fresh clay idols of Isar and Gauri from potter's clay or black earth. The idols are adorned with colorful clothes, ornaments, and full bridal decoration. Fresh flowers, dub grass, sindoor, mehndi, suhaag offerings, and a water-filled kalash are the essential materials of worship. During the puja, Gangaur folk songs are sung — these songs are filled with the feelings of Mata Gauri and Isar's love, memories of the pihara, and the mood of the farewell hour. On the day of Chaitra Shukla Tritiya, the jal-yatra (jalzhelani) is held. Women lift the idols of Gauri and Isar onto their heads and carry them in a procession to a river, pond, or well, where they are immersed in the water — this is Gauri's return to Kailash.

## Observance

**Duration of the vrat:** From the day after Holi until Chaitra Shukla Tritiya — eighteen days. In some places, the vrat is kept only for the final three or five days.

**Idol worship:** Each day, fresh clay idols are made, adorned, and worshipped with sixteen offerings. Dub grass, sindoor, and mehndi are offered with special care.

**Songs and sixteen adornments:** The Gangaur folk songs are an inseparable part of the worship — without them the puja is considered incomplete. Women also dress themselves in the full sixteen adornments and wear yellow and red clothes.

**Jal-yatra:** On Tritiya, the procession of Gauri and Isar to the river or pond and their immersion — the symbolic moment of Mata Gauri's farewell from her pihara.

## Mantras

> ॐ गौर्यै नमः

om gauryai namaḥ

Meaning: Salutation to Mata Gauri — the presiding goddess of saubhagya, love, and auspiciousness.
