## Katha

This is a story from when creation was still young.

Brahma performed a great yajna. The sacred fire blazed, the mantras resounded — and from those flames a Goddess emerged, wearing white, a clay pot on her head, a branch of neem in one hand and a broom in the other.

This was Mother Sheetala.

Brahma granted her a boon: "As long as you hold the grains of black gram in your hand, the world shall worship you. You are the mistress of diseases — by your grace, illness is calmed; by your displeasure, it spreads."

From that same yajna, a demon was born from the sweat of Lord Shiva — Jvarasura. Brahma appointed him as Mother Sheetala's companion. Wherever she went, Jvarasura would follow.

---

Mother Sheetala and Jvarasura began to wander the earth. Wherever they went, smallpox, fever, and various skin ailments broke out. Even the gods grew distressed.

They arrived at the kingdom of King Virat. King Virat was a devoted Shiva-bhakta — steadfast and righteous.

Mother Sheetala said to him: "O King, I wish to be placed above Shiva in your kingdom."

The King folded his hands and replied: "Mother, that is not possible. In this kingdom, the supreme place belongs to Lord Shankara alone. I cannot give that position to anyone above him."

Mother Sheetala was displeased.

She unleashed Jvarasura. Seventy-five kinds of diseases spread through the entire kingdom. Children, elders, youth — all were afflicted. From the palace to the streets, cries of anguish arose.

King Virat was shaken, but he did not abandon truth.

At last, Mother Sheetala appeared herself. Her anger had softened. She said: "Virat, this was your test. You did not forsake truth. Ask — what do you seek?"

The King folded his hands: "Mother, heal my people. Their suffering is my suffering."

Mother Sheetala was pleased. She sprinkled cool water from her clay pot — and wherever those drops fell, the diseases were stilled.

King Virat began the worship of Mother Sheetala from that day, and took a vow that in his kingdom every home would observe Sheetala Saptami.

In the tradition of the Skanda Purana, it is said that the devotee who worships Mother Sheetala with devotion, offers cold food, and presents neem leaves — remains protected from smallpox, fever, and skin ailments.

## Significance

"Sheetala" — she who cools. The burning heat of fever, the scorching of pox, the fire of epidemic — Mother Sheetala is the presiding deity of all these. She holds the power both to send disease and to remove it.

Neem is Mother Sheetala's beloved tree — cooling, astringent, disease-dispelling. This is why neem branches and leaves are essential in her worship.

The most distinctive tradition of Sheetala Saptami is **not lighting the hearth**. Heat, fire, the smell of cooking — these are contrary to Mother Sheetala's nature. So on the sixth day (Shashthi — Randhan Chhath), all food is cooked ahead. On Saptami, that cold, day-old food is offered to the Goddess, and the family eats it too.

**A mother's fast — for her children:** This fast is observed especially by mothers for the health and wellbeing of their children. Mother Sheetala presides over the diseases that afflict children most — smallpox, fever, and skin ailments. A mother keeps this fast praying that Mother Sheetala show grace upon her children and protect them from these diseases. Taking cold food is the outward symbol of this prayer — just as the mother "cools" herself, so she prays that Mother Sheetala cool the fever and burning in her children.

This festival is observed primarily in Gujarat on Bhadrapada Krishna Saptami. In North India, Sheetala Ashtami (Chaitra Krishna Ashtami — Basoda) is more common. In both traditions, the Goddess and her offering are the same, and in both a mother's fast is centered on protecting her children — only the date and region differ.

## Vidhi

- **Shashthi (Randhan Chhath — prep):** Cook all food — rotis, dal, rice, vegetables, curd, kheer; this cold day-old food will be the Goddess's offering tomorrow and the family's meal as well.
- **For whom:** This fast is observed primarily by mothers for their children's health — praying that Mother Sheetala keep them free from fever, pox, and disease.
- **No fire on Saptami:** Women do not light the hearth all day — this is the most important feature of the fast; no freshly cooked food; some traditions also avoid lighting a lamp, follow household custom.
- **At dawn:** Bathe (some traditions prescribe neem-infused water); take the vow to observe Mother Sheetala's worship.
- **Puja:** Pour cold water over the idol or image of Mother Sheetala; offer fresh neem leaves and a neem branch; place the cold offering — day-old food, curd, buttermilk, sweet kheer; perform worship with turmeric, roli, and flowers.
- **Cold food:** Eat only cold, day-old food throughout the day — this is not merely tradition but the symbol of the mother's prayer for her children's protection; curd, buttermilk, neem leaves are Mother's preferred offerings.

## Mantras

**Mother Sheetala Vandana:**

> ॐ शीतले त्वं जगन्माता शीतले त्वं जगत्पिता ।
> शीतले त्वं जगद्धात्री शीतलायै नमो नमः ॥

Oṃ śītale tvaṃ jaganmātā śītale tvaṃ jagatpitā |
Śītale tvaṃ jagaddhātrī śītalāyai namo namaḥ ||

Meaning: "O Sheetala, you are the mother of the world; you are the sustainer of the world. You are the upholder of the world. To Sheetala, salutations, salutations."

---

**Mother Sheetala seed mantra:**

> ॐ ह्रीं श्रीं शीतलायै नमः

Oṃ Hrīṃ Śrīṃ Śītalāyai Namaḥ

Meaning: "Hrīṃ (the power of māyā), Śrīṃ (the power of Lakshmi) — to Mother Sheetala endowed with both these powers, salutation."
