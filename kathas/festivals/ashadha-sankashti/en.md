## Katha

The month of Ashadha — the eve of the monsoon. The heat of Jyeshtha has not yet departed, and clouds are gathering in the sky. In that long waiting period, when both mind and senses are agitated — it was precisely at that time that a demon was born. His name was **Kamasura**.

Kamasura had arisen from fierce desire. He was no ordinary demon — he was the embodied form of that force present in all of creation which pulls every being, misleads it, and draws it away from discernment. He possessed a peculiar boon: no weapon could kill him, because he himself was desire, and desire cannot be cut with a blade.

Kamasura spread his maya across all three worlds.

His illusion fell upon the gods — Indra became so lost in his apsaras that he forgot his royal duties. Varuna, Agni, Vayu — all became absorbed in their own pleasures. Decisions were not reached in assemblies; the mind wandered even while offering oblations into the sacred fire.

His shadow fell upon the sages as well. Ascetics who had practised solitary sadhana for years became restless — their ashrams grew disturbed, their minds filled with vikara.

Kamasura himself wandered about — and wherever he went, the thread of dharma grew slack.

The gods went to Brahma. Brahma said — *"No weapon can defeat this demon. The only one who can vanquish him is one who is himself untouched by any desire. Go — take refuge in Vinayaka."*

The gods came to Ganesha and offered their prayers.

Ganesha listened. And he assumed a wondrous form — that of **Vikata**. Vikata means one who is beyond the ordinary conventions of beauty. One who cares nothing for external appearance. Elephant-faced, Lambodara, vast of belly — and yet utterly serene, utterly established within himself.

Kamasura laughed upon seeing Vikata Ganesha — *"This is your protector? Who would be frightened by this form?"*

He brought his full power to bear. He unleashed upon Vikata every arrow of desire and infatuation — beautiful visions, sweet sounds, imaginations of sensory pleasures. Every weapon in his arsenal was tried.

Vikata Ganesha sat completely unmoved.

Kamasura's arrows struck him — and like water bouncing off stone, they returned. One who is established within cannot be shaken from without.

And then Vikata Ganesha did one thing — he **laughed**.

That laughter was no ordinary thing. It was the laughter of fearlessness — the laughter that knows desire, however powerful, is not permanent. There was a force in the sound of that laughter — and by that force, Kamasura's web of illusion shattered.

The delusion that had covered all three worlds dissolved in an instant. The gods remembered their dharma. The sages remembered their tapas.

Kamasura was drained of his power. His weapons fell from his hands. He bowed at Ganesha's feet.

Ganesha did not slay him. He said — *"Desire is part of creation. But desire that moves in the direction of dharma — that creates rather than deludes — that is sacred. Remain in the world in that form."*

Kamasura bowed and departed — transformed, purified.

In the tradition of the Ganesha Purana, it is said that by hearing this katha of Vikata Ganesha on Ashadha Sankashti Chaturthi, and by completing the fast after sighting the moon at moonrise, the inner disturbances and unnecessary attachments of the mind are stilled. The devotee who observes this vrata with faith has the trials of life removed.

## Significance

**Sankashti** means — the one who removes obstacles. Sankat (crisis) + harana (removal) = **Sankataharana Chaturthi**. This vrata is observed on the Krishna Chaturthi of every month, and each month a particular form of Ganesha is worshipped.

**Vikata** — this is that form of Ganesha which teaches that power lies not in external appearance but in inner stability. Vikata means extraordinary, unusual — but in this unusualness there is a deep truth: one who is beyond external standards cannot be shaken by external things.

**The context of Ashadha:** Ashadha is the time when the heat of Jyeshtha and the anticipation of the monsoon coexist — curiosity in the mind, restlessness in the senses. At this seasonal juncture, the katha of Vikata is especially relevant.

**The moonrise-parana rule:** The fast of Sankashti Chaturthi is broken only after sighting the moon. This rule is the distinctive mark of this vrata — parana without sighting the moon is not considered complete.

## Vidhi

- **Materials:** Modak or laddus; 21 blades of durva grass; red flowers; sindura; ghee lamp.
- **At dawn:** Bathe; install the image or picture of Ganesha; perform panchopachara puja — water, fragrance, flowers, incense, lamp.
- **Offerings:** Offer 21 or 3 blades of durva grass; offer modak or laddus as bhoga; hear the katha of Vikata Ganesha.
- **Fasting:** From sunrise to moonrise; abstain from grains; fruit-based food (fruits, milk, sabudana) may be taken once; no parana before sighting the moon.
- **Evening:** Await moonrise; upon sighting the moon, offer arghya with water and Ganesha's name; then break the fast.
- **Note:** Check the moonrise time for the tithi on DrikPanchang — that is the binding time for parana.

## Mantras

**Ganesha mula mantra:**

> ॐ गं गणपतये नमः

oṃ gaṃ gaṇapataye namaḥ

Meaning: O Ganapati, I bow to you.

**Vighnashana shloka:**

> वक्रतुण्ड महाकाय सूर्यकोटिसमप्रभ ।
> निर्विघ्नं कुरु मे देव सर्वकार्येषु सर्वदा ॥

vakratuṇḍa mahākāya sūryakoṭisamaprabha |
nirvighnaṃ kuru me deva sarvakāryeṣu sarvadā ||

Meaning: O Vakratunda, vast of form, radiant as a crore of suns — O Lord, remove all obstacles from my every endeavour, always.
