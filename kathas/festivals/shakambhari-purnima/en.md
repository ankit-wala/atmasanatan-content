## Katha

The Devi Bhagavata Purana tells of an age when a terrible famine gripped the earth. For a hundred years not a single drop of rain fell from the sky. Rivers ran dry, the earth cracked open, and not one grain of food could grow. Trees turned to bare stumps, animals and birds began to perish, and human beings collapsed on the ground writhing with hunger and thirst. It was a time that seemed like the great dissolution itself — the balance of nature had been shattered by the malign influence of the asuras.

The sages and seers were overcome with anguish. They gathered together and, with one voice, began to praise Adishakti Bhagavati Devi. No other hope remained to them. Day and night, without food or water, absorbed entirely in the chanting of the Goddess's name, they remained immersed in their penance.

Bhagavati heard their desperate cry.

The Devi appeared — resplendent, full of compassion, her eyes brimming with maternal tenderness for all of creation. She said to the sages: "By the evil influence of the asuras, the earth has been stripped of water and vegetation. I shall take a form that will nourish and sustain this world."

And then something beyond all reckoning took place.

From every pore of the Devi's divine body, plants began to sprout. Leafy greens, vegetables, fruits, flowers, herbs, medicinal plants — all manner of life emerged from her limbs. Her entire being became a living forest garden where every form of life was blossoming. She stood not with weapons in her hands but with vegetables and fruits — she had come not to wage war, but to nourish.

Gradually, by her grace, food reached all living creatures. The hungry were fed. The darkness of famine began to lift. Rain fell. The earth turned green again. The earth that had lain as though dead came alive and fertile once more at the touch of the Mother.

The sages prostrated themselves fully before her. Tears flowed from their eyes. They said: "Mother, you are Shakambhari — Shaka meaning vegetables and greens, Bhari meaning the one who sustains and nourishes. It is you who fills the belly of this world."

The tradition of the Devi Bhagavata Purana holds that these events unfolded from the eighth day of the bright fortnight of Paush month through the full moon — and that is why these eight days are called Shakambhari Navaratri, culminating on Shakambhari Purnima.

According to the Devi Bhagavata Purana, one who listens to or reads this katha with devotion will never face a lack of food or water in life. By the grace of Ma Shakambhari, their home remains ever full of wealth and grain, and all the creatures of the world regard them with affection.

## Significance

The very name of Shakambhari Devi declares her nature — Shaka (vegetables, greens, vegetation) + Bhari (the one who sustains and nourishes). She is that form of Adishakti Durga who saves creation not with weapons but with food. Her form is singular: she holds in her hands a lotus, fruits, and leafy greens — this is the Mother in her nurturing aspect.

Shakambhari Devi holds a special place in agrarian life. Farmers, gardeners, and those who depend on forests regard her as their kuldevi. Her worship protects the harvest, holds famine at bay, and preserves the fertility of the earth.

Her principal shrine is at Shankambhara near Sambhar in Rajasthan, where on Shakambhari Purnima lakhs of devotees come for darshan. There is also a celebrated temple in the Shiwalik foothills in Saharanpur district, Uttar Pradesh.

## Vidhi

A simple and devout procedure for Shakambhari Purnima worship:

**Worship materials:** Fresh vegetables and greens (spinach, fenugreek, coriander, bottle gourd, pumpkin), fruits, flowers, incense and lamp, roli, akshat, coconut.

**Order of worship:**
1. Bathe at dawn and wear clean clothes.
2. Install an image or picture of Devi Shakambhari in a clean place.
3. Light the lamp and incense.
4. Offer fresh vegetables, greens, and fruits to the Mother — this is her most beloved naivedya.
5. Chant oṃ śākambharyai namaḥ.
6. Read the Shakambhari katha from the Devi Bhagavata.
7. Perform aarti.
8. Distribute fresh vegetables and fruits as prasad.

Annadaan carries special importance on this day — giving a meal to a hungry person is considered direct worship of Ma Shakambhari.

## Observance

**Shakambhari Navaratri (vrat):** There is a tradition of observing an eight-day vrat from the eighth day of the bright fortnight of Paush month through to the full moon.

**Rules of the vrat:**
- Onion and garlic are forbidden.
- During the vrat days, observe a fruit-only diet (phalahar) or eat only one meal (ekabhukta).
- Fresh vegetables and fruits may be taken during the vrat period.
- Meat, alcohol, and tamasic food must be avoided.

**Purnima vrat paran:** Break the vrat after worship on the full moon day. Take sattvic food for the paran.

**Annadaan:** On Purnima, feeding the hungry and offering fresh green fodder to cows is considered deeply meritorious.

> ॐ शाकम्भर्यै नमः

oṃ śākambharyai namaḥ

Meaning: O Mother Shakambhari, I bow to you.
