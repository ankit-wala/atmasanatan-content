## Katha

The glory of the month of Magh is described in all the Puranas. Yet within this month, the new-moon day — Mauni Amavasya — holds a place apart. In the Magh Mahatmya of the Padma Purana, Maharshi Lomash narrated this katha to King Yudhishthira in the following manner.

**The Katha of Rishi Lomash**

Once, in the earliest ages of creation, Maharshi Medha and a gathering of ascetic sages arrived at Prayagraj during the month of Magh. That place where the Ganga, the Yamuna, and the invisible Saraswati converge — the Triveni Sangam. On that sacred bank the sages bathed at the Brahma-muhurta, before the rising of the sun.

After their bath, they took upon themselves a wondrous vow — mauna, silence, for the whole of that day.

No one spoke, no one conversed. The power of speech was there, yet it had been offered entirely at the feet of the Lord. Outwardly the sages were silent; inwardly they were awake — repeating the name of Lord Vishnu in their minds, sinking deeper and deeper into meditation.

This tapas of theirs was no less than a great yajna. And that day was the Magh Amavasya.

Pleased by the single-pointed sadhana of the sages, Lord Vishnu himself appeared on that bank. He said —

"This tithi — the Magh Amavasya — is the highest of all new-moon days. The devotee who on this day bathes in a sacred tirtha, observes mauna, and meditates upon me — that person receives the fruit of a thousand Ashvamedha yajnas."

A question arose in the sages' minds — why does mauna carry such great importance? Then the Lord explained: "When speech falls still, that divine voice which lies in the depths of the mind begins to be heard. Mauna is not a door that separates one from the outer world — it is the door that unites one with the inner Lord. On the Magh Amavasya this silence is especially fruitful, because on this tithi the tarpan offered for the pitru and the meditation offered to the Lord are both multiplied without limit."

This is why this Amavasya is called Mauni Amavasya.

Maharshi Lomash told King Yudhishthira that among all the days of kalpavasa at Prayagraj during Magh, this day stands supreme. On this day, millions upon millions of devotees bathe at the Sangam. During the Kumbh Mela and the Magh Mela, the bath of Mauni Amavasya is held to be the most important of all — it is known as the Amrit Snan or Shahi Snan.

**Phala-shruti**

The tradition of the Padma Purana declares that the person who, on the Magh Amavasya, bathes in a sacred river before sunrise, observes mauna through the day, worships Lord Vishnu, and performs tarpan for the pitru — all that person's sins are destroyed, the ancestors are satisfied, and the path to moksha is opened before them.

## Significance

Mauni Amavasya is the most sacred tithi of the month of Magh. Among all new-moon days, the Magh Amavasya is held to be the greatest — because at this time the sun is in Uttarayana and the energy of Magh is at its peak.

**The Spiritual Power of Mauna**

"Mauni" means one who observes mauna, silence. Mauna is not merely keeping quiet. It is a vow of speech, a concentration of the mind, and an inward-turning sadhana. Only when speech stops running outward does the mind turn inward. The experience of the Lord comes not through speech but through silence. This is why mauna on this day is regarded as the highest form of worship.

**The Special Significance of Pitru Tarpan**

The Amavasya is the tithi of the pitru. The fruit of pitru tarpan performed on the Magh Amavasya is extraordinary. Offering water mixed with sesame to the ancestors satisfies them, and they bestow blessings upon their descendants.

**The Special Importance of Prayagraj**

The bath at the Triveni Sangam of Ganga, Yamuna, and Saraswati on Mauni Amavasya is considered the highest of all. This is the most sacred bathing day of both the Kumbh Mela and the Magh Mela. According to the Padma Purana and other scriptures, bathing at Prayagraj on this day yields the fruit of a thousand Ashvamedha yajnas.

## Vidhi

- **At dawn:** Rise at the Brahma-muhurta, before sunrise, and bathe in a sacred river (Ganga, Yamuna, Narmada, Godavari) if possible; when bathing at home, add sesame seeds to the water and hold the Ganga in mind.
- **Mauna vow:** After bathing, take the vow of mauna — observe silence from sunrise to sunset or for as long as possible; if speech is unavoidable, speak only what is strictly necessary; use this time for japa of *Om Namo Bhagavate Vasudevaya* and meditation.
- **Fasting:** Observe a fruit-only fast or eat sattvic light food; do not consume tamasic food — meat, alcohol, garlic, or onion.
- **Vishnu puja:** Light a lamp before the image of Lord Vishnu; offer tulsi leaves, yellow flowers, and panchamrita; recite *Om Namo Bhagavate Vasudevaya*.
- **Pitru tarpan:** Facing south, offer tarpan to the ancestors with water mixed with sesame — repeat the tarpan mantra and offer water three times; this may be performed on a riverbank or at home; remember the pitru and give dana in their name.
- **Dana:** Give sesame, blankets, grain, and clothing to brahmanas and those in need — dana given during Magh is especially meritorious.
- **Conduct:** Avoid anger, speaking ill of others, untruth, and argument — mauna means not only silence of speech but also peace of mind; the auspicious window runs from sunrise to sunset.
- **Special merit:** Bathing at Prayagraj (Triveni Sangam), Haridwar, Kashi, or Nashik on this day carries the highest merit — this is the Amrit Snan of the Kumbh and Magh Mela.

## Mantras

**Mantra of Lord Vishnu**

> ॐ नमो भगवते वासुदेवाय

oṃ namo bhagavate vāsudevāya

Meaning: O Lord Vasudeva, I bow to you.

**Pitru Tarpan Mantra**

> ॐ पितृभ्यः स्वधायिभ्यः स्वधा नमः ।
> पितामहेभ्यः स्वधायिभ्यः स्वधा नमः ।
> प्रपितामहेभ्यः स्वधायिभ्यः स्वधा नमः ॥

prapitāmahebhyaḥ svadhāyibhyaḥ svadhā namaḥ ||

Meaning: Salutations with the offering of svadha to the father, the grandfather (pitamaha), and the great-grandfather (prapitamaha) — to the ancestors of all three generations.
