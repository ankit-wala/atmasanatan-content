## Katha

In all creation, Surya is the most immediate of deities — seen every single day, the foundation of all life. Yet even the gods are not beyond the laws of this world; estrangement can come between father and son in the houses of the divine just as it does in the houses of men.

**The Tale from the Bhavishya Purana — The Meeting of Surya and Shani**

Suryadev had two wives — Sanjna and Chhaya. Shanidev was born of Chhaya's womb. From childhood, Shani's heart was not drawn toward the world but toward the worship of Shiva. He would perform tapas in solitude, sit in deep meditation, and remain absorbed in devotion to Mahadev.

Suryadev was not pleased by this. He was the deity of radiance and energy, and he expected his son to remain active in the world with that same brilliance. The distance between father and son grew.

One day Suryadev resolved to visit Shani's dwelling. Shani's rashi is Makar — a deep, cool, and shadowed place, utterly unlike Surya's luminous nature. As Suryadev entered Makar, his radiance lit up Shani's home.

Shanidev welcomed his father with complete reverence. He stood before him with humility. His father had come — after so many years — and Shanidev's heart overflowed. And Suryadev, when he saw his son's devotion and spirit of service, felt his own heart soften.

The estrangement between father and son dissolved in that very moment.

Suryadev blessed Shani: "Those devotees who worship us both on this day — the day I enter your dwelling — shall receive wealth, prosperity, and relief from the suffering Shani brings." This is why both Surya and Shani are worshipped on Makar Sankranti.

**The Tale from the Mahabharata — Bhishma Pitamah's Mahaprayan**

The war at Kurukshetra was over. The Pandavas had been victorious. But Bhishma Pitamah — who had been granted the boon of iccha-mrityu by his father Shantanu — was still alive. He lay on his shara-shaiya, on a bed of arrows, tormented by deep wounds.

Everyone asked — Pitamah, why do you not leave the body?

Bhishma Pitamah answered in a calm voice: "I am waiting. Those who depart during Dakshinayan are reborn. Departing during Uttarayan brings moksha. Suryadev is still on his southward journey. When he enters Makar — when Uttarayan begins — then I will make my journey."

And so he waited.

In the month of Paush, when Suryadev entered Makar Rashi, when the first day of Uttarayan arrived — Bhishma Pitamah gathered all his yoga and fixed it in Suryadev. He turned his gaze toward the sun and peacefully departed from the body.

He attained moksha.

The tradition of the Bhavishya Purana and the Mahabharata holds that this day of Makar Sankranti is not merely a festival — it is the threshold moment when the gates of heaven open, when a father and son's estrangement ends, and when a great yogi chooses liberation of his own will.

**Phala-shruti**

The Bhavishya Purana declares: one who worships Suryadev and Shanidev together on the day of Makar Sankranti receives wealth, good health, and relief from the afflictions of Shani's sade-sati and dhaiya. According to the tradition of the Mahabharata: one who bathes in water mixed with til on this day, offers arghya to Suryadev, and remembers the resolve of Bhishma Pitamah — all their sins are destroyed and they attain the highest state in the end.

## Significance

Makar Sankranti is the only Hindu festival based on solar reckoning — not on a tithi. It is the day Suryadev enters Makar Rashi and Uttarayan begins — the sun's northward journey. The days grow longer, the cold eases, and a fresh awakening stirs through nature.

Spiritually, Uttarayan is the day of the gods. In the Gita, Bhagwan Shri Krishna declares that those who depart during Uttarayan attain Brahman. Bhishma Pitamah proved this truth through his own life.

This festival is celebrated across India by many names — as **Pongal** in Tamil Nadu, as **Lohri** in Punjab, as **Uttarayan** in Gujarat, and as **Khichdi** in Uttar Pradesh. It is a festival of unity within diversity.

Til-gud holds special significance. Til is Shani's symbol — dark and deep; jaggery is the symbol of Surya's warmth — sweet and golden. Eating til-gud together and offering it as dana is a remembrance of that father-and-son reunion which makes Makar Sankranti sacred.

## Vidhi

- **At dawn:** Rise before sunrise and bathe; if possible, bathe in the Ganga, Yamuna, or any sacred river — when bathing at home, add til to the water.
- **Surya arghya:** Take water in a copper vessel; mix in til, red flowers, and jaggery; stand facing east and offer arghya to Suryadev while reciting oṃ sūryāya namaḥ.
- **Til-gud bhog and dana:** Prepare laddus of til and jaggery; distribute within the family and offer as dana to brahmins and the poor; donating til oil, til khichdi, blankets, and warm clothing is especially meritorious.
- **Shanidev worship:** Go to a Shani temple; light a lamp of mustard oil; offer black til; chant oṃ śaṃ śanaiścarāya namaḥ.
- **Pitru tarpan:** Offer tarpan to the ancestors on this day with water mixed with til.
- **Fasting:** This is a festival, not a strict vrat — fasting is not required; eat sattvic food and avoid tamasic food (meat, alcohol).
- **Regional observance:** In Uttar Pradesh and Bihar, khichdi (rice and urad dal) is eaten and donated — giving rise to the name "Khichdi"; in Gujarat and Rajasthan, kite-flying marks the celebration of Uttarayan.
- **Timing:** Bathing and dana after sunset are forbidden — the Sankranti punya-kala ends at sunset.

## Mantras

**Mantra for Suryadev**

> ॐ सूर्याय नमः

oṃ sūryāya namaḥ

Meaning: O Suryadev, I offer you my salutations.
