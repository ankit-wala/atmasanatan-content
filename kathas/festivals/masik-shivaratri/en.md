## Katha

In the tradition of the Skanda Purana, this katha of Masik Shivaratri is well known.

Long ago in a forested region, there lived a hunter named Suswara — one who fed his family by hunting in the forests. He gave little thought to dharma or adharma; life was life, and hunting was hunting.

One day, in search of game, he went very deep into the jungle. The day faded. He lost his way. All around was thick darkness and the sounds of wild animals. That night was the night of Krishna Chaturdashi — the night of Masik Shivaratri.

Suswara thought — sleeping on the ground is dangerous. He grasped a branch of a nearby bel tree (bilva tree) and climbed up. He would have to stay awake through the night — to stay safe from the animals.

So that sleep would not come upon him, he kept plucking bel leaves and dropping them below, one after another, all through the night.

He did not know that at the root of that bel tree there was a Shivalinga — covered in earth, hidden from sight. As he dropped the leaves, they kept falling onto that Shivalinga. Bilva leaves — which are most beloved to Shiva — kept being offered all through the night.

Suswara had eaten nothing since morning — unknowingly he had fasted. He had stayed awake all night — unknowingly he had kept vigil. And he had kept offering bel leaves — unknowingly he had worshipped Shiva.

All three — fasting, vigil, offering of bel — had been fulfilled.

At dawn he descended from the tree and returned home. Life went on as before.

Years later, when Suswara died, the messengers of Yama came. Alongside them came the ganas of Shiva — radiant, majestic, arriving from the direction of Kailash. For a moment there was a dispute between the two.

Shiva's ganas said — *"He is ours."*

The messengers of Yama were startled — *"But this is a hunter. What merit did he perform?"*

The ganas said — *"On the night of Krishna Chaturdashi, he stayed awake all night. He offered bel upon the Shivalinga. He unknowingly fasted. Shiva's Shivaratri was fulfilled — whether done knowingly or not."*

Suswara went to Kailash.

The essence of this katha is this: for Shiva's grace, no philosophy is required. What is required is — to stay awake through that night, to turn toward that linga, to let that bilva leaf fall.

And Masik Shivaratri comes again on every Krishna Chaturdashi — every month an opportunity to perform that same puja consciously.

In the tradition of the Skanda Purana, it is said that the person who observes Masik Shivaratri with devotion — all their sins are diminished and they attain Shiva-sayujya.

## Significance

The word Shivaratri means — the night of Shiva. Every month on the Chaturdashi of Krishna Paksha is called Masik Shivaratri. This tithi comes twelve times in a year — and once in the month of Phalguna in the form of Mahashivaratri, which is the most important.

But Masik Shivaratri is not merely the shadow of Mahashivaratri. It is a complete vrat in its own right.

The Chaturdashi tithi is most dear to Shiva. On this tithi the night holds special importance — that is why the vrat and the puja take place at night. Shiva is the deity of the night — the lord of the moment of dissolution, who transforms even darkness into light.

Bel-patra (bilva leaves) is most beloved to Shiva. Three leaves together — tridala bel — are the symbol of the three devas, or the dissolution of the three gunas — sattva, rajas, and tamas — into Shiva. The offering of bel alone is enough — Shiva is pleased by it.

The deeper meaning of the Vyadha-Katha is that Shiva's devotion demands no external qualification. Caste, learning, wealth — nothing is required. What is required is — to stay awake through that night, to turn toward Shiva.

## Vidhi

- **Fasting:** Complete fast is best; those who cannot may eat once (ekahara) — without salt, without grain; fruit and milk suffice; grain (rice, wheat) is prohibited on this day.
- **At dawn:** Rise before sunrise and bathe; take the sankalpa — *"Today on Krishna Chaturdashi, I observe the vrat of Masik Shivaratri"*; go to a Shiva temple or light a lamp before the Shivalinga at home.
- **Evening puja:** Pour water over the Shivalinga in abhishek while chanting *Om Namah Shivaya*; perform Panchamrita abhishek — milk, curd, honey, ghee, sugar.
- **Offerings:** Offer bilva leaves — three-leafed (tridala), unbroken, clean; offer datura flowers, aak flowers, white flowers; light incense and a lamp.
- **Chanting:** Chant *Om Namah Shivaya* 108 times; recite the Mahamrityunjaya Mantra.
- **Jagaran (vigil):** Keeping vigil through the night holds special importance; remember Shiva during the four prahars; keep vigil at least until midnight; prasad is fruit, milk, panchamrita.
- **What to avoid:** Anger, falsehood, meat, and intoxicants are prohibited on this day.
- **Paran (breaking fast):** The following morning — on Krishna Paksha Pratipada or Dvitiya — bathe, remember Shiva, then break the fast.
- **Regularity:** Greater fruit comes from observing Masik Shivaratri every month; beginning in Shravan with a sankalpa for all twelve vrats of the year is considered auspicious.

## Mantras

**Panchakshari Mantra:**

> ॐ नमः शिवाय

oṃ namaḥ śivāya

Meaning: Salutations to Shiva. This five-syllable mantra — Na, Mah, Shi, Va, Ya — is the symbol of the five elements and is the simplest, deepest form of worship of Shiva.

---

**Mahamrityunjaya Mantra:**

> ॐ त्र्यम्बकं यजामहे सुगन्धिं पुष्टिवर्धनम् ।
> उर्वारुकमिव बन्धनान् मृत्योर्मुक्षीय माऽमृतात् ॥

oṃ tryambakaṃ yajāmahe sugandhiṃ puṣṭivardhanam | urvārukamiva bandhanān mṛtyormukṣīya mā'mṛtāt ||

Meaning: We worship Shiva, the three-eyed one, who is fragrant and who nourishes life. As a ripe cucumber is freed from the vine, so may we be freed from death — not from immortality.
