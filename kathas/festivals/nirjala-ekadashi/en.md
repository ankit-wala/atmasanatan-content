## Katha

This is a story from the age of the Mahabharata. The five Pandavas and Draupadi were in exile in the forest. Yudhishthira, Arjuna, Nakula, Sahadeva, and Draupadi — all of them kept every Ekadashi of the year with full observance. Fasting from food, vigil through the night, remembrance of Lord Vishnu — all performed with discipline and devotion.

But Bhima's situation was singular. In his belly burned a fire called Vrika — which is why he was known as Vrikodara (the wolf-bellied one). Hunger was as necessary to him as breath. Every time an Ekadashi arrived, Bhima would watch his brothers fast and feel a quiet anguish rise within him. He would attempt the fast, but without food his body began to collapse, as though life itself were departing.

This torment never left him. "My brothers earn the merit of Ekadashi. Draupadi too keeps the fast. Will I always be deprived of this blessing? Will this limitation of my body stand as a wall between me and the path of liberation?"

One day Bhima went to the sage Vedavyasa. Vedavyasa was the family's grandfather-figure — the knower of all dharma, the seer of past, present, and future. Bhima touched his feet and said with humility —

"Pitamaha, you are the all-knowing master of dharma. My brothers and Draupadi observe all the Ekadashis of the year — all twenty-four — with full rites. I cannot. My body does not survive without food and water. Is there a single fast through which I may receive the merit of all the Ekadashis?"

Vedavyasa looked into Bhima's eyes and saw the true anguish there — this was no laziness, this was the grief of a devotee. He smiled and said —

"Dear Bhima, I shall tell you of the one Ekadashi whose merit equals all the Ekadashis of the year combined. It is the Ekadashi of the bright fortnight of Jyeshtha month — Nirjala Ekadashi. But its condition is severe. It must be kept utterly without water — from sunset on Dashami until the Parana on Dvadashi, neither food nor water, not even a single drop. Full night vigil. If you can do this, the entire merit of all twenty-four Ekadashis shall be yours."

Bhima accepted without hesitation.

When that Jyeshtha arrived, Bhima sat down to fast. From early morning the scorching loo winds of summer began to blow. The Jyeshtha midday — when the earth bakes, when even the shade grows tired. Bhima's body was like iron, yet by noon his throat had gone dry. His lips cracked. The belly in which fire had always raged now burned even more fiercely with the heat of thirst. Bhima swayed.

In that moment Bhima closed his eyes and called out to Lord Vishnu — not with words, not with a mantra, but with that anguish which is born only from love.

And then Lord Vishnu himself appeared.

He looked at Bhima — on the harshest day of the year, without a single drop of water, standing by the force of devotion alone. The Lord touched his Sudarshana Chakra to Bhima's chest. In that instant Bhima's thirst vanished, his exhaustion dissolved. His body filled as if with divine power.

The Lord said — "Bhima, what even the gods find difficult to achieve, you have accomplished in an ordinary human body. You are blessed."

This is why this Ekadashi is also called Bhimaseni Ekadashi and Pandava Nirjala Ekadashi.

## Significance

Nirjala Ekadashi is regarded as the greatest of all Ekadashis — not merely because its merit equals all twenty-four Ekadashis, but also because it falls in the most extreme season of the year.

Jyeshtha month — the month in the Hindu Panchang when summer is at its peak. The fierce sun, the searing winds, the preciousness of water — it is in this very context that the depth of a waterless fast becomes clear. When water is life itself, to give up water is the most severe of all renunciations. It is at once a trial of physical austerity and total surrender of the soul.

Bhima's katha reveals another truth — devotion is greater than the body's limitations. Bhima's body was incapable without food, yet his faith made him do what lay beyond the reach of his flesh. This katha brings solace to every devotee who cannot, for whatever reason, keep all the fasts — one waterless Nirjala Ekadashi opens a door for them.

From a spiritual standpoint, water donation (jal-dan) holds special significance on this day. Giving water to the thirsty, placing vessels of cold water in public spaces for the weary, donating an earthen pot — these are all counted as righteous acts of this fast. One who gives up water for himself and yet offers water to others — their merit is doubled.

## Vidhi

**Preparation (Dashami):**
On Dashami, eat sattvic food. After sunset, give up all food and water. Before sleeping, remember Lord Vishnu and take a firm resolve (sankalpa).

**Ekadashi Morning:**
Bathe before sunrise. Wear clean yellow or white clothes. Purify the place of worship with Ganga water. Install an image or picture of Lord Vishnu.

**Puja Procedure:**
- Offer Tulsi leaves, yellow flowers (yellow lotus or marigold), and sandalwood.
- Perform abhisheka with Panchamrita — milk, curd, ghee, honey, sugar.
- Light incense and a lamp (dhoop-deep).
- Chant the Vishnu Sahasranama or the japa of ॐ नमो भगवते वासुदेवाय.
- Offer Tulsi manjari, yellow fruits, and mishri to the Lord.
- Read or listen to the Nirjala Ekadashi katha.

**Water Donation (Jal-Dan):**
On this day, fill an earthen pot with water, mix in sugar and jaggery, cover it with white cloth, and offer it in donation to a Brahmin or a person in need. This is the special righteous act of Nirjala Ekadashi.

**Night Vigil:**
Keep vigil through the night with bhajans and kirtan of Lord Vishnu.

## Observance

**Waterless Fast:**
This fast is completely without water — from sunset on Dashami until the Parana time on Dvadashi, neither food, nor water, nor fruit, nor milk. Not a single drop is permitted. This is the fundamental condition of this fast — if water must be taken, the fast is considered broken.

**Prohibited:**
- Any liquid including water — milk, tea, fruit juice, buttermilk
- All food — grains, fruits, and roots
- Breaking of brahmacharya, anger, and false speech
- Sleeping during the day

**Water Donation (Special Merit):**
One who does not drink water himself and yet offers cool water to others — their merit is considered greater than the fast itself. Donating earthen pots (matkas), setting up water kiosks (pyau), distributing cool water — these are the highest acts of this day.

**Parana (Breaking the Fast):**
On Dvadashi, break the fast after sunrise during the Parana time (after the Hari-Vasar period ends in the morning). First take Tulsi water, then take Panchamrita. After that, eat sattvic food. At the time of Parana, feed a Brahmin or someone in need — this is an essential part of this fast.

**For Those Who Cannot Fast:**
Those who are ill, elderly, or pregnant women may take fruit-food (phalahar) with water — but the giving up of grains must be observed. Remembrance of Lord Vishnu and listening to the katha are sufficient to earn merit for them.

## Mantras

**1. Dvadasakshara Mantra**

> ॐ नमो भगवते वासुदेवाय

oṃ namo bhagavate vāsudevāya

Meaning: O Lord Vasudeva, I bow to you. This twelve-syllable mantra is regarded as especially fruitful on the day of Nirjala Ekadashi — while chanting, hold the remembrance of Vishnu in every syllable.

---

**2. Ekadashi Mahatmya Shloka**

> यानि कानि च पापानि ब्रह्महत्यासमानि च।
> तानि सर्वाणि नश्यन्ति प्रदोषे फाल्गुनावुभौ॥

yāni kāni ca pāpāni brahmahatyāsamāni ca
tāni sarvāṇi naśyanti pradoṣe phālgunāvubhau

Meaning: In the tradition of the Brahma Vaivarta Purana it is said that through the power of the Ekadashi fast, whatever sins there may be — even those equal in weight to the slaying of a Brahmin (brahmahatya) — are all destroyed. (This verse is originally in reference to the two Ekadashis of Phalguna month, but in tradition it is cited widely as a verse on the greatness of Ekadashi in general.) On Nirjala Ekadashi it is recited to invoke the remembrance of the glory of all Ekadashis.
