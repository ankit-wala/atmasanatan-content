## Katha

Centuries ago, on the soil of Punjab, there lived a hero whose name still echoes around every Lohri fire — Dulla Bhatti.

Dulla Bhatti's full name was Abdullah Bhatti. He belonged to the Bhatti Rajput clan. During the reign of Emperor Akbar, when the weight of the Mughal empire pressed heavily upon Punjab, Dulla Bhatti refused to bow before that power. Living in the forests and wilds of Punjab, he waged a relentless struggle against Mughal rule — a rebel chieftain, a fearless warrior.

The most beloved tale of him in folk tradition is this: in those days, certain Mughal traders would forcibly seize unmarried girls from Punjab's villages and sell them in slave markets. It is said that two young women named Sundari and Mundari became victims of just such an abduction. When news of this reached Dulla Bhatti, he did not hesitate for a moment.

Dulla Bhatti pursued those traders, freed Sundari and Mundari from their clutches, and brought them back safely. But he did not stop there. He knew that merely returning them home was not enough — the honour of those daughters in society had to be restored as well. Dulla Bhatti himself arranged their weddings. Like a father, he found worthy grooms for them, performed the marriage ceremony, and even gave gifts from his own pocket as a bridal offering.

In the folk tradition it is said that on that night when the weddings took place, there was no grand pomp, no elaborate preparation. Dulla Bhatti spread a lion's hide, completed the marriage rites with jaggery and dry fruits. This was love — the love of a warrior, a protector.

From that time on, every Lohri night in Punjab, people gather around the fire and sing this folk song —

> Sundar muṇḍariye ho! Terā kaun vicārā ho!
> Dullā Bhaṭṭī vālā ho! Dulle dhī viāī ho!

The story of Dulla Bhatti is not merely the tale of a warrior's valour — it is the story of Punjab's very soul, which can stand against an empire for the sake of its daughters.

## Significance

Lohri is celebrated on the eve of Makar Sankranti — the night when the sun is about to turn northward on its course. After the biting cold of the month of Pausha, this festival marks the beginning of spring's arrival.

**Worship of Agni Dev:** The Lohri bonfire is not merely a blaze — it is the living presence of Agni, the fire god. Farmers offer the first portion of their harvest — jaggery, sesame, rewri, peanuts, sugarcane — into the fire. This is an act of gratitude: O Agni, you gave us warmth in the cold; O Surya, you ripened the fields.

**The Sun's Northward Turn:** The day after Lohri is Makar Sankranti. Surya Dev enters Uttarayana — the very moment when days begin to grow longer and nights shorter. The Lohri fire is a welcoming song for that arrival.

**A festival for the newly-wed and the newborn:** In Punjab's tradition, Lohri holds special importance for households in which a new bride has arrived or a son has been born that year. The entire community comes together to bless them — this festival connects the individual to the family, the family to society, and society to nature.

**The legacy of Dulla Bhatti:** The memory of Dulla Bhatti woven into Lohri's folk songs carries this message — just as he restored honour to Punjab's daughters, so too does Agni Dev bring light into darkness and warmth into cold. Protection and gratitude — this is the essence of Lohri.

## Vidhi

- **Place and time:** Observed on the eve of Makar Sankranti; after sunset, light a bonfire in an open courtyard, rooftop, or open ground.
- **Setting up the bonfire:** Arrange logs and upale (dried dung cakes) in a pyramid shape; place sesame seeds and jaggery at the centre; the eldest member offers the first āhuti; all family members light the bonfire together.
- **Āhuti:** Offer sesame seeds and jaggery (energy and sweetness); rewri and gajak (winter confections); peanuts (symbol of the harvest); popcorn/lāī/khīl (lightness and joy); sugarcane (wish for a full life) — while offering, hold Agni Dev and Surya Dev in heart with the spirit *Idaṃ na mama* — this is not mine, it is offered to you.
- **Parikramā and singing:** Circumambulate the bonfire clockwise; with each round offer into the fire and sing the traditional Lohri folk song — *Sundar muṇḍariye ho! Dullā Bhaṭṭī vālā ho!* — in remembrance of Dulla Bhatti and in welcome of Agni and Surya.
- **Newly-wed and newborn:** Seat any new bride or newborn child in a place of honour near the bonfire; elders bless them with sesame-jaggery and sweets.
- **Prasād:** After fire worship, distribute rewri, gajak, peanuts, and sesame-jaggery laddus as prasād among everyone present.
- **Community:** Lohri is a communal celebration — invite neighbours, friends, and relatives to gather around the same bonfire.
- **The next day:** The day after Lohri is Makar Sankranti — observe the tradition of sesame-donation and holy bathing.
- **2026 date:** 13 January 2026 (eve of Makar Sankranti)

## Mantras

**1. Agni Stuti**

> अग्निमीळे पुरोहितं यज्ञस्य देवमृत्विजम्।
> होतारं रत्नधातमम्॥

agnimīḷe purohitaṃ yajñasya devam ṛtvijam | hotāraṃ ratnadhātamam ||

Meaning: I praise Agni Dev, who is the purohita of the yajña, the ṛtvij of the gods, and the hotā who bestows all precious wealth.

---

**2. Prayer for the Sun's Uttarayana**

> उद्वयं तमसस्परि ज्योतिष्पश्यन्त उत्तरम्।
> देवं देवत्रा सूर्यमगन्म ज्योतिरुत्तमम्॥

udvayaṃ tamasaspari jyotiṣpaśyanta uttaram | devaṃ devatra sūryam aganma jyotiḥ uttamam ||

Meaning: Rising beyond darkness, we behold that supreme light — we have reached the divine Surya, the highest radiance among the gods.

---

In the folk tradition of Lohri, it is said that the family which devotedly circumambulates the fire, makes offerings, and sings the songs of Dulla Bhatti will know warmth, abundance, and mutual love throughout the year.
