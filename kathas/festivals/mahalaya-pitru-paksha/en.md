## Katha

Karna — the great donor of the Mahabharata.

He was the son of the Sun god — but was raised in the home of the charioteer Adhiratha. Kunti had set him adrift on the Ganga at birth. He never knew who his ancestors were — who his mother was, which lineage he belonged to.

But this ignorance left a single gap in his life.

---

Karna gave in charity throughout his life. Countless times, countless petitioners.

Whoever came to him to ask — gold, jewels, land, weapons — he never turned anyone away. Even when Indra came to his door disguised as an old brahmin and asked for his kavacha-kundala, Karna tore it from his own body and gave it away.

But one act he could not perform — pitru-tarpan.

Offering water and food in the name of his ancestors. Performing shraddha.

Because he did not know who his ancestors were.

---

After the great war of Kurukshetra, Karna attained the warrior's death.

He was welcomed in Svargaloka. Indra presented him the fruits of his countless donations.

But when the time came for food — Karna found gold before him. Gold in place of water. Gold in place of grain. Gold in place of fruit.

He was hungry.

He asked Indra — "O Lord of the gods, where is my food?"

Indra replied — "Karna, what you donated throughout your life — this is it. Gold and jewels. But you never gave food and water in the name of your ancestors. What is received here is only what was given."

Karna fell silent.

Then he said — "My Lord, this was not my fault — it was my ignorance. I never knew who my ancestors were. Give me one chance."

Indra reflected.

He said — "You are given sixteen days. Go to earth. Give water and grain in the name of your ancestors. Perform shraddha."

---

During those sixteen days, Karna came to earth.

He offered tarpan to his ancestors — water with sesame, food with devotion. Those brahmins who came, he fed. Those who were hungry, he satisfied.

Those sixteen days — from Bhadrapada Purnima to Ashwin Krishna Amavasya — became **Pitru Paksha**.

And that Amavasya — on which Karna's tarpan-time was completed — is called **Mahalaya Amavasya**.

In the tradition of the Mahabharata it is said that whoever performs shraddha for their ancestors during Pitru Paksha, offers tarpan, and on Mahalaya Amavasya gives water in the name of all ancestors — their pitru are satisfied and the burden of pitru-rin is lifted.

## Significance

**Pitru-rin — one of the three debts:**
In the Hindu tradition, every person born carries three debts — dev-rin (debt to the gods), rishi-rin (debt to the sages), and pitru-rin (debt to the ancestors). Dev-rin is repaid through yajna. Rishi-rin through learning and service to the guru. Pitru-rin — through shraddha and tarpan.

**Pitru Paksha — sixteen tithis:**
The sixteen days from Bhadrapada Purnima to Ashwin Krishna Amavasya. Each day corresponds to one tithi. The shraddha for a relative is performed on the tithi of their death. For those whose tithi is unknown, or for all ancestors together — **Mahalaya Amavasya** is the day.

**Sarva Pitru Amavasya:**
This day is called "Sarva Pitru" because it belongs to all ancestors — those whose tithi is known and those whose is not. It is the most important day of Pitru Paksha.

**Mahalaya in Bengal:**
In the regional tradition of Bengal, Mahalaya carries a special meaning — it is the signal of Navratri's arrival. At dawn on Mahalaya, the "Mahishasura Mardini" stotra is recited and Ma Durga is invoked.

**Crow, cow, dog:**
In shraddha, food is set aside for all three. Tradition holds that ancestors come in the form of the crow to accept the offering.

## Vidhi

- **At dawn:** Bathe and wear white or clean dhoti before beginning any rite.
- **Tarpan vessel:** Fill a copper vessel with Gangajal or clean water; add black sesame (kala til), kusha grass, and barley.
- **Tarpan posture:** Stand facing south; saying the ancestor's name, gotra, and relationship, slowly pour the water from the ring finger and thumb — offer three times.
- **Shraddha meal:** Feed brahmins according to one's capacity; set aside food for crow, cow, and dog; give anna-dan to the poor and needy.
- **Crow feeding daily:** Throughout Pitru Paksha, before the family eats each day, set aside a portion for the crow — tradition holds that ancestors come in the crow's form to accept it.
- **Diet during Pitru Paksha:** Meat, liquor, tamasic food, garlic, and onion are forbidden; eat sattvic food — dal, rice, khichdi, sesame-based preparations.
- **Restrictions:** Auspicious events — marriage, griha-pravesh, new ventures — are avoided for the entire sixteen-day period.
