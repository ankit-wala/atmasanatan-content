## Katha

The great king Mandata had ruled his people well for many years. He was just, generous, and devoted. Yet a time came when the rains stopped. Harvests failed. Rivers ran low. People went hungry and sick. Temples fell quiet. Even the king's most earnest prayers seemed to go unanswered.

Not knowing what had gone wrong, Mandata traveled to the ashrama of Sage Angiras. He sat at the sage's feet and spoke honestly: "Guruji, my people suffer. I have tried every remedy I know. What sin has brought this darkness upon our land? How do we find the way out?"

Angiras sat with the king's question for a while. Then he said: "Your questions have a single answer. On the Ekadashi of Ashadha Shukla paksha, keep the fast and worship Lord Vishnu with a full heart. This Ekadashi is called Devshayani — the day on which the Lord himself enters his deep rest. Before you fast, hear why this day carries such weight."

The sage then told the king a story from an ancient time.

Long ago, a demon named Shankhasura rose to great power through years of fierce tapasya. The boon he received made him nearly impossible to defeat. Drunk on this strength, he drove the devas out of their heavens and filled the three worlds with fear and disorder. The gods fled to Vaikuntha and stood before Vishnu, who lay at ease on Sheshanaga in the Ocean of Milk. They cried out: "Lord, the worlds cannot carry this anymore. There is no one else to turn to."

Vishnu heard them. He took up his Sudarshan Chakra (the spinning disc of light) and his Kaumodaki mace, and went to battle against Shankhasura. The fight was long and terrible — it shook the earth and stirred the sea. But in the end, Vishnu destroyed the demon and the three worlds breathed again.

When the battle was done, the gods returned to their heavens. Vishnu returned to the Ocean of Milk — his eternal abode. He lay upon Sheshanaga, closed his divine eyes, and entered yoganidra — the deep, cosmic stillness that is not born of exhaustion but is part of his own eternal play. That day was Ashadha Shukla Ekadashi.

For four full months, through Shravan, Bhadrapada, Ashwin, and Kartik, the Lord would rest. He would wake again only on Devutthana Ekadashi in Kartik — but that is another story.

"These four months are called Chaturmas," Angiras told the king. "While the Lord rests, wandering sadhus stop their travels and stay in one place — studying, fasting, serving. Householders too live more simply and quietly. The one who keeps Devshayani Ekadashi with sincerity, and carries its spirit through all of Chaturmas, finds old burdens falling away."

King Mandata returned home. He kept the fast on Ashadha Shukla Ekadashi with his whole court. Tradition holds that rains came to Mandata's kingdom that very Ashadha.

Angiras had told the king before he left: "The merit of this one Ekadashi equals the merit of a thousand yagnas. Sins carried across many births fall away on the one who observes it with full sincerity. And the one who carries the spirit of Chaturmas through all four months — observing restraint, doing service, staying near scripture — will find the grace of Vishnu steady through the year ahead."

## Significance

Devshayani Ekadashi is one of the most important Ekadashis of the year. It opens Chaturmas — the four sacred months when the pace of life slows, the inner life deepens, and the tradition calls devotees toward simplicity.

Vishnu's yoganidra is not mere sleep. It is a cosmic settling — as if the universe itself pauses to breathe. During these four months, no new auspicious events are started: no weddings, no thread ceremonies, no house-warmings. Wandering ascetics observe *shibir* — they stop moving and stay in one place, making it a period of intensive spiritual practice.

At a personal level, Chaturmas is an invitation to slow down. Less travel, simpler food, more japa. The fast of Devshayani Ekadashi is the first step toward that simplicity.

Vishnu wakes from his rest on Devutthana Ekadashi (also called Prabodhini Ekadashi) in Kartik. The day he wakes, the auspicious activities of the year resume — which is why so many weddings happen in Kartik month.

## Vidhi

- **Day before (Dashami):** Eat sattvik food in the evening — avoid non-veg, onion, garlic, and excess; sleep early.
- **At dawn (Ekadashi):** Wake before sunrise; bathe with clean water; wear clean clothes.
- **Puja setup:** Set up the puja space with an image or idol of Vishnu or Narayan; if you have a shayana (resting) image, this is the perfect occasion for it.
- **Offerings:** Offer fresh flowers, tulsi leaves (especially dear to Vishnu — do not skip), and a ghee diya; perform abhishek with panchamrit or clean water while chanting Vishnu's names.
- **Fasting:** Observe the fast through the day; grain, rice, wheat, dals, and legumes are forbidden; onion, garlic, leafy greens, and eggplant are also avoided in many traditions; fruit, milk, sabudana (sago), singhare ki roti, and sendha namak are allowed.
- **Evening prayer:** Sing Vishnu bhajans or recite Vishnu Sahasranama in the evening; stay awake in prayer if possible, especially into the night.
- **Breaking the fast (Dwadashi parana):** Break the fast after sunrise but before the Dwadashi tithi ends — always verify the exact parana window on DrikPanchang; offer food to a Brahmin or any hungry person before eating yourself.
- **Chaturmas sankalpa:** Many devotees use this day to take a four-month commitment — giving up a particular food, waking earlier, doing extra japa, or reading one scripture chapter daily; these commitments are released on Devutthana Ekadashi in Kartik.

## Mantras

**Vishnu shayana prayer — recited while visualizing the Lord at rest:**

> शान्ताकारं भुजगशयनं पद्मनाभं सुरेशम् ।
> विश्वाधारं गगनसदृशं मेघवर्णं शुभाङ्गम् ।
> लक्ष्मीकान्तं कमलनयनं योगिभिर्ध्यानगम्यम् ।
> वन्दे विष्णुं भवभयहरं सर्वलोकैकनाथम् ॥

*Śāntākāraṃ bhujagaśayanaṃ padmanābhaṃ sureśam |
Viśvādhāraṃ gaganasadṛśaṃ meghavarṇaṃ śubhāṅgam |
Lakṣmīkāntaṃ kamalanayanaṃ yogibhir dhyānagamyam |
Vande Viṣṇuṃ bhavabhayaharaṃ sarvalokaikānātham ||*

Meaning: "I bow to Vishnu — calm in form, resting on the great serpent, lotus-navel, lord of gods, support of the universe, vast as the sky, dark as a rain cloud, beloved of Lakshmi, lotus-eyed, reached through meditation — the one Lord of all worlds, who takes away the fear of rebirth."

---

**Mool mantra for Ekadashi japa:**

> ॐ नमो नारायणाय

*Oṃ Namo Nārāyaṇāya*

Meaning: "I bow to Narayana — the one who is the refuge of all beings." This is the eight-syllable *ashtakshara* mantra of Vishnu, considered among the most sacred mantras in the Vaishnava tradition.
