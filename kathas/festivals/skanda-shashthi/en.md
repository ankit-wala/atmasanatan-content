## Katha

**The Terror of Surapadman:**

Ages ago, there was a demon — Surapadman.

He was of immense power. He performed fierce austerities before Brahma and asked for a boon that would make him practically immortal — "Let no god, demon, or human be able to kill me. Only the son of Shiva shall be able to defeat me."

Brahma granted the boon.

Surapadman thought — this was armour of the surest kind. Shiva is the great yogi, the renunciant — how could he ever have a son?

---

Together with his brothers — Tarakaksha, Vidyunmali, and Kamalaksha — he marched upon all three worlds.

He defeated the gods. Indra was taken captive and thrown into a dungeon. Surya, Chandra, Vayu — all the gods who carried out their appointed duties — were stripped of those duties. Darkness fell over creation. Yajnas ceased. Dharma began to crumble.

The gods wandered in terror — no refuge, no protector.

---

**The Birth of Skanda:**

The gods went to Brahma. Brahma said — "There is only one way. Wait for the son of Shiva and Parvati."

The gods offered their praises to Shiva and Parvati.

The divine radiance of Shiva and Parvati blazed forth. That radiance was so fierce that even Agni, the fire-god, could not fully contain it. It fell into a lake — and from that sacred water, six infants were born within six reeds.

The Krittika goddesses lifted those infants into their arms and began to nurture them — and so this child came to be known as "Kartikeya."

Mother Parvati came running. In her love she drew all six infants at once to her breast — and the six became one.

That child was **Shadanana** — the six-faced one. He was **Skanda**.

---

**The Six-Day War:**

The gods appointed the child Skanda as their commander. Indra gave him his daughter Devasena in marriage. He came to be called "Devasenāpati" — lord of the divine army.

With Devasena, Skanda launched his assault upon Surapadman's forces. Fierce battle raged for six days. Surapadman's generals were defeated one by one.

---

On the sixth day — the sixth and final day of the war — Surapadman himself came onto the battlefield.

He kept changing forms — appearing in a new guise each time and fleeing. At last he took the form of a great mango tree and stood in the middle of the ocean.

Skanda aimed his **vel** — the divine spear of power that Mother Parvati had given him — at that tree.

The vel flew. The tree was split into two.

One half became a **peacock** — and became Skanda's mount.
The other half became a **rooster** (cock) — and rose upon Skanda's banner.

Surapadman was slain. His brothers Tarakaksha and Kamalaksha were also defeated.

Indra was freed. The gods returned to their appointed duties. Dharma was restored in all three worlds.

---

That day — Kartik Shukla Shashthi — has since been celebrated as **Skanda Shashthi** or **Soorasamharam**.

The tradition of the Skanda Purana holds that a devotee who observes a six-day fast on this Shashthi, worships Lord Skanda's vel, and listens to his divine deeds, will have all obstacles and fear of enemies removed from his life, and will attain courage and victory.

## Significance

**Soorasamharam — the Destruction of Evil:**
"Soorasamharam" = the slaying of Surapadman. In Tamil tradition this festival is celebrated with grand temple processions and festivities, especially at the temples of Tiruchendur and Palani. Surapadman — who symbolises ego, ignorance, and defiance of the divine order — being slain by Skanda signifies that the power of knowledge (vel) ultimately destroys all demonic tendencies.

**The Vel — the Spear of Divine Knowledge:**
Skanda's vel (spear of power) is not merely a weapon — it is the descent of Mother Parvati's divine shakti. The vel is the symbol of the power of knowledge that cuts through ignorance. The battle cry "Vel Murugan" exists for this very reason — vel = shakti, Murugan = Skanda.

**The Peacock and the Rooster — the Transformed Demon:**
The two symbols always seen with Skanda — the peacock (mount) and the rooster (on his banner) — are themselves the two halves of Surapadman's body, transformed into the Lord's service. This is the message — one who surrenders completely, even an enemy becomes a servant.

**An All-India Festival:**
In South India this is celebrated as a Shadai (six-day) fast and Soorasamharam. In North and Central India it is known as "Kartik Shashthi" or "Kartikeya Shashthi."

## Vidhi

1. **From Kartik Shukla Pratipada to Shashthi** — begin a six-day fast (if not possible, observe a single meal or fast on Shashthi itself).
2. Bathe in the morning and visit a Skanda/Kartikeya temple — if a Murugan temple is nearby, this is especially auspicious.
3. **Vel Puja:** Offer flowers, sandalwood, and naivedya to Skanda's vel. Bananas, peacock feathers, and red flowers are offered with particular devotion.
4. Recite the **Skanda Shashthi Kavacham** — this is the principal hymn of praise to Skanda (extremely widespread in Tamil tradition).
5. **Kavadi tradition:** In South India, devotees carry the kavadi — a semicircular bamboo frame — on their shoulders and undertake a foot pilgrimage to a Murugan temple.
6. In the evening, perform Skanda's aarti and vel puja, and listen to the katha.
7. **On Shashthi (Soorasamharam):** Special celebration — on this day, remembering the lila of the slaying of Surapadman, raise the victory cry of Lord Skanda.

**2026 Date:** Verify on DrikPanchang (Kartik Shukla Shashthi — ~October/November 2026).

## Observance

**Six-Day Fast:**
From Kartik Shukla Pratipada through Shashthi — six days. Each day, one meal (ekasana) or a fruit-only diet. Throughout these six days, daily recitation of the Skanda Shashthi Kavacham.

**Shashthi Vrat:**
At minimum, fast on the day of Shashthi — complete fast or fruit-only diet. Break the fast after the evening puja and aarti of Lord Skanda.

**Special Traditions:**
In South India — the kavadi pilgrimage, offering of peacock feathers, and witnessing the dramatic enactment of the Soorasamharam at the temple. In North India — worship of a six-faced (Shadanana) image or picture of Skanda and a special abhisheka of the vel.

**Dhyana Mantra:** During these six days, chant "Oṃ Śaravaṇabhava namaḥ" — 108 times each day.

## Mantras

**Skanda Mula Mantra:**

> ॐ शरवणभव नमः

Oṃ Śaravaṇabhava namaḥ

Meaning: "Salutation to Skanda, born in Sharavana (the forest of reeds)." — This is the bija mantra of Skanda. "Sharavanabhava" is one of Skanda's names — he descended into a shara-vana (forest of reeds).

---

**Vel Puja Mantra:**

> सरवणभव षण्मुख स्वामी शक्तिवेल पहिमाम् ।
> देवसेनापते देव सुब्रह्मण्य नमोस्तु ते ॥

Śaravaṇabhava ṣaṇmukha svāmī śaktivel pahimām |
devasenāpate deva subrahmanya namostu te ||

Meaning: "O you born of Sharavana, O six-faced Lord, O bearer of the vel of power — protect me. O Devasenāpati, O God, O Subrahmanya — I bow to you."
