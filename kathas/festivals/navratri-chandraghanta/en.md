## Katha

The third day of Navratri's journey — and the goddess of this day is **Maa Chandraghanta**.

This is the story of the moment when a woman had to recognise the full extent of her own power for the very first time — right after her marriage.

---

The second day had ended with the tapas of Maa Brahmacharini. Parvati had performed intense sadhana for years — rigorous fasting, a diet of leaves and water, and then only breath itself. Her tapas pleased Shiva and he accepted Parvati as his wife.

Now came the preparations for the wedding.

---

There was celebration in the palace of Himalayraja. Gods, rishis, gandharvas — all had come bearing good wishes. Maa Menaka had made grand arrangements for the welcome.

But then Shiva's wedding procession arrived at the gates.

The manner in which they came — no one had ever seen anything like it.

With Shiva came his ganas — bhoots, pretas, pisachas. Some were half-bodied, some headless. Serpents were coiled around his body. The ash of the cremation ground covered him. His matted locks were tangled, his eyes blazed.

This was no ordinary groom — this was Rudra himself, in person.

Panic broke out in the palace of Himalayraja.

Menaka — Parvati's mother — saw the sight and fell unconscious.

The maidservants screamed and fled.

The other guests too were seized by fear.

---

Parvati watched all of this.

What was in her heart — fear? Astonishment? Confusion?

No.

She loved Shiva — and she also knew that this form of Shiva was meant to startle the world, but to his devotees he is ever compassionate. Parvati recognised Shiva — and was not afraid of him.

But Parvati also understood that she had another role to play.

She was no longer merely a tapasvin. She was about to become Shiva's wife — and that meant she would have to be the bridge between Shiva's fierce world and the world of gods and men. She would have to be a mother — a wife, and a warrior too.

In that moment Parvati assumed a new form.

---

Her complexion became — **radiant gold**. Like the light of the rising sun.

**Ten arms** appeared — bearing in them weapons given by the gods: sword, trident, bow, arrow, kamandal, lotus, japa-mala.

Her mount was — **the lion**.

And on her forehead — **the crescent moon**.

But this crescent moon was no ordinary one. It was shaped like a **bell** — exactly like the bell that rings in a temple, whose sound spreads far and wide.

When Maa tilted her head — the sound of that bell-shaped crescent resounded through all three worlds.

Hearing that sound, the demons trembled.

Hearing that sound, even Shiva's fearsome wedding procession grew calm.

That sound — it strung both worlds together in one thread.

This is why she came to be named — **Chandraghanta**.

---

On the tritiya tithi of Navratri, the form of Maa Chandraghanta reminds devotees that courage is not only found on the battlefield — courage is also found in that moment when life's circumstances are terrible and you must stand in the midst of them and fulfil your dharma.

In the tradition of Devi Bhagavata Purana, it is said that the devotee who worships Maa Chandraghanta with proper rites on Tritiya — receives courage, the Manipura chakra is awakened, and negative forces are driven far from their life.

## Significance

**The bell-shaped crescent:**
The crescent moon on Maa Chandraghanta's forehead is shaped precisely like a bell — it does not represent a phase of the moon, but carries the form of a bell. A bell is rung in a temple so that negative energy is dispelled and the deity is invoked. In the same way, the bell-shaped crescent on Maa's forehead rings eternally — frightening the asuras, reassuring the devotees.

**The warrior form of the married woman:**
Shailputri was — the daughter of the Himalayas. Brahmacharini was — the ascetic sadhika. Chandraghanta is — the wife, riding a lion, bearing weapons. This form teaches that accepting the responsibilities of life does not diminish a woman's power — rather, she becomes more complete.

**Manipura Chakra:**
The sadhana of the third day of Navratri is associated with the Manipura chakra (navel region) — the centre of self-confidence, willpower, and radiance. Worship of Maa Chandraghanta is said to awaken this chakra.

**Colour — orange and grey:**
In some traditions the colour of Tritiya is **orange** — symbol of energy and enthusiasm. In some traditions **grey** is also observed — signifying the destruction of enemies and steadfastness. Both colours reflect the two facets of Maa Chandraghanta's nature.

## Vidhi

- **At dawn:** Bathe early and wear **orange** or **grey** garments — orange is the colour of Tritiya (enthusiasm and energy); in some traditions grey is also accepted.
- **Puja setup:** Establish the image or idol of Maa Chandraghanta at a clean place; light a lamp of ghee and incense.
- **Offerings:** Offer sweets made from milk (kheer or mawa sweets) as bhog; offer white and orange flowers (jasmine, marigold).
- **Bell:** Ring a bell in the temple or at home — this is the specific tradition of Tritiya, in remembrance of the bell-shaped crescent on Maa's forehead; the sound drives away negative forces.
- **Mantras:** Recite the dhyana shloka of Maa Chandraghanta and the Ya Devi stuti (see Mantras).
- **Fasting:** The Navratri vrat continues — devotees keeping the nine-day fast should continue with a fruit diet or one meal.

## Mantras

**Dhyana Shloka:**

> पिण्डज प्रवरारूढा चण्डकोपास्त्रकैर्युता ।
> प्रसादं तनुते मह्यम् चन्द्रघण्टेति विश्रुता ॥

Piṇḍaja pravarārūḍhā caṇḍakopāstrakairyutā |
prasādaṃ tanute mahyam candraghaṇṭeti viśrutā ||

Meaning: "She who rides the finest lion, who is armed with fierce wrath and weapons, and who is renowned by the name 'Chandraghanta' — may she shower her grace upon me."

---

**Ya Devi Stuti:**

> या देवी सर्वभूतेषु माँ चंद्रघंटा रूपेण संस्थिता ।
> नमस्तस्यै नमस्तस्यै नमस्तस्यै नमो नमः ॥

Yā devī sarvabhūteṣu māṃ candraghaṇṭā rūpeṇa saṃsthitā |
namastasyai namastasyai namastasyai namo namaḥ ||

Meaning: "To the goddess who abides in all beings as Maa Chandraghanta — salutations, salutations, salutations."
