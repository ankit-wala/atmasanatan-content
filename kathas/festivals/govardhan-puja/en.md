## Katha

Every year in Vrindavan, preparations would begin for a grand worship ceremony. Nanda Baba and all the cowherds would come together to worship Lord Indra. The reason was simple — Indra is the god of rain. If rain falls, the earth stays green and lush. If the earth stays green, the cows will graze. If the cows graze, there will be milk and curd. If there is milk and curd, Gokul will live.

That day, as the preparations were underway, young Krishna walked up to Nanda Baba and asked — "Baba, for whom is all of this?"

Nanda Baba explained that they worshipped Lord Indra so that he would not be displeased and would continue to send rain.

Krishna was silent for a moment. Then he said — "Baba, think about it. We are cowherds. The grass our cows eat comes from Govardhan Hill. We drink from its springs. Its plants and forests nourish our cattle. It is Govardhan who truly sustains us. We should worship Govardhan, not Indra."

Nanda Baba and the other cowherds were startled at first. To forgo the worship of Indra — this took courage. But their faith in Krishna was unshakeable. They agreed.

That year in Vrindavan, instead of Indra, Govardhan Hill was worshipped in grand celebration. At the foot of the hill, a great Annakut was arranged — a mountain of food offerings made from hundreds of dishes. The cows and calves were adorned, lamps were lit, flowers were offered. All the people of Gokul took circumambulation of Govardhan and were filled with joy.

**Indra's Wrath:**

The news reached the heavenly realm. Indra's brow darkened. A question burned in his mind — "A small cowherd's child stopped my worship? Me — the king of the three worlds?"

He was consumed with fury. He commanded the Samvartaka clouds — the clouds of annihilation — saying: "Drown Vrindavan. Destroy Gokul."

Pitch-black clouds surged forth. Lightning thundered. Such a torrent of rain fell that it showed no sign of stopping. Water everywhere. The lanes became rivers. The cows bolted in panic. Children wept. The cowherds looked to one another — "What do we do now? Where do we go?"

**Krishna Lifts Govardhan:**

Krishna watched everything.

He walked to Govardhan Hill. He did not pause for even a moment. He placed his little finger — his smallest finger — at the base of the mountain and lifted it up. That vast hill — like an enormous umbrella — rose upon his finger.

Krishna called out to everyone — "Come! Bring your cattle and your families and gather here."

All of Vrindavan — cows, calves, elders, children — came and took shelter beneath Govardhan. There, not a single drop of rain fell.

One day passed. Two days passed. Three, four, five, six days. Indra did not stop the rain. For seven days and seven nights the downpour continued without cease. Yet Krishna's finger did not tremble in the slightest. He held the mountain on that small hand — the way a child holds an umbrella.

**Indra's Remorse:**

After seven days, Indra understood. This was no ordinary child. He stopped the rain. The clouds parted.

Lord Indra himself descended from the heavens. He came before Krishna, bowed his head at his feet, and said — "I did this in arrogance. I believed myself to be the master of the three worlds. But you are the Supreme Brahman. It is you who have given me my power. Forgive me my error."

Krishna forgave him and taught him — one must not take pride in one's power. Whatever strength one is given is meant for service, not for punishment.

Krishna gently set Govardhan Hill back in its place. The sun came out. The cows went out to graze. Joy filled Gokul.

From that day forward, Govardhan Hill has been regarded as a form of Sri Krishna himself. The Bhagavata Purana says — whoever hears this lila with devotion and love, all their fears and obstacles fall away just as darkness vanishes at the rising of the sun.

## Significance

The name Govardhan means — **Go** (cow / earth) + **Vardhan** (one who nourishes). That which nourishes the earth and the cows — that is Govardhan.

This story is not merely the story of a miracle. It carries a profound message:

**Worship of local nature:** Honour the mountain from which the people of Vrindavan lived. Remain grateful to the river, the forest, and the animals that make your life possible. This message rings just as true today.

**Dissolution of ego:** The story of Indra teaches that whatever power, position, or authority one has received — it is not cause for arrogance, but a weight of responsibility. Even Indra had to learn this.

**The Annakut tradition:** On Govardhan Puja, thousands of dishes are prepared and arranged in the shape of a mountain — this is Annakut. It is the living memory of that first celebration held at the foot of Govardhan Hill. It is also an expression of gratitude — the livelihood that the Lord has given us, we offer back to him.

**Its place in the five days of Diwali:** Govardhan Puja is celebrated on the day after Diwali — on Kartik Shukla Pratipada. It is the fourth day of the five-day Diwali festival.

## Vidhi

- **Morning puja:** Govardhan Puja is performed in the morning; consult DrikPanchang for the precise auspicious hour within Kartik Shukla Pratipada.
- **Govardhan form:** In the courtyard or outside the home, shape a form of Govardhan Hill from cow dung; decorate with flowers, leaves, and rangoli. Some families also make figures of Nanda Baba, cows, and Krishna.
- **Annakut offering:** Prepare as many dishes as possible — traditionally the Chappan Bhog (fifty-six varieties) — and arrange them as an offering to the Lord.
- **Materials:** Keep roli, akshat, flowers, a lamp, incense, water, and panchamrit ready; invoke Govardhan as a form of Krishna and worship with Panchopchar.
- **Honouring the cow:** Bathe the cow, apply a tilak, garland her, and touch her feet — cow worship on this day is considered especially meritorious.
- **Circumambulation:** Take parikrama of the Govardhan form seven times (or at least three times) remembering Govardhan Maharaj in your heart. Those near Mathura-Vrindavan undertake the full ~21 km circumambulation of Govardhan Hill — barefoot, singing bhajans — which is considered greatly meritorious.
- **Annakut prasad:** After puja, distribute all dishes as prasad among family and neighbours. Temples — particularly at Vrindavan and Nathdwara (Rajasthan) — arrange large community Annakut displays.
- **Vrat (optional):** Some devotees eat only once (ekahaar) or only fruit (falahaar) on this day; this is not obligatory — worship and devotion are what matter most.

## Mantras

**Salutation mantra for Govardhan Puja:**

> गोवर्धनधराय नमः गोकुलत्राणहेतवे ।
> विष्णवे प्रणतक्लेशनाशनाय नमो नमः ॥

Govardhanadharāya namaḥ gokultrāṇahetave |
viṣṇave praṇatakleśanāśanāya namo namaḥ ||

Meaning: "Salutations to the one who lifted Govardhan, salutations to the protector of Gokul — again and again I bow to Lord Vishnu, who destroys all the afflictions of those who take refuge at his feet."

---

**Mantra for Govardhan circumambulation:**

> गोवर्धन गिरिराज प्रभो नंदनंदनप्रिय ।
> प्रसीद सर्वदा देव भक्तानुग्रहकारक ॥

Govardhan girirāja prabho nandanandanapriya |
prasīda sarvadā deva bhaktānugrahakāraka ||

Meaning: "O Govardhan Giriraj, O one most beloved of Nanda's son — O Lord, remain ever gracious and shower your blessings upon your devotees."
