## Katha

Four months had passed.

Ever since Lord Vishnu had gone to rest in Yoga-nidra on the bed of Shesha-naga in the Kshirsagar on Ashadha Shukla Ekadashi — after slaying the conch-demon Shankhasura — the four months of Shravan, Bhadrapada, Ashwin, and Kartik had gone by. The world continued in its ordinary rhythm, yet a deep waiting remained — in the hearts of devotees, in the temples, in the leaves of the Tulsi plant.

That waiting ended on the morning of Kartik Shukla Ekadashi.

The katha of the Brahma Vaivarta Purana tells us — on this day, the first right to awaken the Lord belongs to Tulsi.

Tulsi's bond with Vishnu is singular. She is the rebirth of Vrinda, Vishnu's beloved — a journey from Vrindavan to Vrinda, and from Vrinda to Tulsi. The plant we keep in our courtyards is no ordinary plant — it is the living form of a love and devotion that has bowed at Vishnu's feet across countless lifetimes.

On this day, even before sunrise, devotees gather beside the Tulsi plant. Lamps are lit. Conches and bells are sounded. And a sweet awakening-song rises — "Arise, O Lord, morning has come. The night was long, but now the dawn breaks. Your devotees stand at Your door."

That call, rising from beside the Tulsi, reaches straight to the Kshirsagar.

Lord Vishnu's Yoga-nidra breaks.

He slowly opens his eyes. Shesha-naga too awakens. The gods, who had been waiting for four months, cry out in unison with joyful proclamation. Celebration fills the heavens.

And in that very moment — on earth too, the doors are thrown open.

The auspicious ceremonies that had been paused during Chaturmas — marriages, mundan, griha-pravesh, yajnopavita — may all resume. This is why so many shehnais are heard in the month of Kartik — this month is filled with auspicious moments for weddings.

After the Lord's awakening, the first sacred rite is his marriage to Tulsi. Beginning from this very Ekadashi, or on the Dwadashi that follows, the Tulsi Vivah ceremony takes place. This is no symbolic occasion — it is the repayment of the debt that Vishnu feels toward the love of Tulsi.

It is said in the Brahma Vaivarta Purana — whoever observes the fast on this Ekadashi, participates in the Vishnu-jagaran, and worships Tulsi — receives the accumulated merit of all the fasts of Chaturmas. This one Ekadashi equals four months of spiritual practice.

## Significance

Devutthana Ekadashi is not merely a date — it is the full stop at the end of a great cycle.

When the Lord had gone to sleep on Devshayani Ekadashi, creation moved into a different rhythm. Saints and monks stayed put in one place. Householders practiced restraint. Auspicious ceremonies paused. This pause was not idleness — it was an inward journey. Chaturmas repeats this teaching every year: stop outwardly, travel inwardly.

And Devutthana Ekadashi is the celebration of that inward journey's completion — the Lord awakened, the journey fulfilled.

**The end of Chaturmas:** The four-month period of brahmacharya, simplicity, and self-study formally concludes on this day. Wandering sadhus may once again set out on their travels. Householders may celebrate weddings and festivals.

**The beginning of Tulsi Vivah:** The very first rite of the wedding season takes place on this day — the marriage of Tulsi to Vishnu. A family that plans to conduct their child's wedding that year begins counting auspicious moments from here.

**Dev-Prabodhini:** This name reveals that this is not only about Vishnu's awakening — it is the celebration of the entire divine principle of creation becoming awakened.

**The fruit for the devoted seeker:** The fast of this Ekadashi gives the combined merit of all the fasts of Chaturmas. For one who could not observe the regular fasts during Chaturmas for some reason, this is the final opportunity.

## Vidhi

- **Day before (Dashami):** In the evening, take a light sattvic meal with grains and lentils in moderate quantity; sleep early.
- **At dawn (Ekadashi):** Wake before sunrise; bathe and wear clean clothes.
- **Tulsi jagaran:** Clean the Tulsi plant; light oil or ghee diyas around it; keep a conch and bell nearby; adorn Tulsi with a garland, drape a red or yellow cloth, and decorate with sindoor and kumkum.
- **Awakening the Lord:** Sound the conch and recite the Vishnu awakening mantra "Uttiṣṭha Govinda…" (given below); perform the jagaran-song accompanied by bells and conch.
- **Vishnu puja:** Offer Tulsi leaves, yellow flowers, and panchamrit to the image or idol of Vishnu; perform Vishnu Sahasranama or Vishnu bhajans in the evening; if possible, stay awake through the night in kirtan — this jagaran is the special essence of this Ekadashi.
- **Fasting:** Observe a full fast throughout the day; grains, rice, wheat, lentils, and legumes are forbidden; onion, garlic, and leafy vegetables are also avoided in many traditions; fruits, milk, sabudana, water chestnut flour, and rock salt may be consumed.
- **Breaking the fast (Dwadashi parana):** Break the fast after sunrise and within the Dwadashi tithi; check the precise parana time on DrikPanchang; feed a brahmin or someone in need before breaking the fast yourself.
- **Tulsi Vivah:** On this day or on Dwadashi, arrange the Tulsi-Shaligram wedding at home, or participate in a Tulsi Vivah at a temple.
- **Completion of Chaturmas sankalpa:** Devotees who took a Chaturmas sankalpa on Devshayani — a vow to give up something, daily recitation, simple eating — should consider that sankalpa fulfilled today, release it formally, and offer thanks to the Lord.

## Mantras

**Vishnu awakening mantra — recite before dawn beside the Tulsi plant:**

> उत्तिष्ठ गोविंद त्यज निद्रां जगत्पते ।
> त्वयि सुप्ते जगत् सुप्तं उत्थिते चोत्थितं भवेत् ॥

Uttiṣṭha Govinda tyaja nidrāṃ jagatpate |
tvayi supte jagat suptaṃ utthite cottithaṃ bhavet ||

Meaning: "Arise, O Govinda, abandon sleep, O Lord of the universe — when You slept, the world slept; when You arise, the world shall arise."

---

**Tulsi puja mantra — recite while garlanding Tulsi and lighting the lamp:**

> तुलस्यमृतजन्मासि सदा त्वं विष्णुवल्लभा ।
> नमस्तुभ्यं देवदेवि पापं हर हरिप्रिये ॥

Tulasyamṛtajanmāsi sadā tvaṃ viṣṇuvallabhā |
namastubhyaṃ devadevi pāpaṃ hara haripriye ||

Meaning: "O Tulsi, you are born of nectar, ever the beloved of Vishnu — O Goddess of goddesses, O Haripriye, take away our sins."
