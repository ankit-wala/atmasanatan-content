## Katha

King Indradyumna ruled the city of Avanti and was a devoted worshipper of Vishnu. His one great longing was to behold the divine form of the Lord that he had heard of since childhood. Word came to him that at Puri — the sacred land called Purushottama Kshetra, where sea and sky meet on India's eastern coast — there existed a hidden image of Vishnu, kept in secret by a local tribal king.

Indradyumna sent his trusted priest Vidyapati to find it. After great effort, Vidyapati located the divine Neela Madhava — the blue form of Vishnu. But by the time Indradyumna himself arrived at Puri, the image had vanished, buried by the sands and the tide. The Lord had gone unseen.

Indradyumna was broken. He sat on that very ground and began to fast and pray.

Then came the dream. The Lord spoke to him: *"Do not despair. Tomorrow the waves will carry a great piece of sacred wood to shore — a divine log bearing the marks of Panchajanya (the conch), the Sudarshan Chakra (the spinning disc of light), Kaumodaki (the mace), and the lotus. Have my image, Balabhadra's image, and Subhadra's image carved from that wood."*

And the Lord added: *"A divine craftsman will come to do this work. He has one condition — keep the door closed. Do not watch, do not disturb him."*

The craftsman arrived — ancient in bearing, ordinary in appearance, but the tools he carried were not of this world. The door was sealed. The sounds of a chisel carried through the walls for twenty-one days. Then, one day, silence.

Both the king and queen grew anxious — perhaps the craftsman had fallen ill. Their patience gave way. The door was opened.

Inside were three forms. But they were unfinished — Jagannath's arms and feet were not yet complete. And the craftsman had vanished without trace.

Indradyumna, grief-stricken, performed penance and prayed to Brahma. Brahma himself appeared and spoke gently: *"This is not a mistake. This is the Lord's own will. In this form he is Jagannath — the Master of the Universe, who holds all of creation in himself, who needs no limbs to extend his grace. These forms are complete."*

Brahma performed the prana-pratishtha and opened the eyes of all three idols. A great temple was built. A magnificent ashvamedha yagna was performed.

And then the Lord gave Indradyumna one final gift — a promise: *"On the second day of the bright half of Ashadha, every year, I will ride in procession through the city with Balabhadra and Subhadra. He who cannot come to my temple — the poor, the sick, the forgotten — let him stand on the street. The rope of my chariot is open to all hands. Whoever sees me for these seven days will be freed from the bondage of birth and death."*

The Skanda Purana records the Lord's own words: whoever sees me together with Balabhadra and Subhadra for seven days attains union with me. The merit of darshan at night is ten times that of the day.

## Significance

Rath Yatra is one of the most extraordinary festivals in the world. In most temples, the sanctum sanctorum is out of reach for many devotees — by distance, by circumstance, or by the gates that separate. But on Rath Yatra, the Lord comes out.

The procession is also called Gundicha Yatra — the Lord is going to his aunt's home, to the garden temple at the far end of the road. It is a family outing. And the chariot rope is not reserved for the high-born or the learned — anyone can touch it, anyone can pull.

The message is stark and beautiful: the Lord who lives behind temple walls goes to meet those who have no access to those walls.

## Vidhi

The primary observances of Rath Yatra are darshan, pulling the chariot rope, and parikrama.

**Darshan:** On Ashadha Shukla Dwitiya, seek darshan of Lord Jagannath, Balabhadra, and Subhadra on their chariots. If you cannot travel to Puri, visit the nearest Jagannath temple or attend a local Rath Yatra procession.

**At home:**
1. Bathe in the morning. Set up images of Jagannath, Balabhadra, and Subhadra.
2. Offer yellow flowers, tulsi, and a ghee diya.
3. Lord Jagannath's beloved offering is mahaprasad — especially rice and khichdi.
4. Chant "Jai Jagannath" through the day.

**Fasting:** Many devotees observe a fast on this day and take prasad in the evening.

## Observance

**Mahaprasad:** The prasad cooked at the Jagannath temple in Puri is considered supremely sacred. It is called Mahaprasad, and by tradition it may be received by anyone, regardless of caste — a living demonstration that before the Lord, all distinctions fall away.

**Bahuda Yatra:** Nine days after Rath Yatra (Ashadha Shukla Dashami), the three deities return to the main temple. This return journey is called Bahuda Yatra and is celebrated with equal devotion.

**Seven days:** According to the Skanda Purana, the full seven days of the festival carry special merit — the Lord himself said the darshan of each day accumulates differently.

> कदाचित् कालिन्दीतटविपिनसङ्गीतकरवो
> मुदाभीरीनारीवदनकमलास्वादमधुपः ।
> रमाशम्भुब्रह्मामरपतिगणेशार्चितपदो
> जगन्नाथः स्वामी नयनपथगामी भवतु मे ॥

*Kadācit Kālindī-taṭa-vipina-saṅgīta-karavo
Mudā-bhīrī-nārī-vadana-kamala-āsvāda-madhupaḥ |
Ramā-Śambhu-Brahmā-mara-pati-Gaṇeśa-arcita-pado
Jagannāthaḥ svāmī nayana-patha-gāmī bhavatū me ||*

Meaning: "He who once made music in the forests along the Yamuna, the bee who savours the lotus-faces of the joyful gopi women, he at whose feet Lakshmi, Shiva, Brahma, Indra, and Ganesha all bow — may that Lord Jagannath come within the path of my eyes."
