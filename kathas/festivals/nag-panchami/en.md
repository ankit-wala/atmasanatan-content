## Katha

This is a story from the age after the Pandavas, when King Parikshit of their lineage ruled Hastinapura.

Parikshit — grandson of Arjuna, king of the Kuru dynasty. Once, while hunting in the forest, he reached the hermitage of Rishi Shamika. The rishi was deep in meditation — he did not recognize the king, gave no reply.

Parikshit's anger flared. He picked up a dead serpent lying nearby and draped it around the rishi's neck.

When Shamika's son Shringi learned of this — he cursed in fury: *"The one who insulted my father will die by the bite of the serpent Takshaka within seven days."*

Parikshit heard. He had a palace built atop a tall pillar — no serpent could climb it.

But Takshaka came. Disguised as a brahmin, hidden inside a fruit. On the seventh day, before Parikshit, the fruit opened — and Takshaka struck.

Parikshit was gone.

His son Janmejaya's heart blazed with grief and fury. He vowed: *"Every serpent in all creation shall be burned to ash."*

The sarpa satra — the serpent sacrifice — began. A terrible fire, ghee, and mantras. The power of the yagna was such that from every corner of the earth, serpents rose into the sky and were pulled helplessly toward the flames — falling in, one by one.

There was a wailing in the heavens. There was chaos in the realm of the nagas.

Takshaka, terrified, fled to Devraj Indra. Indra hid him beneath his own throne.

The mantras of the yagna intensified — Takshaka and Indra both began to be pulled toward the fire. Indra's throne itself shook in the sky.

At that very moment — a young brahmin entered the yagnashala.

This was Astika. Son of Rishi Jaratkaru and Naga-mata Manasa Devi. His mother had sent him: *"Son, go. Save your nagas."*

Astika entered and praised the yagna — the quality of the rituals, the proper observance of the Vedic rites, the king's excellence. He praised it so sincerely and well that King Janmejaya was genuinely pleased.

Janmejaya said: *"Young brahmin, you have delighted me. Ask — what do you want?"*

Astika said: *"Rajan, stop the yagna."*

Janmejaya paused. He looked. Then he bowed his head.

The yagna stopped. Takshaka was saved. The serpents were protected.

In the tradition of the Mahabharata, Adi Parva, Astika Parva it is said — one who worships the nagas on this day, places food and water for them, and prays for freedom from serpent-fear — will not face the nagas' wrath.

## Significance

Nag Panchami is not merely the worship of snakes — it is the remembrance of an ancient debt.

Serpents are the guardians of our earth. In the fields, on the riverbanks, beneath the soil — these creatures hold the balance of the earth in place.

In the Indian tradition, the naga is divine — Vishnu rests on Shesha, Shiva wears the naga as an ornament, Krishna subdued Kaliya — all these stories say the same thing: to honor the serpent is to honor creation.

Astika's katha teaches something more — compassion and forgiveness prevail. What Janmejaya's fury could not accomplish, was accomplished by a child's wisdom and a mother's prayer.

## Vidhi

- **At dawn:** Take an early bath; install an image or clay figure of a serpent for worship.
- **Offerings:** Worship with milk, durva grass, flowers, and sandalwood.
- **Katha:** Listen to or read the Nag Panchami katha.
- **Temple/burrow:** Offer milk at a serpent's burrow or at a naga temple; chant the naga mantra.
- **Fasting:** Not obligatory — but some devotees observe a one-meal sattvic fast.
- **Restrictions:** Do not harm serpents in any form; do not dig the earth (mark of respect for the naga's dwelling); in some regional traditions, avoid working with needles and thread.

## Mantras

**Ashtanaga Mantra — the traditional mantra of Nag Panchami:**

> वासुकिस्तक्षकश्चैव कालियो मणिभद्रकः ।
> ऐरावतो धृतराष्ट्रः कार्कोटकधनञ्जयौ ॥
> एतेऽभयं प्रयच्छन्ति प्राणिनां प्राणजीविनाम् ।

*Vāsukis takṣakaś caiva kāliyo maṇibhadrakaḥ |
Airāvato dhṛtarāṣṭraḥ kārkoṭaka-dhanañjayau ||
Ete'bhayaṃ prayacchanti prāṇināṃ prāṇajīvinām |*

Meaning: "Vasuki, Takshaka, Kaliya, Manibhadrak, Airavata, Dhritarashtra, Karkotaka, and Dhananjaya — these eight nagas grant fearlessness to all living beings."
