## Katha

This is the story of the very beginning of creation.

Brahma ji brought the universe into being through the power of his divine will. The earth took form, the sky spread wide, the five elements — water, fire, air, earth, and space — each settled into their place. Forests, mountains, rivers, living beings — everything had taken shape. And yet, for all this abundance, there was a strange emptiness. There was no sound, no voice. Birds existed, but no song rose from their throats. Rivers flowed, but the murmur of their waters was silent. The wind moved, but carried no music. Human beings walked the earth, but their tongues held no words.

Brahma ji looked upon this voiceless creation and his heart grew troubled. Everything was present, yet everything was incomplete — like a lamp that holds no flame. He sought the refuge of Lord Vishnu and prayed, "Lord, this creation has life, but it has no living. It has no speech, no awareness, no beauty."

Lord Vishnu understood Brahma ji's anguish. He said, "Invoke Adi Shakti, the Primordial Power. She alone can make this creation whole."

With a concentrated and steady mind, Brahma ji meditated upon Adi Shakti. According to the tradition of the Devi Bhagavata Purana, from Lord Vishnu's divine speech — from the Shabda-Brahman, the word-essence that arose from his presence — a luminous light manifested. (In some traditions, this light is said to arise from Brahma ji's own resolve and contemplation.) And from the centre of that radiance, she appeared — Ma Saraswati.

She was seated upon a white lotus. Her garments were pure and spotless — her form held the lustre of kunda blossoms and the cool glow of the moon. In one hand she held the veena, in another a book. In her third hand was the akshamala, the rosary of beads, and in her fourth the kamandalu, the sacred water pot. Upon her face rested such serenity that it seemed all knowledge had stilled itself and taken its seat there.

Ma Saraswati drew her fingers across the strings of the veena.

And in that very instant — sound awakened within creation.

Songs poured from the throats of birds. The rivers rang with their gentle murmur. The wind took on a sweet and rustling breath. Words rose upon the lips of human beings. The world that had until then been mute began to speak, to sing, to learn. Creation had found its voice.

Brahma ji bowed at Ma's feet and said, "Ma, you have given this creation its voice. You are Vagdevi — the sovereign of speech."

This event took place on the Shukla Panchami of the month of Magha — the very day that heralds the arrival of the spring season. From that day forward, yellow mustard flowers bloom across the fields, as though the earth herself dons basanti yellow to welcome Ma Saraswati.

The tradition of the Devi Bhagavata Purana holds that Ma Saraswati's manifestation took place on this very day — and it is for this reason that Vasant Panchami became the great festival of devotion to learning, speech, art, and music.

## Significance

Ma Saraswati's very name carries the essence of her form — **Sara + Swa** meaning "she who causes the essence (the core of all things) to flow." She is the presiding deity of vidya (knowledge), vak (speech), music (melody), and art (creation).

On Vasant Panchami, three streams converge as one:

**The Day of Manifestation** — This is the day of Ma Saraswati's appearance in the world. Just as a birthday calls for celebration, her birth-tithi calls for her special worship.

**The Arrival of Spring** — Magha Shukla Panchami is, in the Hindu panchang, the first day of the spring season. The stillness of winter withdraws, mustard flowers open in yellow abundance, mango blossoms appear. Nature itself begins to learn, to take on new colours — and this feeling merges perfectly with Ma Saraswati's spirit of knowledge.

**Vidyarambham** — This is considered the most auspicious moment to begin new learning. Children are guided to write their first letter on this day by a teacher or elder — this rite is called the **Vidyarambham** ceremony.

**Yellow** is the colour of this festival — basanti yellow, the yellow of mustard, the gold of saffron. This colour is the symbol of the brilliance of knowledge, of energy, and of all that is auspicious.

## Vidhi

- **At dawn:** Bathe and wear yellow or white garments.
- **Offerings:** Yellow flowers (marigold, mustard), white cloth, books, musical instruments, akshamala; offer saffron-tinged yellow rice, mishri, and curd as bhog; light a lamp, incense, and agarbatti.
- **Puja:** Install an image or portrait of Ma Saraswati; place books, pens, musical instruments, and the tools of your craft at her feet; recite the Saraswati Vandana ("Ya Kundendu Tushara Hara Dhavala...").
- **Vidyarambham:** Have children write their first letter under the guidance of a teacher or elder — this is the auspicious rite of beginning new learning.
- **Observance:** On this day books, musical instruments, and implements of one's art are not placed on the floor — they rest at Ma's feet; wearing yellow garments is the tradition of this festival.
- **Special:** In some families this is also Ayudha Puja — artists worship the tools of their calling; after puja, sing the songs of spring; distribute yellow prasad — saffron rice, besan laddus, or sweet yellow rice; books are taken up again the following day.
