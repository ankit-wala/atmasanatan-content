## Katha

Nine days of worship. The tenth day: victory.

From Ashwin Shukla Pratipada, Lord Rama spent the nine days of Navaratri in the worship of Maa Aparajita — Jagadamba Durga. He knew that the battle ahead was not merely a contest between two kings; it was a great war between dharma and adharma. Without the grace of the goddess who presides over all shakti, that battle would remain incomplete.

On the tenth day — when both armies stood facing each other on the battlefield — the great sage Agastya came to Rama. He had descended to the field of battle for one purpose alone: to deliver a message. He said, "Raghava, now I will recite for you the hymn that is eternal, the one that grants victory over all enemies — the Aditya Hridayam." Rama chanted that sacred hymn to the Sun three times. The radiance of Surya entered every pore of his being; new strength surged through his body, and an unshakeable resolve arose within his mind.

Now Rama and Ravana stood face to face.

Ravana had been granted a boon through years of fierce penance — whenever his heads were cut off, they would grow back. The battle stretched from hours into days. Each time Rama's arrow severed one of Ravana's heads, that head returned, renewed. The gods watched from above; even their hearts were gripped with anguish.

Then Brahma, from his divine knowledge, revealed to Rama the secret that was the door to Ravana's death: within Ravana's navel lay the source of amrita — the nectar of immortality. As long as that nectar remained, Ravana could not die.

Rama lifted his bow. He drew the string taut. He placed upon it the divine Brahmastra given by the sage Agastya — an arrow that held within it the combined power of wind, fire, the sun, and Brahma himself. All of creation seemed to pause in that single moment. Rama's arrow found Ravana's navel. The amrita dried up. Ravana's great body fell to the earth.

Flowers rained down from the heavens. The gods broke into cries of "Jai! Jai!" Gandharvas and apsaras danced in joy. Lord Indra and all the devas sang Rama's praise. Lanka was freed; the path to Mother Sita's liberation was opened.

The tradition of the Valmiki Ramayana teaches that whoever hears this katha of victory is freed from all sins, wins over all enemies, and attains long life and glory. Ashwin Shukla Dashami — that day, that very moment — came to be called "Vijayadashami." May the remembrance of dharma's triumph endure for as long as creation lasts.

## Significance

Vijayadashami is not merely the memory of a war — it is the celebration of an eternal truth: no matter how powerful adharma may grow, dharma will ultimately prevail. The story of Rama and Ravana reminds every generation that the end of injustice, arrogance, and greed is certain — if one walks the path of dharma and takes refuge in the Lord.

Ravana's ten heads are regarded in Hindu tradition as symbols of ten inner vices: lust, anger, greed, attachment, pride, envy, selfishness, injustice, violence, and ego. Each year at Ravan Dahan, these ten heads are reduced to ash — and with them comes the resolve to burn the same ten enemies within ourselves.

On this day, the worship of Devi Aparajita is especially important. "Aparajita" means the one who has never been defeated — that very form of Maa Durga whom Rama worshipped throughout Navaratri. The Shami tree is also worshipped on this day; it is the tree beneath whose branches the Pandavas concealed their weapons during their year of incognito exile — and so the tree became a symbol of power and victory.

The celebration of Vijayadashami takes a different form in every corner of India. In northern India, enormous effigies of Ravana, Kumbhakarna, and Meghnada are set ablaze. In Bengal, this same day marks the immersion of Durga — after ten days of the Mother's presence, She departs, and the air rings with the cry "Ashe bachhar abar hobe" (may it happen again next year). In Mysore, the Dussehra festival is renowned across the country and beyond for the grand royal procession of the Maharaja. In southern India, it is celebrated as "Ayudha Puja" or "Shastra Puja" — on this day, one's tools, vehicles, and craft are all worshipped.

## Vidhi

The traditional observance of Vijayadashami includes Aparajita Pujan, Shami Pujan, and Ravan Dahan:

1. **Morning bath and sankalpa.** Bathe before sunrise or during the Brahma Muhurta and put on clean clothes. Hold this resolve in mind: "Today, on Ashwin Shukla Dashami, I perform the worship of Devi Aparajita."
2. **Aparajita Pujan.** In a clean spot facing east or the north-east (Ishan) corner, invoke Devi Aparajita. Place Shami leaves and an image or an evocation of the Nilakantha bird (the blue-throated roller, also called Nil-Jayant) before you. Offer roli, akshat, flowers, and incense with a lit lamp. On this day, seeing the Nilakantha bird is considered especially auspicious.
3. **Shami Pujan.** Go to a Shami tree, or place a Shami branch in your home. Worship it with water, akshat, flowers, and roli. Recite the Shami mantra given below. Distribute Shami leaves among family members as "gold" — this is the ancient tradition of "looting gold."
4. **Seemollanghan (crossing the boundary).** In the old custom, kings would cross the boundary of their kingdom on this day — a symbolic journey of victory. In today's world, this takes the form of a resolve: beginning a new endeavour, a new journey, or a new enterprise.
5. **Ayudha-Shastra Pujan.** In the tradition of southern India, vehicles, tools, books, and craft-works are worshipped on this day. Householders too may worship the instruments of their work and livelihood.
6. **Ravan Dahan.** In the evening, take part in the local burning of the effigies of Ravana, Kumbhakarna, and Meghnada. This burning is a resolve to destroy the ten inner vices — not merely a theatrical spectacle.
7. **Exchange of gold and sweets.** Send Shami leaves or sweets to family and friends. This is a symbol of victory and abundance.
8. **Evening aarti and prasad.** Perform the aarti of Rama, Durga, or your family deity and receive prasad.

## Observance

Vijayadashami is not a day of fasting — there is no mandatory upavasa on this festival. It is a day of celebration and victory. Those who have observed the nine-day Navaratri fast conclude their fast on Dashami.

This day is considered extremely auspicious for beginning new endeavours — starting one's education, a new business, a new home, setting the date of a marriage, or the start of a journey. The belief is that any work begun on this day will certainly meet with success.

The exchange of Shami leaves is a special rite of this day — family members and friends give each other Shami leaves as though offering "gold"; it is a symbol of love, abundance, and goodwill.

In southern India, "Ayudha Puja" is the distinctive tradition of this day. Farmers, craftsmen, drivers, and artists — all worship their respective tools and instruments. The following day, as Vijayadashami proper, they return to their work renewed.

## Mantras

**Aparajita Stuti — remembrance of the Devi's power**

> या देवी सर्वभूतेषु शक्तिरूपेण संस्थिता ।
> नमस्तस्यै नमस्तस्यै नमस्तस्यै नमो नमः ॥

Yā devī sarvabhūteṣu śaktirūpeṇa saṃsthitā | namastasyai namastasyai namastasyai namo namaḥ ||

Meaning: To the Goddess who abides in all living beings in the form of Shakti — salutation to Her, salutation to Her, salutation to Her, again and again.

---

**Shami Pujan Mantra**

> शमी शमयते पापं शमी शत्रुविनाशिनी ।
> अर्जुनस्य धनुर्धारी रामस्य प्रियदर्शिनी ॥

Śamī śamayate pāpaṃ śamī śatruvinā́śinī | arjunasya dhanurdhārī rāmasya priyadarśinī ||

Meaning: Shami destroys sin; Shami destroys enemies. She who bore Arjuna's bow and is dear to Rama — that is the Shami tree.
