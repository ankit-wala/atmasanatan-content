## Katha

One day the king of the gods, Indra, was forced to abandon his throne. Mani and Malla — two ferocious asuras — had performed fierce austerities and wrested an invincible boon from Brahma. Terror of them made all three worlds tremble. They launched an assault on the celestial realm and routed Indra along with every other god. Helpless, the gods sought refuge with Lord Shiva and pleaded for his protection.

Hearing the gods' cry, Shiva's heart filled with both compassion and wrath. Witnessing the advance of adharma upon the earth, he himself appeared as a supreme warrior — **Martanda Bhairava**, whom Maharashtra's tradition of devotion calls **Khandoba**. "Khand" means sword and "ba" means father — he is the sword-bearing father who stands as protector. With him was his Shakti, **Mhalsa** — the very form of Parvati herself. Khandoba mounted his beloved horse **Aindra**, raised his gleaming sword, took his faithful dog alongside him, and set forth for battle.

The war-conch sounded on Shukla Pratipada of the month of Margashirsha. Mani and Malla hurled all their sorcery and power against him, yet they could not withstand Khandoba. Day and night, one after another — the terrible war raged for six days. Mhalsa stood unshaken at his side throughout, becoming his strength in every moment.

**Shashthi tithi** — the sixth day — Khandoba's sword rose, and the end of both asuras was at hand. At that very moment, Mani and Malla dropped their arrogance. They fell at Khandoba's feet and implored: "O Lord! We have taken your shelter. Accept us as your servants — birth after birth." Khandoba's heart was generous. He stayed his sword, accepted the surrender of Mani and Malla, and made them his gatekeepers in service — and creation was freed from terror.

On the very ground where this victory was won, champa flowers bloomed that day of their own accord — as though the earth itself were greeting its protector. That is why this Shashthi came to be called **Champa Shashthi**, and the day began to be observed as Khandoba Jayanti.

Ever since, every year in Margashirsha, this six-day festival is celebrated in memory of those same six days of battle. The Khandoba temple at Jejuri, near Pune, is his principal abode, where on the day of Shashthi devotees release streams of turmeric powder — bhandardara — into the air. That golden turmeric is the symbol of Khandoba's victorious radiance.

The Maharashtra tradition within the Skanda Purana holds that devotees who worship Khandoba with sincere faith across these six days receive his blessing of protection from enemies, freedom from illness, and victory in life.

## Significance

Champa Shashthi is not merely a festival — it is the celebration of a faith that lives in Maharashtra's soul. Khandoba is counted among Maharashtra's five principal kula-devatas. The Dhangar (shepherd) community, Maratha warriors, and farming communities all revere him as their kula-dev with special devotion.

Spiritually, Khandoba embodies Shiva's warrior aspect that descends to the earth to destroy adharma. His katha carries the message that however powerful the asura-forces may be, the power of the Divine is always active in the protection of dharma.

The final surrender of Mani and Malla is the deepest spiritual lesson of this katha — only after the abandonment of ego does the grace of God become available. Even enemies, when they bow at the feet, are made servants by the Lord — this is the proof of Khandoba's generosity and of Shiva's compassion.

## Vidhi

- **Duration:** Six-day vrat observed from Margashirsha Shukla Pratipada through Shashthi; devotees unable to keep all six days should at minimum fast and perform the special puja on Shashthi.
- **Each morning (days 1–6):** Bathe; meditate on Khandoba; offer turmeric (bhandardara) on his image; offer coconut, flowers, and incense; light a lamp; sing or listen to ovi or gondhal.
- **Each evening (days 1–6):** Light a lamp at home and take Khandoba's name; organise a gondhal — traditional bhajan-kirtan — which is the special mode of his worship.
- **Diet:** For all six days take only sattvic food; abstain from non-vegetarian food, alcohol, and tamasic substances.
- **On Shashthi — main puja:** Perform abhisheka with water and turmeric; offer a stream of turmeric powder (bhandardara — the central element of this puja); offer coconut and champa flowers; pray for the protection and victory of the family; raise the victory cry "Yeḷkoṭ yeḷkoṭ jaya malhār."
- **Breaking the fast:** Devotees observing the vrat may break their fast after accepting prasad following the Shashthi puja.
- **At Jejuri:** Carry packets of turmeric to the Khandoba temple — releasing bhandardara at the temple is the hallmark of this festival; Jejuri (near Pune) is his principal abode.
- **Note:** This is primarily a regional festival of Maharashtra, celebrated with particular fervour in Pune, Solapur, Nashik, and Kolhapur districts.

## Mantras

> ॐ खंडोबाय नमः

oṃ khaṇḍobāya namaḥ

Meaning: O Khandoba, I bow to you.

---

> येळकोट येळकोट जय मल्हार।

yeḷkoṭ yeḷkoṭ jaya malhār

Meaning: Victory to Khandoba (Malhār) — this is Maharashtra's traditional victory cry that devotees chant at every puja.
