## Katha

Lord Krishna once told Dharmaraja Yudhishthira an ancient tale.

"Dharmaraja, long ago a terrible war broke out between the gods and the asuras. Indra's army was being pushed back on every front. Amaravati itself was in peril. Indra grew deeply troubled."

He went to Devaguru Brihaspati and asked — "Gurudeva, what is to be done?"

Brihaspati replied — "On Shravan Purnima there is a sacred rite of protection. A holy thread — consecrated with mantras. Let your Shakti, Shachi, tie it on your right wrist. This raksha-sutra will become your armour."

Shravan Purnima arrived. Goddess Shachi prepared the thread with her own hands — rice grains, mustard seeds, sandalwood, kumkuma, strengthened with mantras. And she tied it on Indra's wrist.

Indra went into battle.

That same Indra — who had been losing only the day before — was invincible. One by one the asuras were defeated. Amaravati was saved.

After the war, the asuras asked their guru Shukracharya — "Gurudeva, Indra was on the verge of defeat. What changed today?"

Shukracharya said — "Shachi tied a raksha-sutra on Indra's wrist on Shravan Purnima. By the power of that thread, he became unconquerable."

---

Lord Krishna continued:

"Yudhishthira, there is another story — of Mother Lakshmi. When Lord Vishnu became the gatekeeper of King Bali and left Vaikuntha, Mother Lakshmi was heartbroken. On Shravan Purnima, she herself went to Bali. She tied a raksha-sutra on his wrist and called him her brother. Bali asked — 'Sister, what do you wish?' Mother Lakshmi said — 'Return my husband.' Bali immediately freed Lord Vishnu."

---

In the tradition of the Bhavishya Purana, Uttara Parva, it is said — "A raksha-sutra tied on Shravan Purnima becomes a protector. The thread holds mantra-power, devotion, and love — and it is these three things together that protect."

## Significance

Raksha Bandhan is the meeting of two traditions.

One is the warrior's tradition — when a beloved one ties a protective thread on the wrist of someone setting out for battle. The thread was not a shield; it was a feeling — "We are with you."

The other is the brother-sister tradition — when Mother Lakshmi called Bali her brother. That raksha-sutra was a bond of love — "You are mine, I am yours."

The spirit of the day remains the same. A sister's thread for her brother is a blessing — "Go, grow, succeed — but come home."

## Vidhi

- **For whom:** The sister observes this rite; brothers receive the rakhi.
- **Fasting:** The sister fasts and breaks her fast only after tying the rakhi on her brother's wrist.
- **Auspicious timing:** Tie the rakhi only after Bhadra kala has passed — tying during Bhadra is inauspicious (Bhavishya Purana, Skanda Purana); check the Atma Sanatan app for the exact muhurta.
- **Preparation:** Seat the brother on an aasan (seat of honour); apply roli or kumkuma tilak on his forehead.
- **Aarti:** Perform the aarti of the brother.
- **Tying the rakhi:** Tie the rakhi on his right wrist while reciting the mantra; offer sweets.
- **Brother's vow:** The brother takes a vow to protect his sister.

## Mantras

**Recite this mantra while tying the rakhi:**

> येन बद्धो बली राजा दानवेन्द्रो महाबलः ।
> तेन त्वामभिबध्नामि रक्षे मा चल मा चल ॥

*Yena baddho Balī rājā dānavendro mahābalaḥ |
Tena tvām abhibadhnāmi rakṣe mā cala mā cala ||*

Meaning: "By the same (raksha-sutra) with which the mighty demon-king Bali was bound — I bind you with that. O protection, remain firm, remain firm." (In tradition, this Bali is the same demon-king on whose wrist Mother Lakshmi tied her raksha-sutra — as told in the Katha above.)

---

**Auspicious blessing — after the rakhi:**

> ॐ यदायुर्वर्धनं रक्षा भ्राता त्वां परिरक्षतु ।
> चिरायुर्भव सौभाग्यं पुत्रपौत्रप्रवर्धनम् ॥

*Oṃ yadāyur-vardhanam rakṣā bhrātā tvāṃ pariraksatu |
Cirāyur-bhava saubhāgyam putra-pautra-pravardhanam ||*

Meaning: "May this protection increase your lifespan; may your brother protect you. Be long-lived, may you have good fortune, may your sons and grandsons flourish."
